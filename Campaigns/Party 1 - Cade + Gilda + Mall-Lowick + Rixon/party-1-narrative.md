# Party 1: Characters and Narrative

**A Story About Four People Who Became Necessary to Each Other**

Party 1 is not a fellowship assembled by prophecy or a band of heroes answering a call. It is four people who were thrown together by proximity, professional convenience, and a cargo manifest that someone tampered with — and who discovered, over the course of twelve days on a trading ketch and three days fleeing overland, that they were better together than apart. The story that emerges from their creation documents, campaign setup, and pre-campaign vignettes is one about trust built slowly, from the ground up, between people who have every reason to be cautious about extending it.

The campaign's tone — pulpy low fantasy set inside a high-fantasy world — frames everything. The great kingdoms and dragon flights exist somewhere far away. These four live in the margins: trade roads, port towns, tavern back rooms. Their stakes are personal and immediate. Rent. Reputation. A friend who hasn't written back. The cargo that went wrong. The world is larger and stranger than the Fenway road suggests, but what matters right now is whether these four can pay their way and keep each other alive.

## The Characters

### Cade — The Gentle Storm

Cade is the party's moral anchor, and the writing knows it. An eight-foot Goliath healer and storm-priest with storm-grey skin, faded nautical tattoos, and a holy symbol shaped like a breaking wave, he is described as the blending of "a ruthless deck-enforcer and a gentle, devoted holy man." That contradiction is the heart of him: he looks like something dangerous and is, fundamentally, someone who cannot stop caring.

He grew up in Stoneveil Reach, a mixed harbour town at the base of the highland ridge where the Goliath communities keep their strongholds. His people came down from the mountains a generation or two back and became coastal folk, shaped by the sea. He never intended to be a sailor or a man of faith. He was content. But his childhood friends — Akander, Sael, Corvan — were restless, and when they signed on with a merchant fleet, Cade knew their reckless nature would get them killed. So he went with them. Not because he wanted adventure, but because the people he loved were walking into danger and someone needed to watch the door.

This is the pattern that defines Cade entirely: he does not seek out responsibility; he inherits it, because he cannot bring himself to let people he cares about face the world unprotected. He joined the ship as chaplain, medic, and unofficial bodyguard. His divine connection is not a conversion story — it is something in his Storm Giant blood that was recognised, "in the way that storms notice what they will notice." He doesn't fully understand what is listening. He knows something is. His faith is practical and undramatic, expressed through spoken prayers that knit wounds closed and hands laid gently over broken skin rather than sermons.

The vignettes capture him beautifully. In one, he stands below Mall-Lowick as she climbs the ship's rigging to take navigational readings. She doesn't need him. He knows she doesn't need him. He's there anyway. In another, he treats a cook's burn below decks without being asked, and when Gilda challenges him on why he's doing unpaid work, he answers simply: "I did it because it mattered that he suffer less. That's the whole of it." He says it without heat, as a fact he stopped defending a long time ago. This is a man who has made peace with being exactly who he is.

He reads people with an accuracy that borders on the uncomfortable. He sees through Rixon's cultivated cynicism, through Gilda's transactional armour, through Mall-Lowick's cheerful recklessness. He sees, and he does not comment, and this restraint is its own form of generosity. He gives people the space to be who they are while quietly noting where their floors are.

His unresolved threads are poignant: Sael, his navigator friend, has disappeared — a message left at a port chapel was collected but never answered. Cade checks for replies at every town with a chapel. Nothing yet. He is giving it time. He is trying not to calculate how much time is too much.

### Gilda — The Calculus of Self-Interest

Gilda is the party's sharpest mind and its moral wild card. A five-foot Dwarf merchant-scholar who practises magic with mahogany skin, dark velvet robes trimmed with gold thread, and fingers heavy with rings that are "as much emergency currency as they are decoration," she carries her quarterstaff like a sceptre. Everything about her presentation is deliberate: she is a woman who has decided what she is worth and communicates it through every material choice she makes.

She never cared for the "honour and heritage" of the deep mines. The sound of gold coins was more beautiful to her than the strike of a pickaxe. She built a career as a logistics expert, managing trade between the Dwarven mountain holds and the Halfling river communities. To Gilda, magic was never a mystical calling — it was a series of high-end tools for a high-end career. She learned to conjure a platform of force that carries cargo without porters because it was cheaper than hiring them. She learned Halfling so she'd never be cheated at the docks.

Her moral philosophy is not amorality but a specific, considered position: she views every encounter through the lens of a contract. If it doesn't pay, or if it costs too much in resources, she isn't interested. She is not cruel. She keeps her word. She does not cheat honest partners. She does not take more than her agreed share. But she will not do something for free just because it is the right thing to do, and she finds the concept genuinely baffling.

The vignette between her and Cade below decks is the most revealing scene about her in the entire corpus. She watches him treat a wounded cook's mate — for free, without being asked — and cannot let it go. "He's not your crew," she says. "You're not being paid for it." When he answers with his quiet, undeniable truth, she reads him "with the same attention she gave to manifests — reading for information, not for sentiment." After a long silence, she concedes: "I find that position genuinely difficult to locate a flaw in. That isn't the same as agreeing with it." And then: "She found this more interesting than she'd anticipated. She filed it, and did not discard it."

That last line is the key to Gilda's character arc. She is a woman who files things. She does not discard what she cannot refute. Her eventual trust in Cade comes not from being moved by his faith but from observing that he consistently honours his end of a working relationship. In her moral vocabulary, "reliable contractual partner" is the highest category she knows how to offer, and when she allocates resources to keeping someone alive, that is her equivalent of affection.

Her unresolved thread — the Thornwater consignment, where someone added undeclared cargo to her manifest and got her entire operation impounded — is deeply personal. Gilda is owed a reckoning from someone, and finding out who jeopardised her commercial reputation is her driving motivation. She is running on limited liquid funds for the first time in her career. The financial pressure is real and sharpens every decision she makes.

### Mall-Lowick — The Cartographer Who Sees the World as It Is

Mall-Lowick is two feet six inches of relentless curiosity in studded leather. A Halfling wilderness scout and professional cartographer, she is driven by a single, consuming purpose: to map the unmapped and document the strange, forgotten places of the world. Her most distinctive feature is her oversized, weather-proofed map case, slung across her back alongside a longbow almost as tall as she is, which she handles with effortless grace. Her fingers are perpetually ink-stained from her work.

Her moral compass points toward a deep, practical conviction that the truth of the land belongs to everyone. Borders, treaties, and "keep out" signs are suggestions. If ignoring them means uncovering a new geographical wonder or saving someone lost in the wilderness, she won't hesitate to trespass. She does not experience this as rule-breaking; she experiences it as correct prioritisation.

The vignettes give her the most vivid physical writing in the entire set. Up in the rigging of the Grey Cormorant, "one foot braced against a ratline, one hand gripping a stay, her other hand pressing a folded piece of oilskin paper against her knee while she worked with a stick of charcoal." On the hillside fleeing Thornwater, making reference marks "with a precision that looked mechanical from the outside," negotiating with Rixon — who wants five minutes of exposure on the ridge; she counter-offers seven; they settle on six and a half, and she tells him to eat something because she can tell from his gait that he hasn't. That last detail — diagnosing hunger from how someone walks — tells you everything about the quality of her attention.

Her relationship with the Cormorant Stack, a cliff formation she's been trying to chart for eighteen months, is a small professional detail that matters deeply to her identity. She finally got a usable bearing from the Grey Cormorant voyage. The partial notes from two years ago can now be triangulated into something she trusts. It won't come up in conversation. But it is the kind of small satisfaction that defines who she is: a person for whom getting the map right is not work but vocation.

Her prior relationship with Gilda — a cartography commission with three unresolved points of professional dispute — gives them a shared history that is both textured and affectionate. Their arguments about whether a rock formation is called "Breaker's Head" or "the Cormorant Stack," and whether a 400-yard discrepancy is magnetic variation or surveying error, are how they communicate. Both of them enjoy it.

### Rixon — The Man Behind the Mask

Rixon is the party's most layered character, and the one whose backstory holds the most unexploded ordnance. A 28-year-old human soldier-turned-mercenary, five foot nine and built "broad and sturdy rather than sleek or graceful," he moves with the heavy, deliberate tread of someone whose hands remember both the hammer and the sword. His face "naturally settles into the gruff, unbothered expression of a career tough guy — a look he gleefully cultivates."

That word *cultivates* is doing heavy lifting. The entire point of Rixon is the gap between performance and reality. He projects the image of a cynical, bruised-knuckle henchman and admits he revels in playing that part. But "behind that theatrical henchman exterior, his eyes are sharp, betraying the keen intellect and practical imagination that keep him alive in the demimonde." He is, depending on his mood, "just as capable of rakish heroics as he is of theatrical villainy."

His backstory is a series of doors that closed. Apprenticed at fifteen to Orrath Stone-Handed, a Goliath blacksmith who emigrated from Greymantle Keep to the city of Ashford. Orrath was arcane in ways that went beyond ordinary smithing — "something in the way the metal moved when he worked it." He taught Rixon the craft, the basics of arcane metalworking, and the Giant tongue — partly for practical reasons, and partly because he was privately pleased to pass something of his home to a human boy who was genuinely interested. The arrangement fell through when Rixon was twenty-one. The reasons are "his own business." What is documented: the forge was shut by the Ashford Guild on unspecified charges, the records were sealed, and Orrath disappeared.

After the forge, the city militia. After the militia, a discharge that was "technically honourable" under "sufficiently complicated" circumstances. After the discharge, whatever pays: guard contracts, escort jobs, the occasional group venture. He considers himself a denizen of the demimonde, where "morals are fickle and loyalties are worse" and "true friends are as rare and precious as diamonds."

The night-watch vignette aboard the Grey Cormorant is the emotional centrepiece of his character introduction. He's working a tangled line in the dark and swears at it — in Giant, in a Greymantle dialect construction that you don't learn from textbooks, something Orrath said in the forge when things refused to cooperate. Cade, twenty feet away, goes completely still. The recognition is not about the language itself but about the specificity of it — the phrasing Cade's own father used, the dialect of the settlement over the ridge from his home. Neither of them names what has happened. They stand the rest of the watch in silence. But "something had altered in the way they occupied the same dark and moving space... and neither of them named it, and both of them knew it was there."

This thread — the Orrath connection, the sealed forge, what was really being made in there — is the dormant engine of Rixon's character arc and of the arcane principles Orrath embedded in his muscle memory beginning to surface as genuine magical ability. The knowledge lives in his hands, in the language of the forge, and will find expression at the moment of crisis. He has pieces of understanding he does not yet know how to name.

Rixon's role in the party is pivotal. He is the swing vote — sitting between Cade's principled clarity and Gilda's transactional calculus, his decision in any given moral dilemma determines which way the group goes. He is "mildly uncomfortable with" this role and "projects no awareness of" it. He has a genuine moral floor that is lower than Gilda's but less rigid than Cade's. When forced to choose between what is right and what works, he tends to land on right — eventually, grudgingly, without making a speech about it.

## The Relationships

Their six pairwise relationships are each drawn with remarkable specificity.

**Cade and Rixon** share the deepest unspoken bond. They fight together as if designed for it, communicate in Giant when they want privacy, and have reached "an unspoken agreement" about Rixon's performed cynicism: Cade sees through it; Rixon knows Cade sees through it; neither brings it up. The Orrath/Greymantle thread gives this bond a foundation that is older than either of them — a shared cultural inheritance passed through different channels, meeting unexpectedly on a cold deck at sea.

**Cade and Gilda** represent the party's sharpest philosophical divide — sacred conviction versus calculated self-interest. Their debates, when they come, will be "genuinely difficult." And yet there is a "slow thaw" as Gilda comes to trust Cade not because she is moved by his faith but because he is, by her standards, a reliable partner. They share Dwarvish as a private language, and Rixon has learned to be "mildly suspicious" when they slip into it.

**Cade and Mall-Lowick** are "fond exasperation, in both directions." The giant and the halfling. She reminds him of the childhood friends he left home to protect; he reminds her that some forms of watchfulness are not overcaution but presence. She can vanish behind anyone larger than herself — "the image of a halfling cartographer using a Goliath storm-priest as a bush" — and has started doing it "slightly more extravagantly." He has stopped commenting on it.

**Gilda and Mall-Lowick** have the most established prior relationship — the cartography commission, the three disputed points, the shared Halfling language. Their dynamic is a comfortable professional shorthand shot through with affectionate disagreement. Gilda views discovery as inventory to be valued; Mall-Lowick views it as knowledge to be shared. They want the same things for different reasons.

**Gilda and Rixon** recognise each other instantly as operators — two pragmatists who understand the world the same way without needing explanation. They are formidable together in cities and negotiations. The risk is real: their combined pragmatism, unchecked, can produce "genuinely questionable conclusions."

**Rixon and Mall-Lowick** are professional friction that has accidentally produced trust. He always has an exit planned; she is frequently already inside before she has considered the exit. Their recurring argument — "the exit's wrong on this one" / "there's always an exit" — has resolved into Rixon simply positioning to cover her escape rather than explaining the problem. "Which is, he will privately concede, what she was doing all along."

## The Narrative So Far

The campaign setup describes events that have already occurred before play begins, and they form a complete story in miniature:

The four were connected through Gilda's trading operation. She booked passage on the Grey Cormorant, a working ketch, to move a cargo of Dwarven metalwork to Thornwater. She hired Mall-Lowick, whom she already knew, as cartographer. Rixon was taken on as security. Cade was already aboard as the ship's chaplain. Twelve days at sea — eating at the same table, keeping overlapping watches, becoming familiar. Not a party. Just people sharing a ship.

At Thornwater, the manifest turned out to be wrong. Someone — not Gilda, who was furious — had added an undeclared consignment to the hold. The captain was detained. The cargo was impounded. Two unknown parties in Thornwater had an interest in whatever had been smuggled, and they knew the Grey Cormorant was the ship.

The four left Thornwater together. Not out of sentiment, but because each had something the others needed: Gilda had resources, Rixon had the instincts to move quietly, Mall-Lowick knew the terrain, and Cade had the healing and the steadiness "that none of the others would admit to finding useful." Three days overland by back routes to Kelstead Port.

At Kelstead, Sael was gone. The message had been collected but not answered. No one could explain where the navigator had gone. The party took a contract at the hiring hall for work west along the Fenway toward Brindlemark — a small walled town that needs things done and pays for them. Gilda needed revenue while her cargo situation resolved. Mall-Lowick wanted to map the country between Kelstead and Brindlemark. Rixon had nothing against Brindlemark. And Cade would go where the group was going, because the group was now his to watch out for.

## The Open Threads

The campaign is designed around six dormant story threads, each tied to a specific character:

1. **The Thornwater consignment** — What was it? Who added it? Who were the interested parties? This is Gilda's reckoning.
2. **Sael's disappearance** — Where did the navigator go? Was it voluntary? This is Cade's quiet dread.
3. **Orrath and the sealed forge** — What was being made? Where is Orrath now? What do the Guild records say? This is Rixon's past, and the key to the arcane power sleeping in his hands.
4. **Cade's divine connection** — Something in his Storm Giant blood is listening. What, and to what end?
5. **Rixon's discharge** — Technically honourable. The specifics are intentionally blank, to surface through an NPC or a document rather than a voluntary confession.
6. **Gilda's unilateral tendencies** — The scenario where she makes a decision on the party's behalf based on her own cost-benefit analysis, without consulting the others, and turns out to be right — which makes the breach of trust "more rather than less complicated."

## What Holds It Together

What holds this group together despite their significant differences in worldview comes down to three things:

**Competence.** Each member is excellent at what they do. This creates professional respect that predates personal trust and will survive its occasional failures.

**Self-reliance.** None of them came from positions of safety. They know how to function under pressure and do not require the team to be stable in order to operate within it.

**Adaptability.** None of them are ideologues. Cade will bend method for loyalty. Gilda will accept a lower return if the alternative costs her integrity. Rixon will fight past his cynicism when he decides something matters. Mall-Lowick will stop at a fence if someone she cares about is on the other side. They have each learned, provisionally and carefully, where the others' floors are.

This is a party built on the premise that disagreement is not an obstacle to narrative but the engine of it. Four worldviews, four different relationships to law, money, duty, and risk, held together by the slowly accreting weight of shared experience and grudging mutual regard. They are not yet friends. They are becoming something that might turn out to be more durable than friendship: people who have seen each other clearly and decided to stay.
