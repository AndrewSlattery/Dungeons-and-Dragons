# Pre-Campaign Vignettes

*Six scenes from the period before the campaign begins — one for each pair of characters, arranged in chronological order across the events described in the campaign setup.*

---

## I. Gilda + Mall-Lowick
*The Counting House, Kelstead Port — three days before The Grey Cormorant departed*

The room Gilda had taken smelled of brine and rope oil from the harbour two streets over, a smell that no amount of imported candle wax could entirely overcome. She had rented it from a chandler's house — a practical choice, because the chandler owed her a favour from a dispute about wax consignments, and practical choices were the ones she made. Her spellbook was on the desk, closed, beside a brass travelling inkwell and a small locked case. Her quarterstaff was in the corner, dark hardwood with a carved head — not a walking stick, a tool, an arcane focus for a woman who treated magic the way she treated everything else: as an instrument for specific purposes. Her rings caught the grey coastal light from the window. There were six of them across both hands, different metals and weights — gold, iron, one that looked like electrum — none of them decorative for their own sake. Emergency currency, carried where it couldn't be separated from her.

The maps were already on the table. She had weighted the corners: the inkwell, the brass compass, a small lock she kept for the purpose of weighting corners of things.

The door opened. Mall-Lowick arrived.

The Halfling came in at two and a half feet of her, which was a height that allowed her to enter most rooms without ducking but tended to put her at chest level with the furniture. She was slight and quick-eyed, with curly brown hair escaping its leather tie in several directions simultaneously. Three fingers of her right hand were ink-stained at the first joint, and there was a smudge on her left wrist she hadn't noticed. Her studded leather armour was the muted green-brown of things that spent a lot of time outdoors, and her map case — an enormous thing, leather-sealed and weather-proofed — was slung across her back as though it weighed nothing. It contained, by Mall-Lowick's own estimate, several years of her professional life.

She looked at the maps on the table and then at Gilda, and said nothing for a moment.

"The secondary harbour entrance," Gilda said, before Mall-Lowick had finished closing the door.

"Magnetic variation."

"Four hundred yards of magnetic variation."

"It's a documented phenomenon."

"In a tidal approach, four hundred yards is the difference between a successful docking and an interesting report to the Harbour-Masters' board." Gilda moved a finger across the chart, stopping on the disputed notation. "I need it corrected."

"You need it marked with a note about local compass deviation and re-surveyed with a reference instrument." Mall-Lowick hung her coat. "Which I said at the time."

Gilda looked at her. "You did."

"I did."

This was how Gilda conceded points she couldn't refute: she acknowledged them in the same voice she used for everything else, filed them, and moved on without ceremony. No softening of expression, no apology. Mall-Lowick had come to find it, in its way, more honest than most of the alternatives.

"The northern run." Gilda slid a folded contract across the table. "Full coastal profile, both approaches. Same rate as the last commission, less the outstanding balance on the three disputed sections."

"The three sections are disputed."

"The rate reflects that."

Mall-Lowick unfolded the paper and read it. Through the window, Kelstead Port was getting on with itself: the rattle of chain on chain, the knock of hull on dock, a dockworker somewhere calling out a length of rope. The harbour smell, salt and fish and tar, following it in. On the quayside below, a pair of Dwarven merchants were arguing with a cartman about something that had been misloaded. Gilda's people, in the broad sense — though Gilda would have found that categorisation inexact.

"There's a formation," Mall-Lowick said, without looking up from the contract. "About a day's sail north. The Cormorant Stack." She set the paper down. "I've been trying to get the correct bearing on it for a year and a half. The contract route takes me past it twice."

"The contract covers the full route."

"I know it does." She folded the paper. "When do we leave?"

## II. Cade + Mall-Lowick
*The Grey Cormorant, day four — passing the Cormorant Stack*

The formation had no agreed name. Mall-Lowick called it the Cormorant Stack; the name on Gilda's chart, which Mall-Lowick considered wrong for three separate reasons, was Breaker's Head. It was a long, ragged face of dark stone where the cliff dropped straight into the sea, carved at the waterline — over more centuries than anyone had counted — into a row of sea arches. Four of them, the largest spanning perhaps forty feet, the smallest a mere crack of light through the rock. She had a partial bearing on the site from two years ago, corrected by estimate, that she did not trust. She needed new data, and new data required elevation.

The shrouds of *The Grey Cormorant* put her high enough: two-thirds of the way up the port side, one foot braced against a ratline, one hand gripping a stay, her other hand pressing a folded piece of oilskin paper against her knee while she worked with a stick of charcoal. The longbow was down on the deck — she'd left it there because it was impractical to carry at elevation, which was a concession she'd made without being asked.

Below her, Cade stood and said nothing.

He was the largest person on the ship, which was saying something given the crew of seventeen. Close to eight feet of Goliath, with the broad-shouldered build of someone who had spent years hauling rope and breaking up fights — the storm-grey skin of his people, marked on his forearms with faded nautical tattoos in the style of the coastal Fenway ports. He wore a chain shirt that had been made to measure and still looked like something that had simply grown on him. His holy symbol hung at his chest on a leather cord — a silver-grey amulet, the shape abstract, like a breaking wave considered by someone who didn't quite trust representation. His shield was propped against the mainmast. His hands were loose at his sides.

He had learned, over some years of watching over people who found danger negotiable, that shouting up at them made them rush. He was very large. The deck was hard. He had assessed the distance and decided on presence over intervention.

"I need the baseline," she called down, not looking at him. "First arch to the fourth. Can you sight along your arm from where you're standing?"

He looked at the cliff face, reading the geometry. "What am I sighting to?"

"The fourth arch where it meets the waterline. Degrees off the bowsprit."

He extended his arm — a considerable arm, the arm of someone built for work — and squinted. He had the navigator's habit of thinking in angles. "Twenty-one, twenty-two degrees. Port."

Silence from above, except for charcoal on oilskin. *The Grey Cormorant* rose and fell with the long swell. To the north, the Cormorant Stack was beginning to recede into coastal haze. The sky above the cliffs was the grey-white of this stretch of coast in early season.

"You've done that before," she said.

"Ships require it."

"So does cartography." A pause. "You can stop watching me. I've done this in worse weather."

"I know," he said. He kept watching.

She almost said something about Goliath stubbornness, or the particular quality of overcaution that large people sometimes directed at small ones. She decided against it. He wasn't overcautious — she could tell the difference. He was just there, in the way of someone who had made a quiet decision about where he should be.

At the top of the descent she released the stay and swung herself down the last section without looking. Cade had moved, without making anything of it, to a position below her on the ratlines. She didn't need him. He knew she didn't need him. He was there anyway.

"The fourth arch has a distinct profile," she said, opening her notes on the deck. "I can triangulate from this and my reference from two years ago and get a reliable bearing."

"Good." He picked up his shield and settled it on his arm with the ease of long practice.

She looked at him briefly. He sounded, she thought, like someone who had been waiting for something to go correctly and was relieved that it had.

## III. Cade + Gilda
*Below decks, The Grey Cormorant, day seven*

The mess smelled of salt pork and damp wood and tallow, and it was low-ceilinged enough that Cade navigated it with the particular care of a man who had learned, through repetition, exactly where every beam was. The lantern swung with the ship's movement, making shadows move in the corners.

The cook's mate — a young human called Pell, nineteen or twenty, dark-haired — was sitting at the end of the bench with his right hand wrapped in a cloth that had soaked through at the palm. He was the kind of person who found injury more embarrassing than painful and was directing most of his attention toward the tabletop.

Cade was already there. He had his Priest's Pack open on the bench: grey canvas, battered, with the accumulated professional clutter of someone who was both a medic and a divine — tin of salve, clean cloth, a small set of instruments for the work that didn't require a spell, a sealed vial of something for infection. He was cleaning the cut with practised efficiency, talking to Pell about something in a low voice. Not invoking anything. Not performing. Just talking.

Pell, who had come in rigid, slowly stopped being either rigid or embarrassed.

Gilda arrived for her manifests and found the scene already in progress. She was in her merchant robes — the dark velvet was slightly wrong for a working ship, but the difference between overdressed and authoritative in commerce was a matter of conviction, and conviction was something she had in quantity. Her rings caught the lantern light as she sat down. Her spellbook was with her in its case, and she set it on the bench and opened her manifest folder and began to read.

She read the same line four times.

Cade wrapped Pell's hand neatly, then found a grease burn on the forearm he hadn't been asked about and treated that too. He packed his kit with the ease of someone who had done this in every condition a ship could produce. Pell went back to the galley.

Cade sat down with a cup of barley that someone had left covered on the bench.

"He's not your crew," Gilda said.

"No."

"You're not being paid for it."

"No."

She waited. He drank his barley without adding anything.

"That's not a complete answer," she said.

He looked at her across the table. She was watching him with the same attention she gave to manifests — reading for information, not for sentiment. He had noticed, over seven days of the voyage, that Gilda observed things carefully and said very little about what she observed. This was a quality he recognised. It was not the same quality as his own, but he understood it.

"It wasn't meant to be," he said. "His hand will be fine. He'll be cooking tomorrow."

"That's also not an answer."

"I know." He set the cup down. "I don't have one that would satisfy you. I did it because it mattered that he suffer less. That's the whole of it."

He said it without heat, without the particular quality of someone making a point. Just a fact, delivered plainly. It had the texture of something he'd stopped defending a long time ago.

Gilda returned to her manifests. She made a note in the margin — a small thing, the kind of notation she made when she was thinking about something other than the document. He didn't ask what she was writing. The ship moved around them, the lantern swung, and the smell of salt pork had a quality of permanence, as though it had been there before the ship was built.

After a while: "I find that position genuinely difficult to locate a flaw in," she said, as though continuing a conversation that had only been happening in her head. "That isn't the same as agreeing with it."

"I know," he said. He sounded, she thought, like he'd expected nothing different, and found it entirely acceptable.

She found this more interesting than she'd anticipated. She filed it, and did not discard it.

## IV. Cade + Rixon
*The Grey Cormorant, night watch, day ten*

The sky was overcast and moonless, and the watch had been quiet for two hours. The ship's lantern at the stern threw a pale circle that reached the after-deck and gave up on the forward. Forward was dark.

Rixon was forward. He was not easy to miss even in darkness: five foot nine and built square and heavy, a broad-shouldered human in chain mail that made a faint shifting sound with every movement, like a purse of coin. The links were old but maintained, the kind of armour that had been worked into shape by years of wear as much as by the armourer. His longsword was at his left hip, the two handaxes at his right. His hands were dealing with a badly-coiled line, working it loose in the dark. His hands were not delicate instruments — thick-fingered, calloused at the specific angles of a hammer and a sword both — and the line was not cooperating.

At the rail twenty feet aft, Cade stood and watched the water. He was never not visible on a deck, even in the dark: eight feet of grey-skinned Goliath had a quality of presence that made you aware of it before you'd consciously registered it. His holy symbol caught a pale fraction of the lantern light — the silver-grey amulet at his chest. He was looking north-east, where the overcast had a quality he'd noticed: not lightning, or not exactly. A kind of restlessness in the cloud, the way the sky looked before weather that hadn't arrived yet. He'd felt it more than seen it. He had learned, from a childhood spent below the ridge where the autumn storms came off the highlands, that this was a thing worth attending to, even when it was far away.

The line caught. Rixon swore.

He swore in Giant, which was not something he did consciously in company; it had been Orrath's language in the forge when something refused to cooperate, and it had simply become Rixon's language for those moments too. The phrase was a Greymantle construction, the particular kind that didn't appear in primers because it had grown in kitchens and forge-rooms, from people complaining to each other about things that were difficult. Something about rope. Something about the ancestry of inanimate objects.

Cade went still.

It was not a small stillness — a man of that size going still had a quality to it, a sudden absence of the minor ambient movement that even patient people produced. Rixon felt it from twenty feet away before he'd registered it consciously. He looked over.

Cade was looking at him. Not the horizon. Not the water. The expression on his face was not surprise and not recognition and was somewhere between them — something that had no name yet, something private, that he had not decided what to do with.

"Something wrong?" Rixon said.

"No." Cade looked back at the water. A pause during which the ship creaked twice and the loose line tapped against the mast. "Where did you learn your Giant?"

Rixon watched him. "A smith. In Ashford." A beat. "Why?"

The overcast shifted somewhere to the north-east. Whatever was moving in it, Cade was no longer looking at it.

"That's Greymantle phrasing," he said, carefully. "That particular construction. It's not a general highland phrase — it's specific to Greymantle Keep. I know it because my father used it."

The sea moved under the hull. A long moment in which the only sounds were the ship and the water and the far-off moan of wind through the rigging.

"The smith was from Greymantle Keep," Rixon said.

Cade nodded once, slowly. It had the quality of a confirmation rather than a surprise, as though he'd allowed himself the thought and was now permitting it to be real. He did not ask anything further. He looked back out to sea. The amulet at his chest moved with his breathing.

They stood the rest of the watch in the ordinary silence of a cold night at sea, and did not return to it. But something had altered in the way they occupied the same dark and moving space — something incremental, present in the air between them for the remaining two days of the voyage — and neither of them named it, and both of them knew it was there.

## V. Gilda + Rixon
*Thornwater quay, two hours after Vetha's detention*

The impound seal on the cargo shed was Thornwater red wax, the Harbour-Masters' mark, still soft at one edge. Gilda had read it from fifteen feet without moving closer, because moving closer would appear in a clerk's ledger under someone's name. A Dwarf in dark velvet robes examining an impound seal on the Thornwater quayside was the kind of image that did not go unrecorded.

She had moved instead to the harbour wall, which was the correct place to stand when you wanted to look like you were watching the boats. She was watching the boats. She was also watching the warehouses to her right, the impound seal, the distance to the market gate, and the light, which was flat and grey and had the quality of a northern afternoon that had committed to nothing.

Rixon was already there.

He was leaning on the wall in the manner of a man with nowhere to be and no particular interest in acquiring somewhere — his travelling cloak over the chain mail, the bulk of him obscured and unremarkable. His hands rested on the stone. He had, she noted, positioned himself so that the winch housing at the east end of the quay was in his peripheral vision. The handaxes at his hip were covered by the cloak. He might have been any man watching any harbour. He was clearly not.

"Two parties," he said, without looking at her.

"I counted two. There may be a third." She kept her eyes on the water. "Someone added to my manifest. Not me."

"How certain?"

"Entirely."

A beat while he processed this — not the content, which she suspected he'd assumed, but the implication about what kind of problem they were dealing with.

"The man by the winch housing has been there for the better part of an hour," he said. "He's not a dockworker."

"And the woman with the cart has moved it three times without loading anything."

"Yes."

A fishing vessel dragged past the harbour mouth on its way in, trailing net. The sky above it was the particular grey of a Fenway coastal afternoon. Gilda's rings caught what light there was — gold, iron, the electrum one, emergency currency worn where it couldn't be separated from her. She was quietly calculating whether they would become relevant to the next twelve hours.

"The cargo is legitimate," she said. "Every item I can document. The difficulty is that a magistrate's process in a town where I have no relationships runs a week at minimum, and by that point whatever was in the additional consignment will have been associated with the vessel that brought it in."

"Which is us."

"Which is us." She looked at him. His expression was the same expression he wore at everything: attentive, slightly flat, the eyes doing something more complicated than the face. "You've already thought about exits."

"There's a track west that doesn't cross the main road." He moved his eyes across the quay — the winch housing, the woman and the cart, the sealed shed, the harbour mouth — in a sweep that took approximately three seconds. "Your cartographer will know the terrain."

Gilda had reached this conclusion at approximately the same moment. She found it, in its way, reassuring rather than irritating. There were people who were useful in a problem and people who were a component of the problem, and knowing quickly which category applied was its own form of intelligence.

"She'll know it," Gilda said. "I'll find her."

"The Goliath?"

"Will follow without needing to be asked." She straightened from the wall, not briskly — nothing that would draw the eye from the winch housing or the woman with the cart. "Two hours, before the watch changes."

"Two hours," he agreed.

Neither of them said anything further about it. They stood at the harbour wall another moment in the way of two people watching boats, and then separated, walking in different directions, and went to find the others.

## VI. Rixon + Mall-Lowick
*The overland track, coastal scrub north of the Fenway road — day two of three*

The rough ground north of the Fenway road was scrub grass and wind-bent oak and wet limestone pushing through the surface, ground still soft from two days of rain. The main road was a mile south and below them; in the wrong wind they could hear it distantly, the sound of carts, which was the sound of other people going in the wrong direction.

Mall-Lowick moved through the scrub like she was made for it, which in certain specific respects she was. A Halfling in studded leather the colour of dead bracken could get through undergrowth at a pace that made no sense relative to her dimensions, and the leather made no sound of consequence. The longbow across her back was almost as long as she was tall, and she'd long since stopped noticing it. Beside it, her map case. In her right hand, her charcoal. Her notes were already open in her left.

Rixon was behind her. He was making more noise.

Chain mail in undergrowth was not silent even on a careful man, and he was a careful man. His dark wool cloak helped with colour but not with sound. The longsword was at his hip, the two handaxes beside it, and in his pack there was a map of the local roads that he had stopped consulting two hours ago in favour of watching where she was going. He had not commented on this.

"We're not stopping," he said.

"I'm not asking to stop." She didn't break pace. "Ten minutes, at the crest of this ridge. I need the western bearing on the Fenway confluence."

"Anyone tracking from Thornwater is tracking on the main road. Which means a ridge is a poor choice."

"Forty-five seconds of profile. Then the treeline." She pointed ahead with the charcoal. The north side of the ridge had a fast treeline — she'd noted it. "Less if we move straight."

He looked at the ridge. The treeline did come in fast. She was, he noted with the particular irritation of being unable to dispute something, not wrong about the profile exposure. The ten minutes was the problem.

"Five minutes," he said.

"Seven. Two reference points minimum."

"Six."

She was already going up the slope. "Six and a half, and eat something while I work. I can tell by the way you're moving that you haven't."

He was, in fact, hungry. The fact that she had apparently diagnosed this from his gait was annoying and accurate and too good a point to argue with. He followed her up the slope instead, pulling a strip of dried meat from his pack, and positioned himself on the north side of the crest in a place where he could watch the country below without presenting a profile against the sky.

The chain mail quieted when he went still.

She worked the way she always worked: charcoal in short certain strokes, her Halfling hands making reference marks with a precision that looked mechanical from the outside. The view west opened out below them — the long grey-green sweep of the Fenway valley, the river catching what light the sky was offering, a smudge of woodsmoke that might have been a farmstead or a travellers' camp. The road, distantly, a pale line.

"Where are we headed?" she said, without looking up. "After Kelstead."

"West. Contract at Brindlemark."

She made a mark. "I've got notes on that country. Not as complete as I'd like." A pause. "I was planning a proper survey of the stretch between Kelstead and Brindlemark this spring."

"Other things intervened."

"Yes." She turned her paper slightly, sighting a new reference. "It's not the worst outcome. At least I'll be going there with a reason to look carefully."

"Hmm," he said. He was watching the lower ground. Nothing on the track below. Nothing on the road that he could see. The smoke from the farmstead, if it was a farmstead, was not moving in a way that suggested urgency.

At six minutes she straightened and closed her notes. "All right."

He was already moving south toward the treeline. She had her map case shut and was on his heels in the time it took him to take three steps, which was the kind of efficiency he had stopped being surprised by.

They went down the north slope at pace and into the trees. Behind them, the ridge was empty — a fold of hill grass and limestone, unremarkable, a shape in the country that she had now correctly placed on her map for the first time.

The pace came up naturally in the trees, without either of them discussing it, as though the stop had given something back rather than taken something away. They made the river crossing before dark.
