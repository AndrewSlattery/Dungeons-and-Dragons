# Pre-Campaign Vignettes

*Six scenes from the period before the campaign begins -- one for each pair of characters, set across the weeks in which they found each other.*

---

## I. Osctra + Gwenwyn
*A harvest supper, Millbrake village -- six weeks before the road*

The fire had burned down to the coals by the time the last of the dancers sat. Millbrake was not a large village -- forty souls, perhaps, in the houses that lined the stream, and another dozen from the outlying farms who came down for the supper and would leave before full dark. The tables had been set on the green in the old style, planks on trestles, and the platters were nearly cleared: root vegetables, a hard yellow cheese, a mutton stew that had been cooking since morning. The shawm player had put her instrument down and was eating with both hands.

Osctra was at the far end of the table, which was the end nearest the road, which was the end he always chose. He had arrived with a cartload of late-season tubers and departed from the trading with his usual efficiency: fair weights, exact change, no pleasantries beyond those strictly necessary to confirm that the goods were sound. His canvas trousers were clean in the way that trousers belonging to a man who worked in soil were ever clean, which was to say they had been beaten against a rock that morning and represented the best available outcome. His pitchfork was leaning against the cart behind him. He was eating stew and watching the treeline.

He had been watching treelines for three weeks now. The journal in his coat pocket held seven pages of observations he did not yet know how to assemble into a conclusion.

The shawm player sat down across from him. She was slight and angular, with a battered wide-brimmed hat that she had not removed indoors or out, and her braids were pinned up under it in a way that suggested long practice at keeping hair out of the way of things. She had a bowl of stew and a heel of bread and the particular quality of appetite that belonged to people who had been playing music for three hours.

"Your tubers," she said, breaking bread. "The ones you brought down. Erdwell's wife was telling me about them."

"They're a reliable strain."

"She said they were the best she'd had in four years." She ate. "She also said the last time you brought a full cart was spring, and this load was half what you usually carry."

He looked at her. She was eating bread with the focused attention of someone who had said something precise and was waiting to see whether it had been received.

"It was a difficult season," he said.

"Erdwell's wasn't. Nor Hapworth's, nor the Linney farm on the south road." She took a spoonful of stew. "I play their suppers too. Good yields across the valley this year, except from the high ground. Except from you."

He said nothing for a moment. The fire cracked and settled. Somewhere behind them, two children were arguing about who had won a game that appeared to have no rules.

"You play a circuit," he said. It was not a question.

"Millbrake, Hapworth, the Briar villages, Thornhollow when they'll have me. I carry news. It's what the music is for, partly -- people talk to the person who played the dancing."

"And you listen."

"I listen." She set her bread down. "The Linney farm lost a goat last month. Something came down from the ridge and took it in daylight. Broad daylight, middle of the afternoon. A wolf, they said, but it was too far south for wolves by thirty miles and it was alone, and wolves that far from their range don't hunt alone unless something has driven them out."

The journal in his pocket seemed, briefly, heavier than it was.

"The Briar villages," he said. "East or west?"

"Both. West Briar had a bear come through a kitchen garden three weeks ago. East Briar hasn't seen a deer on the north trail since midsummer. I asked the huntsman -- he's been tracking that herd for six years. They moved. Overnight, he said. The whole herd."

He looked at her with a different quality of attention now. She was watching him with the quick dark eyes of someone who had already assessed what she was looking at and was confirming what she'd thought.

"I have notes," he said.

"I thought you might."

He reached into his coat and produced the journal -- leather-bound, soil-stained at the corners, the pages dense with a small, precise hand. He opened it to the most recent entry and set it on the table between them. She leaned forward and read without touching it, tilting her head to follow the notations.

"You've been tracking displacement patterns," she said.

"For three weeks. Since the ridge."

"I've been tracking them for three months." She straightened. "From the road. Different angle, same picture."

The fire had gone to grey ash. The children behind them had resolved their dispute or abandoned it. Most of the village was drifting toward the houses. The stream made its sound in the dark beyond the green, the same sound it had made for longer than either of them had been alive.

"You should come back through in a fortnight," he said. "I'll have more."

"I'll come back through in a fortnight."

She picked up her shawm and slung it across her back, collected her hat from where it had shifted, tapped the brim once, and walked toward the road. He watched her go. He had not asked her name. He realised, with the particular clarity of a man who had spent eighty years finding conversation unnecessary, that he had not needed to. She had told him everything that mattered in the space of six sentences, and the rest was detail.

He opened his journal and added an entry. Then he went back to watching the treeline.

## II. Osctra + Ix-Nambuth
*A dry-stone wall on the ridge road -- three weeks before the road*

The wall ran along the ridge for a quarter of a mile before it gave way to open grass and the long slope down to the valley floor. It was old work -- whoever had laid it had understood the stone, placing each piece so that weight held weight without mortar, the way walls were built by people who expected them to outlast the builder. Osctra had stopped to examine a section where frost had shifted a capstone. He was not in a hurry. He was rarely in a hurry. He had set his pack down and was resetting the stone with the unhurried precision of someone who could not pass a broken wall without mending it.

The sound reached him before the person did: a viol, played somewhere below the ridge on the valley side, not a performance piece but a series of phrases repeated and adjusted, the bow finding a line and then finding it again more cleanly. He recognised the quality if not the player -- someone practising, not performing. Someone who treated the instrument as a problem to be solved rather than a trick to be displayed.

He finished the capstone, dusted his hands on his apron, and looked down the slope.

She was sitting on a boulder at the edge of the treeline, sixty feet below, with the viol across her knee and the bow in her right hand. She was large -- six foot three of grey-green Orc, broad-shouldered, with a single dark braid wound with tooled leather. She wore traveller's clothes over what he could see was chain mail at the collar, and a quarterstaff was propped against the rock beside her. Her hands, which were very large even for her frame, held the bow with a precision that did not match their size, and the sound she was producing had the quality of something being built rather than something being shown.

She stopped playing, adjusted her left hand on the neck, and played the phrase again. Better. She nodded once, to herself, and looked up.

She saw him on the ridge. He saw her see him. Neither of them moved for a moment.

"The capstone was loose," he said, by way of explanation. He did not raise his voice; the ridge carried sound downward, and the air was still.

"I noticed." She set the bow across her knee. "You've been up there for ten minutes. I assumed you were either fixing the wall or deciding whether to come down."

"I was fixing the wall."

"Good wall."

"Good stone." He looked at her instrument, then at the chain mail, then at the quarterstaff. "You play the festival circuit."

"I do." She stood. Standing, she was a head taller than most humans and carried the height with the deliberate ease of someone who had spent years deciding what to do with it. "Ix-Nambuth. I was meant to play Thornhollow last week. The fair was cancelled."

He went still in a way that she noticed. Not fear -- something closer to confirmation. The particular quality of a person hearing something they had been expecting to hear and finding no comfort in being right.

"Why was it cancelled?"

"Animals on the northern road. Predators -- things that shouldn't be there, behaving in ways they shouldn't behave. The village decided it wasn't safe to gather." She picked up her quarterstaff and came up the slope toward him, moving with the steady, ground-covering stride of someone accustomed to distance. "That's the third cancellation in six weeks on my circuit. Hapworth cut their supper short. West Briar moved theirs indoors."

She reached the wall and leaned against it, the viol case on her back and the staff in her hand. Up close, her eyes were warm in a way that did not match the rest of her, which was built for a kind of work that had nothing to do with music.

"I've been noting the same thing," he said. He produced the journal. She took it when he offered, and read with the careful attention of someone who was not a naturalist but who recognised careful work when she saw it.

"Displacement patterns," she said. "You're tracking where they've moved from."

"And when. The timing matters. It's not random."

"No." She handed the journal back. "It's not." She looked out over the valley -- the long sweep of it, the treeline dark against the afternoon sky, the ridge road winding south. "Something is pushing them. Not hunting them -- pushing them. Out of the deep places."

He said nothing. He agreed, and did not need to say so. She seemed, he noted, to be a person who expected her presence to communicate things that other people communicated with words. He found this intermittently irritating and intermittently accurate.

"There's a woman on the road," he said. "Plays a shawm. Works some of the same villages you do. She's been noticing the same absences."

"Gwenwyn." Ix-Nambuth nodded. "I know her by reputation. We've played the same suppers but not the same evenings." She looked at him. "You're the farmer with the bounty."

"Small bounty."

"I heard you threw a lord over a fence."

"He was standing near it."

Something shifted in her expression -- not quite a smile, because she was not, he was beginning to understand, a person who smiled easily at things she found genuinely satisfying. More like a settling, the way a stone settled into a wall when it found its correct position.

"I'm heading south," she said. "Gwenwyn's circuit brings her through the Briar villages in a fortnight. If your notes and her notes and what I've seen on the road are all pieces of the same thing, then it would be useful to compare them in the same room."

He considered this. He was not a man who travelled in company. He had spent eighty years finding company unnecessary and, in many cases, actively detrimental to the quality of his thinking.

"The Millbrake supper," he said. "Two weeks. I'll be there for the trading."

"I'll be there for the music."

She settled the viol case on her shoulder, picked up her staff, and went south along the ridge road. He watched her go. She moved well -- not fast, but with the steady, unhurried economy of someone who had decided where she was going and did not intend to be distracted from it.

He opened his journal and made a note: *Thornhollow fair cancelled. Third in six weeks on the eastern circuit. Confirmed by Ix-Nambuth (viol, Orc, paladin?). Independent observation consistent with displacement pattern.*

He put the journal away and went back to the wall.

## III. Osctra + Cognodec
*The edge of the Ashen Wood -- ten days before the road*

The trail into the Ashen Wood was not a proper trail -- it was a deer path that had been used by people often enough to widen but not often enough to maintain, and in the wet season it became a series of muddy suggestions between the oaks. Osctra was following it because the displaced predators he had been tracking for a month had come from this direction, and because the soil here had a quality he wanted to examine: a faint greyness in the topsoil that did not correspond to any mineral deficiency he recognised. He was on one knee in the mud, holding a pinch of earth between his thumb and forefinger, sniffing it.

"The soil's wrong," said a voice behind him.

He did not startle. He turned, slowly, and found a Forest Gnome standing on the trail ten feet back, three feet and four inches of him in a dark green coat that fell to his knees, with amber eyes and dark grey hair and an orb at his shoulder that caught the filtered light in ways that were not entirely natural. His hands were clasped behind his back. He had the bearing of a person who had been watching for some time and had chosen the moment of his announcement with care.

"I know the soil's wrong," Osctra said. "That's why I'm looking at it."

"Of course." The Gnome came forward, his boots making almost no sound on the wet path. He was, Osctra noted, wearing fine clothes under the coat, which was the kind of choice that suggested either vanity or a professional requirement. His arcane focus -- the orb -- seemed to pulse faintly at the periphery of vision, though when looked at directly it was merely glass. "Cognodec. I work these woods." He paused, with the particular quality of a pause that was doing work. "In a professional capacity."

"What capacity?"

"Forest mystic. Consultant on matters of the natural and, when called upon, the slightly supernatural." He smiled, and the smile was exactly the right width and exactly the right warmth, and Osctra identified it immediately as a product of long practice rather than spontaneous feeling.

"You're a performer," Osctra said.

The smile held, but something behind it shifted -- a flicker, the barest adjustment, as though a mechanism had encountered an unexpected input. "I prefer the term 'interpretive naturalist.'"

"You're a performer who works the villages. I've seen your type. The mushroom blessings, the field consecrations. Grandmother Willowroot or whatever the name is this season."

Cognodec regarded him. The amber eyes were sharp and still, and for a moment the performance was not in them. "You're the farmer from the upper valley. The one with the bounty."

"Everyone seems to know about the bounty."

"It's a good story. A man of your description threw a lord over a fence and the lord filed charges from his sickbed. Stories like that travel quickly on the circuits I work." He came closer, and now Osctra could see the Book at his side -- leather tooled to resemble bark, brass fittings that seemed to have shifted position since the last time he'd glanced at them. It was pressed against the Gnome's ribs with the ease of long habit, and it looked, Osctra thought, heavy. Heavier than a book that size should be.

"The soil," Cognodec said, and the performance voice was gone. What replaced it was quieter and more careful, and it surprised Osctra in the way that encountering an actual plant among artificial ones was always surprising. "I've been noticing it for a fortnight. The greyness. It follows the stream west, deeper into the wood. The animals are aware of it. They won't cross it."

Osctra looked at him. The Gnome held his gaze steadily, and in that steadiness was something that was not a performance at all -- a concern that had no audience, directed at a problem that was not going to be solved by good illusions and well-timed sound effects.

"You talk to the animals," Osctra said.

"It's what I do."

"Not the performance version. The real version."

A silence. The oaks moved above them in a wind that did not reach the trail. The orb at Cognodec's shoulder caught a fragment of light and held it.

"Yes," Cognodec said. "The real version." He said it as though he were still becoming accustomed to the distinction, which Osctra suspected he was.

"What do they say?"

"Fear. Not of anything specific -- they can't name it. Something wrong in the deep places. They've been saying it for weeks. The phrasings are different -- animals have limited vocabulary and heavy concerns -- but the content is the same." He looked toward the deeper wood, where the trail darkened under the canopy. "Something is in there that should not be, and it has been in there for longer than I've been listening."

Osctra stood, brushing soil from his knees. He was a foot and a half taller than the Gnome, and broader by a proportion that made the difference comic from certain angles. But the Gnome did not seem uncomfortable with it. He seemed, Osctra thought, like someone who had spent his professional life adjusting his presence to accommodate the physical space of others, and who did it now without thinking.

"I have a journal of displacement data going back five weeks," Osctra said. "Predator movements, herd shifts, den abandonments. I've been mapping the pattern."

"I would very much like to see it."

He handed the journal over. Cognodec took it with both hands -- the hands of someone who understood that a field journal was not a casual document -- and read. He read slowly, with the attention of someone who was not pretending to understand the content, and Osctra revised a small number of his initial estimates.

"Your notation system," Cognodec said, still reading. "The shorthand for direction of movement. Is that your own?"

"Standard Orcish agricultural notation. Adapted."

"It's good." He looked up. "You're methodical."

"I'm a farmer. Methodology is the profession."

Cognodec closed the journal and handed it back. "There's a supper at Millbrake in a few days. I've been asked to perform a harvest blessing. The standard kind -- the kind that makes people feel the season has been properly acknowledged." He paused. "There are others who are noticing. A bard on the road. A paladin on the festival circuit."

"I know them. Or know of them."

"I thought you might." He adjusted the orb at his shoulder, a habitual gesture that Osctra suspected was as much nervous habit as practical necessity. "I think we are all looking at the same problem from different positions. I think comparing those positions would be useful."

Osctra studied him. The Gnome was, he was fairly certain, still a charlatan. The fine clothes, the theatrical manner, the carefully constructed persona -- these were not the tools of a man who dealt in straightforward truth. But the soil concern was real. The animal reports were real. And the Book at his side was heavier than it should be, in a way that Osctra could not explain and that Cognodec, he suspected, could not either.

"Millbrake," Osctra said. "I'll be there."

"Excellent." The performance voice returned, smooth and warm and perfectly calibrated. "I'll bring the blessing. You bring the journal."

He turned and walked back down the trail, the green coat swaying, the orb catching light. Osctra watched him go and wondered, not for the first time, what it looked like when a person discovered that the story they had been telling was true.

## IV. Ix-Nambuth + Gwenwyn
*The Briar crossroads -- twelve days before the road*

The crossroads where the Briar road met the south track had a well, a milestone, and a signpost that had been repainted so many times that the underlying wood had developed the texture of candle wax. The well was good -- Gwenwyn had been drinking from it for two years of circuit riding and had never once been sick, which was not something she could say about every well between here and the eastern road. She was sitting on the well wall with her legs crossed and her hat tipped back, eating an apple she had been given in East Briar in exchange for three songs and the news that the Hapworth bridge was passable again.

The sound of the viol reached her before the player did.

It was not a performance. It was the same quality of sound she sometimes heard from serious craftspeople working alone -- iterative, precise, not meant for anyone. A phrase played, considered, adjusted. She stopped chewing and listened. The player was good. Very good, in the specific way that meant years of training under someone demanding, because no one taught themselves to hold a line that cleanly.

Ix-Nambuth came around the bend in the south track with the viol under her chin, still playing, the bow moving in her right hand with the controlled precision of a stonemason's chisel. She was in traveller's clothes over chain mail, the quarterstaff lashed to her pack, and she moved with the steady ground-covering walk of someone who measured distances in days rather than miles. She saw Gwenwyn and did not stop playing. She finished the phrase, set the bow across the strings, and looked at her.

"Good well?" she said.

"Best between here and Hapworth." Gwenwyn took another bite of apple. "You're Ix-Nambuth. You played the Thornhollow midsummer two years ago. I was there -- I was doing the dancing music. You played something afterwards that made the entire room go quiet, and then you left."

"I remember the room going quiet." She set the viol in its case and came to the well. "I don't remember the dancing music."

"You wouldn't. I was playing the shawm. Nobody remembers the shawm -- they remember the dancing. That's the point."

Ix-Nambuth drank from the well in the efficient manner of someone who had been walking for hours. She set the cup back on its chain and sat on the wall beside Gwenwyn, which meant she was a foot and a half above her and had to angle herself to make the conversation feel like a conversation rather than an address from a height.

"The Thornhollow fair was cancelled," Gwenwyn said.

"I know. I was going to play it." Ix-Nambuth looked at the crossroads, the signpost, the south track winding away. "That's three."

"Four, if you count the Linney gathering. They didn't cancel, but they moved it indoors and half the valley didn't come." She set the apple core on the wall. "I've been keeping count. It's accelerating."

"What have you heard?"

Gwenwyn answered the question the way she answered most questions -- sideways, through detail, letting the picture assemble itself. She described the wolf at the Linney farm, the bear in West Briar's kitchen garden, the deer herd that had vanished from the north trail. She described Erdwell's wife talking about the quiet that had settled over the upper pastures, and the huntsman in East Briar who had stopped going out alone. She described these things not as a list but as a landscape, connecting each observation to the next the way a road connected villages, so that by the time she had finished Ix-Nambuth was seeing not individual incidents but a pattern.

Ix-Nambuth listened the way she always listened: with her whole body still and her eyes doing something more careful than her posture suggested. She did not interrupt. She did not ask clarifying questions until Gwenwyn had finished, and then her questions were precise and few.

"The timing," Ix-Nambuth said. "You said the Linney wolf was three weeks ago. The deer herd -- when did they move?"

"Midsummer or just after. The huntsman couldn't give me an exact date."

"That's earlier than I thought." She looked at her hands -- the large, precise hands that held the bow. "I first noticed at a fair that wasn't. Five weeks ago. But if the deer moved at midsummer, whatever is causing this has been building for months."

"It has."

A silence, which was not uncomfortable. Gwenwyn had learned, across two years of reading rooms and roads, to distinguish the silences of people who had nothing to say from the silences of people who were deciding what mattered enough to say. This was the second kind.

"There's a farmer," Ix-Nambuth said. "Wood Elf, upper valley. He's been tracking the displacement patterns in a field journal. I met him on the ridge road."

"Osctra. I know him. Or I know his tubers, which is almost the same thing." She pulled her hat forward and tapped the brim -- the habitual gesture she made before crossings, before decisions, before things that changed direction. "He told me to come back through Millbrake in a fortnight. He'd have more data."

"He told me the same thing." Ix-Nambuth stood. Standing, she was a silhouette against the afternoon sky, and the viol case on her back and the chain mail at her collar produced the particular contradiction that was, Gwenwyn was beginning to understand, the entire shape of her. "There's also a Gnome. Forest mystic, works these woods. Cognodec. I've heard his name but we haven't met."

"I've seen his work. He did a field blessing at Hapworth last spring that had the old women in tears." Gwenwyn paused. "Couldn't tell if it was real."

"Does it matter?"

The question landed in a way that surprised Gwenwyn, because it was the kind of question she usually asked other people, and hearing it from outside was a different experience. She considered it.

"It might," she said. "If whatever is happening in the forest needs something real to answer it."

Ix-Nambuth nodded once. Not agreement, exactly -- acknowledgement. She picked up her pack, settled the viol case, and checked the signpost with the air of someone confirming what she already knew.

"Millbrake," she said. "The supper."

"I'll be playing."

"I'll be playing too." She looked at Gwenwyn. "We could play together, if the room has space for it."

"A viol and a shawm." Gwenwyn considered this. "That's either going to be beautiful or extremely confusing."

"Both, probably." Something in her expression shifted -- not a smile, but the warmth in her eyes deepened in a way that communicated something she had not put into words and probably could not. Then she turned and walked south on the track, and Gwenwyn watched her go, and thought about the particular quality of a person who believed that being present was the same as speaking, and wondered whether she was right.

She picked up her shawm and played a phrase -- not the dancing music, not the road music, but something she hadn't played before, a line that wanted a viol under it.

## V. Ix-Nambuth + Cognodec
*A clearing in the Ashen Wood -- eight days before the road*

The clearing was small and ringed with ash trees that had been old when the nearest village was a farmstead. The light came through the canopy in the particular way that forest light came through in autumn -- diffuse, golden-grey, the shadows long and soft. There was a quality to the air that was difficult to name: a stillness that was not silence, a weight that was not pressure, the sense of being observed by something that had been there for a very long time and was not in any hurry.

Ix-Nambuth knew this clearing. She had slept here three years ago, on a night between villages, and had woken to find something watching her -- not a creature, not exactly. More like a quality of attention. Ancient, unhurried, certain. She had not spoken any vow she could recall. She had woken the next morning understanding one had been made.

She came back to it when she could. Not often -- the clearing was off the main tracks, and reaching it required an hour's walk through undergrowth that her chain mail did not make quieter. But when the circuit brought her near, she came, and sat, and did not play. The oath asked many things of her, and one of them, she had decided, was that she return to the place where it had been given and be still.

She was sitting against an ash trunk with her eyes closed when she heard the Gnome.

He was not quiet, exactly, but he was small and light-footed, and what she heard first was not footsteps but a murmur -- low and theatrical, directed at something near the ground. She opened her eyes.

Cognodec was kneeling at the edge of the clearing, twenty feet from her, speaking to a hare. The hare was sitting upright in the leaf litter, ears forward, and was apparently listening. The Gnome's amber eyes were focused, and his voice had the particular quality she had heard in certain Gnomish elders -- a register that was not loud and not soft but was somehow both intimate and projecting, designed to be heard by exactly one listener.

He was speaking Gnomish.

"Lle thau vaen," he said to the hare. *You are safe here.* "Mas erthiel?" *What frightened you?*

The hare said something. Gwenwyn would have heard only a hare's chittering. Ix-Nambuth, who spoke Gnomish and who had spent three years learning to listen to things she could not fully explain, heard the Gnome's responses and understood the shape of the conversation: fear, displacement, a territory abandoned because something in it had changed, though the hare could not say what.

"Henion," Cognodec said. *I understand.* He reached into his coat and produced a small piece of dried apple, which he set on the ground. The hare considered it, ate it, and departed into the undergrowth.

Cognodec stood, adjusted his orb, and turned. He saw Ix-Nambuth.

"That was not a performance," she said, in Gnomish.

He went still. Not the theatrical stillness of a man holding a pose, but the genuine stillness of someone who had been seen doing something private. His Gnomish, when it came, was fluent in the wrong registers and slightly formal in the right ones -- native-born, she could tell, but spoken in a voice that had been trained to be other voices.

"No," he said, in Gnomish. "It was not."

She studied him. He was three feet and four inches of carefully constructed persona, from the fine green coat to the orb at his shoulder to the amber eyes that he had, she suspected, implied were a blessing rather than a bloodline feature. But the conversation with the hare had not been constructed. The concern in it had been real, in the way that music played alone in the woods was real -- not for anyone, not performing anything, simply present.

"You speak Gnomish as a birthright," she said, still in Gnomish.

"And you speak it as Zib Tumbletock taught it." He said the name with recognition but not familiarity. "I know Zib by reputation. The Gnomish musicians' circuit talks about her."

"Him."

"Him. My apologies." He came closer, into the clearing proper, and she noticed for the first time that the Book at his side had a quality she could not name -- a weight that did not correspond to its size, a presence that was not quite inanimate. The brass fittings had shifted since she had first looked at them. She was certain of this. "You come here."

"I come here."

"May I ask why?"

She considered the question. In Common she would have deflected it -- would have said something about the route, about resting between villages, about the convenience of the clearing. In Gnomish, which was the language Zib had taught her to be precise in, she said: "Something spoke to me here. Three years ago. I don't know what. I come back because the oath requires it."

He looked at her. The performance was entirely absent from his face. What was there instead was recognition -- not of the specific experience, but of the specific quality of being claimed by something you had not chosen and could not fully explain. She saw him see it. She saw him decide not to pretend otherwise.

"The patron," he said, "came to me approximately one year ago. Or made itself apparent. I suspect it had been watching longer." He touched the Book at his side, a gesture that was habitual and slightly nervous. "I was a charlatan before that. I am still, by trade, a charlatan. But the power is real, and the Book is real, and the animals' fear is real, and I am -- " He paused. "I am becoming something I was pretending to be. I find this theologically irregular."

She almost smiled. "I find my own situation theologically irregular."

"Then we are well matched."

A silence in the clearing. The ash trees moved in a wind that did not reach the ground. The quality of attention was still there -- ancient, unhurried -- and both of them were aware of it, and neither of them commented on it, because some things were better served by being present than by being named.

"There's a gathering," she said. "Millbrake. The harvest supper. A farmer and a bard are comparing notes on what is wrong in these woods. I think the notes will be worth seeing."

"I'll be there. I've been asked to perform a blessing."

"Will it be real?"

The question was not hostile. It was precise, in the way Zib had taught her to be precise -- a Gnomish question, asked in the Gnomish register that did not permit evasion.

He held her gaze. "I don't know yet," he said. "It is becoming more real every time. I think that may be the point."

She nodded. It was the most honest thing she had heard him say, and she suspected it was the most honest thing he had said in a considerable time. She did not comment on this. She had decided to wait and see, and waiting was something she was very good at.

They sat in the clearing for a while longer, in the particular silence of two people who had both been claimed by things they could not name, and who found in each other's company a quality of recognition that did not require further explanation.

Then Cognodec stood, dusted his coat, and said in Common, with the full warmth of the performance voice: "It has been a genuine pleasure."

"Likewise," she said, in Common, and watched him walk back into the trees, the green coat disappearing into the green wood, the orb catching the last of the afternoon light.

## VI. Gwenwyn + Cognodec
*The lane outside Millbrake, the evening before the harvest supper*

The lane ran between hedgerows that had been laid in the old style -- hawthorn and blackthorn woven together until they formed a wall of thorns and small white flowers, dense enough to stop livestock and tall enough to block the wind from the east. The evening light was amber and level, the sun dropping behind the western ridge, and the lane was in that particular state between day and night when colours became more vivid before they disappeared entirely.

Gwenwyn was walking toward Millbrake from the east, her shawm across her back, her pack slung low on one shoulder. She was carrying a bundle of dried herbs in her right hand -- rosemary and thyme, collected from a kitchen garden two villages back, payment in kind for a song about a pig that had escaped and been found in a chapel. The herbs smelled of the road, which was the smell of dust and distance and the particular sweetness of crushed thyme that happened when you carried it in a warm hand.

She heard the performance before she saw the performer.

It was not music. It was a voice -- warm, measured, pitched to carry without shouting -- describing, to an audience she could not yet see, the properties of a particular fungus that grew on the north side of ash trees. The description was confident, detailed, and delivered with the cadence of someone who had rehearsed the authority until it was indistinguishable from the genuine article.

She came around the bend and found Cognodec sitting on a stile, speaking to a cat.

The cat was a large tabby with one torn ear and the expression of an animal that had heard better, and it was sitting on the stile post and regarding the Gnome with the tolerant contempt that cats reserved for people who talked too much. Cognodec was gesturing with one hand -- the other held the Book pressed to his side -- and his amber eyes had the particular brightness of someone enjoying his own material.

He saw her and did not stop talking. He finished his point about the fungus, addressed a brief closing remark to the cat -- which ignored him -- and turned to her.

"The cat is not impressed," Gwenwyn said.

"Cats rarely are. That's what makes them reliable critics." He hopped down from the stile. At three foot four, the landing was a considered operation, and he managed it with the practised grace of someone who had been navigating a world built for larger people for longer than most humans had been alive. "Gwenwyn. The shawm."

"Cognodec. The blessings." She leaned against the stile. "I was at Hapworth when you did the spring planting ceremony. You made three old women cry."

"They were moved by the ritual."

"They were moved by you. The ritual was excellent theatre -- the timing, the incense, the Minor Illusion of the sprouting seedling. Very convincing."

He looked at her. She was looking at him with the quick dark eyes that had, he noticed, the particular quality of someone who watched performances for a living and could read the machinery behind them. She was not hostile. She was not even critical. She was, he recognised, a professional assessing another professional, and the assessment was neither approving nor disapproving but something more dangerous: accurate.

"You see the mechanics," he said.

"I see the mechanics." She adjusted her hat. "I also see that the seedling illusion was backed by something that wasn't an illusion. The light changed when you spoke the closing words. Not an effect -- I know effects. Something responded."

A silence. The hedge-sparrows were calling in the hawthorn, the evening sound, repetitive and urgent. The light had gone from amber to gold.

"The patron," he said. "The -- entity I work for. It does that occasionally. Adds emphasis at moments I haven't planned."

"Is that comfortable?"

"Not entirely." He looked at the Book, which was, she noticed, pulled tighter against his ribs than it needed to be. "I was a charlatan for forty years. I was extremely good at it. The work was genuine -- people needed what I provided, and I provided it carefully. But the mechanism was performance. Illusion, timing, the right words at the right moment. What is happening now is --" He paused, and she watched the pause, and saw in it the shape of someone reaching for the right words and discovering that the right words required honesty he had not planned on offering. "What is happening now is that the performance is becoming the thing it was performing. And I am not entirely certain what that means."

She nodded. She did not say anything for a moment, and the silence was the kind she used when she wanted someone to hear what they had just said, without adding anything that might let them retreat from it.

"I answer questions sideways," she said eventually. "It's what I do. People tell me I've changed the subject, and then three sentences later they realise I answered completely. I do it because sideways is usually the truest angle on anything worth seeing."

"That's an excellent description of misdirection."

"It's an excellent description of perspective." She shifted the herbs to her other hand. "Here's a sideways question: when the Book gets heavy, what story are you in?"

He looked at her. The performance voice was gone. The amber eyes were still and sharp and surprised, the surprise of a man who had spent decades asking the right questions of others and was not accustomed to having one asked of him.

"I don't know yet," he said.

"That's the first honest answer you've given me."

"I know."

She pushed off from the stile and walked toward the village. He fell into step beside her -- or rather, into the three-steps-to-her-two rhythm that Gnomes and humans developed when they walked together. The lane narrowed as it approached Millbrake, and the hedgerows gave way to fences, and then to gardens, and then to the first houses with their evening smoke rising into the last of the light.

"Tomorrow," she said. "The supper. Osctra's bringing his journal. Ix-Nambuth is bringing her observations from the circuit. You're bringing a harvest blessing that may or may not be real."

"It will be as real as I can make it. Which is, at this point, more real than I'm comfortable with."

"Good." She tapped the brim of her hat. "I'm bringing the dancing music. That's always been real." She glanced at him. "We should talk about what we're going to do. All four of us. Not separately, not at the edges of conversations. Properly."

"At the supper?"

"After. When the village has gone to bed and the fire is coals. That's when the useful talking happens."

He considered this. She was right, and he knew she was right, and the fact that she had arrived at this conclusion by a route he recognised -- reading rooms, reading people, knowing when the moment was correct -- produced in him a quality of professional respect that was, he suspected, mutual.

"After the supper," he said. "I'll bring my notes."

"Bring the Book too." She did not look at him when she said it. "I want to see how heavy it is."

They walked into Millbrake together as the light failed, and the smoke from the chimneys rose into a sky that was darkening from the east, and somewhere in the deep places of the forest behind them, something that had been wrong for months continued to be wrong, patient and unhurried, waiting for someone to come looking for it.
