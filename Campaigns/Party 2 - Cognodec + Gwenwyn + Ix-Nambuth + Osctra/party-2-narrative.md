# Party 2: Characters and Narrative

## A Story About Four People Who Were Looking at the Same Thing

Party 2 is not a group that was assembled. It is a group that converged. Four people, working the same stretch of rural country from different angles and for different reasons, who independently noticed that something was wrong with the world and, one by one, found their way to the same table. There is no inciting contract, no hiring hall, no professional convenience that explains why they are together. What explains it is simpler and stranger: the forest is breaking, and they are the ones who noticed.

The campaign's tone is low fantasy in focus, not in fact. The Greenwild is genuinely ancient, genuinely strange, and whatever is in its deep places is genuinely getting closer. But the party does not live in the world of the Greenwild's interior. They live in the world of harvest suppers and seasonal fairs, of village greens and ridge roads and the Thornvael circuit, where itinerant musicians play for dancers and traveling mystics bless fields at planting. Their stakes are ecological and communal before they are epic: cancelled fairs, displaced predators, frightened animals, the slow darkening of a rural landscape that depends on rhythms now being disrupted. The extraordinary is surfacing through the ordinary, and these four are the people standing at the boundary, looking in.

What makes them distinctive as a party is their shared moral outlook and the particular kind of tension this creates. All four of them share the same instinct: do the right thing, and do not wait for permission. They do not argue about whether to help. They argue about how, and when, and who gets to decide. The absence of anyone inclined to follow procedure and the absence of a selfish calculator means they will sometimes overspend on doing the right thing and sometimes do it in ways that a more cautious group would have argued themselves out of. They are a more internally coherent party, and in some ways a more dangerous one -- a party that agrees on ends can take actions that a more fractious group would argue themselves out of.

## The Characters

### Osctra -- The Farmer with the Pitchfork

Osctra does not look like an Elf. He looks like a brick oven with pointed ears. At roughly eighty years old, built broad and thick-muscled from decades of hauling soil, digging irrigation trenches, and wrestling stubborn livestock, he has the deeply tanned, weather-beaten skin and callused hands of a man whose relationship with the earth is not spiritual but transactional in the most honourable sense: he gives it attention, and it gives him extraordinary produce. His dark hair is tied back in a utilitarian knot that mostly conceals his heritage. His eyes -- "the clear, sharp green of a healthy leaf held up to sunlight" -- are the one feature that gives him away, and the one detail that does not fit the rest of him at all.

His weapon is a pitchfork. Not a polearm dressed up as a pitchfork -- a pitchfork, beautifully balanced and wickedly sharp, clearly a craftsman's tool. His shield is the reinforced ironwood lid of a massive pickling barrel. His framing hammers hang from his belt. Everything about him communicates a man whose combat capability is an extension of his working life rather than a departure from it. When he works his small, innate connection to the land, there is no mystical flourish: "he snaps his fingers to check the wind direction, sniffs the air to gauge soil composition, or once -- to win an argument about alkalinity -- made an entire hillside of wildflowers bloom mid-sentence and then went back to eating."

He was born into a small Wood Elf community at the head of Hawthorn Valley and, unlike most of his kind, stayed -- not from inability to leave but because the soil was exceptional and there was work to do. Over six decades, he transformed a modest plot into a living laboratory of botanical cultivation, centred on a magically-enhanced strain of Orcish root tubers known for extraordinary yield, remarkable shelf life, and maddening sensitivity to growing conditions. His twice-yearly trading brought him into regular contact with Orcish merchants whose exacting quality standards he found oddly agreeable. He learned Orcish because it has, in his considered view, far more precise words for agricultural concepts than Elvish does. He was never social. But he was known: a fair dealer, a patient neighbour, and someone who expected to be left alone.

The destruction of his farm is the wound that drives him onto the road. A local lord -- Vaylen Torth, a man of inherited everything -- rode into Hawthorn Valley with a hunting party, flushed a displaced predator through Osctra's property, and between the beast and the horses, demolished two hundred years of carefully maintained botanical work. When Osctra requested compensation, the lord laughed and called him a "dirt-grubbing peasant." What followed was not rage in the conventional sense. He went very, very quiet -- "the kind of quiet that had the knights' hands moving toward their hilts before they fully understood why" -- picked up his pitchfork, and systematically dismantled the entire hunting party without killing anyone. He threw the lord bodily over his own ruined fence. There is now a modest bounty on his head.

His personality is expressed through brevity, precision, and a reflex for assessing the health of every living thing he encounters -- plants, animals, and, disconcertingly, people. He communicates in brief sentences, long silences, and the occasional pointed clearing of the throat. He considers small talk a form of agricultural waste. His contempt for authority and entitlement is so deeply ingrained that it can misfire against genuinely reasonable people simply because they happen to be wearing a title or a fine coat. His ideal -- Growth -- is a philosophy of reciprocity: things that take without giving back are the enemy, applied broadly.

The vignettes give him the best entrance in the set. At the Millbrake harvest supper, sitting at the end of the table nearest the road, eating stew and watching the treeline with seven pages of displacement observations in his coat pocket, he is approached by Gwenwyn, who proceeds to demonstrate that she has been tracking the same phenomenon from the road. The exchange is spare, exact, and deeply satisfying -- two competent people recognising competence in each other without needing to say so. He does not ask her name. He does not need to. "She had told him everything that mattered in the space of six sentences, and the rest was detail." On the ridge road with Ix-Nambuth, he watches her practice the viol and identifies the quality of her attention -- "someone who treated the instrument as a problem to be solved rather than a trick to be displayed" -- and something in this registers as familiar, even if the expression is entirely alien to his own methods.

His unresolved threads are concrete and painful: a farm in ruins, a prize herd scattered across four valleys, a greenhouse in ashes, a bounty that makes returning home inadvisable, and the field journal full of displacement data that represents months of watching the world he tended for decades come apart at the seams.

### Gwenwyn -- The Road's Own Instrument

Gwenwyn is slight and angular, built for slipping through a crowd rather than standing in one. Her face reads younger than it is until she makes direct eye contact, at which point the road shows through. She wears layered traveller's browns and faded greens, adorned with small road-collected tokens: dried seedpods at her collar, a red thread around one wrist, a wooden button she claims came from somewhere interesting and will not specify further. Her battered wide-brimmed hat is treated with superstitious regularity -- straightened and tapped at the brim before crossing bridges, before decisions, before things that change direction. Her shawm, slung across her back, sounds like "a cheerful argument between a goose and a trumpet." Her two polished hardwood clubs she has never once introduced as weapons. They are, she will tell you, walking sticks.

She was born on a cart -- literally, though the cart was not going anywhere interesting. She does not remember her parents with much clarity. What she remembers is city streets, learning which doors to knock on and which to read from a distance. She fell in with traveling performers young, then found the old herbalist -- or rather, the herbalist found her. Edda spoke to plants when she harvested them, apologised to animals she had to put down, and maintained that the world had a great deal to say if you would stop making noise long enough to hear it. Gwenwyn absorbed this selectively: she speaks to animals constantly and plays the shawm at a volume that would embarrass a battlefield. But something from those years took root -- a sense of the land as a thing that communicates, a feeling for when it is speaking wrongly.

Edda died two winters ago, somewhere on the eastern road. Gwenwyn buried her in a copse of birch trees, sat there for three days, and then got up and went back to work, because that was what Edda would have done. She still sometimes catches herself starting sentences addressed to her. The gap Edda left has a shape that other things could fill.

For the past two years, Gwenwyn has been working a circuit of rural settlements -- Kettleford, Millbrake, Greenwild Edge, the farming hamlets along the Thornvael road. She plays music people can dance to, passes news from village to village, and has accumulated a comprehensive mental map of the region's rhythms. Her ideal -- Passing It On -- frames what she does as a single integrated function: music and news are the same thing done differently, and both are forms of care. She answers questions sideways, appearing to change the subject, and you realise three sentences later that she answered completely. She is constitutionally incapable of passing a sleeping animal without confirming it is all right.

She has excellent luck, in the specific way that makes superstitious people uncomfortable. She has noticed this and occasionally exploits it, which is worse.

The vignettes capture her intelligence in action. At the Millbrake supper, she approaches Osctra not with a greeting but with an observation -- his cartload is half what he usually carries, and she knows this because she plays the circuits that buy his produce. She lays out the displacement pattern from the road perspective -- the wolf at the Linney farm, the bear in West Briar's kitchen garden, the deer herd that vanished overnight -- not as a list but as a landscape, connecting each observation to the next the way a road connects villages. At the crossroads with Ix-Nambuth, she identifies the Orc musician from a performance two years prior, remembering not the performer but the effect: the room going quiet. And with Cognodec on the lane outside Millbrake, she watches his performance with professional precision, sees both the mechanics and the moments where something real interrupts them, and asks the question he is not prepared for: "When the Book gets heavy, what story are you in?" She tells him it is the first honest answer he has given her. He knows.

She is the party's social connective tissue -- the one who suggested they pool their observations properly, the one who knows when the useful talking happens (after the supper, when the fire is coals), the one who reads rooms and reads people and adjusts in real time. Her relationship to information is kinetic: it moves, it connects, it is for passing on. Where Osctra documents and Cognodec interprets, Gwenwyn circulates.

### Ix-Nambuth -- The Viol and the Oath

Ix-Nambuth is six foot three of deliberate contradiction. She has the broad shoulders, grey-green skin, prominent lower canines, and general architectural scale of someone built to end arguments -- and she carries a viol case on her back and a quarterstaff in her hand, "which means she always looks simultaneously like she is going to perform and like she is going to hit something, and the ambiguity is entirely intentional." Her hair is in a single thick braid wound with tooled leather she carved herself and has never explained. Her hands are very large, even for her frame, but anyone who watches her play notices the precision of the bow-hold, and the contrast produces a specific quality of silence in a room that she has found professionally useful. Her eyes are unexpectedly warm. This catches people off guard, and she has long since decided to let it.

She grew up in Ashgrove, a borderland settlement mixed enough that nobody quite belonged to anything, which meant everyone had to decide for themselves what they were. She decided early that she was not going to be what people assumed when they looked at an Orc of her size, and she has been refining that decision ever since. Music crossed borders that her appearance did not. She was seventeen when she first played for coin -- a drum, at a market, badly -- and something in the crowd's response told her this was worth pursuing with more seriousness than she had applied to anything else.

Zib Tumbletock found her four years later and changed everything. An elderly Gnomish musician with exacting opinions about technique, a second-hand viol, and the conviction that beauty was a serious undertaking and the world required more of it. Zib corrected her technique, taught her Gnomish, and introduced her to the idea that music was not a tool for crossing borders but a thing worth doing for its own sake. Ix-Nambuth absorbed this completely. Zib is one of approximately three reasons she is not a completely different person. Zib lives, she believes, in a cottage near Mosshallow. She tries not to worry about this with any regularity.

The oath came three years ago, in a clearing in the Greenwild, on a night between villages. She woke to find something watching her -- "not a creature, exactly. More like a quality of attention. Ancient, unhurried, and entirely certain about what it was doing." She did not speak any vow she can recall. She woke understanding one had been made. She found this theologically irregular and has never fully resolved it, but the power is real, and the weight of what it asks is real, and on balance she has decided that whatever claimed her had reasonable taste. Her ideal -- Presence -- treats joy not as decoration but as infrastructure: a village where the fair has been cancelled, where the fire went unlit, where the music went unplayed, is not a small thing. She treats its absence with the seriousness other people reserve for war.

Her flaw is precise and poignant: she assumes that being present communicates that she cares. She is consistently surprised when it does not, and consistently too late to say it another way. She is quieter than people expect. She notices small beautiful things -- a chord that resolves unexpectedly, the quality of late afternoon light, a bird doing something technically interesting -- with the unsentimental attention of a craftsperson. She does not comment on these observations. She stops for a moment, and then continues.

The vignettes give her remarkable scenes. On the ridge road with Osctra, practising alone on a boulder, she is observed without knowing it -- and when she does, the exchange that follows has the quality of two careful people sizing each other up by what they do rather than what they say. Her reaction to the cancelled Thornhollow fair -- the third in six weeks -- registers on Osctra not as surprise but as confirmation: "the particular quality of a person hearing something they had been expecting to hear and finding no comfort in being right." In the Ashen Wood clearing with Cognodec, she returns to the place where the oath was given and finds him speaking to a hare in Gnomish -- real Gnomish, not performance -- and the recognition between them is immediate and private: two people who have been claimed by things they cannot name, finding in each other a quality of understanding that does not require further explanation. At the Briar crossroads with Gwenwyn, she proposes they play together at Millbrake -- a viol and a shawm -- and when Gwenwyn says it will be either beautiful or extremely confusing, Ix-Nambuth's response is "Both, probably," followed by something in her expression that is not a smile but communicates something she has not put into words and probably could not.

### Cognodec -- The Charlatan Who Became the Thing He Was Pretending to Be

Cognodec is three feet and four inches of precision-crafted persona. He has amber eyes common to certain Forest Gnome bloodlines, though he has, in certain towns and certain lights, implied they are the result of a blessing. He has never corrected the impression. His dark grey hair has always been dark grey; he was born with it, his grandmother had the same, and he considers it distinguished. His coat is technically two sizes too large, falls to his knees, and he considers this a feature. His orb catches light in ways that are not entirely explicable. The book his patron gave him is bound in leather tooled to resemble bark, with brass fittings that appear to shift position if you look away from them. He looks, in total, "exactly like a Forest Gnome performing the role of Forest Gnome Mystic. The unsettling detail is that he now is one."

He was the fourth of seven children in Mosshallow, a Forest Gnome community in the edge-wood that shifts slightly with the seasons and which most outsiders pass without noticing. He was not the most magical of his siblings, nor the most woodwise. He was the most watchful -- a talent for understanding what people expected to see. He left at forty-three with a knack for conjuring small illusions, a borrowed coat, and the working theory that a traveling Forest Mystic willing to provide genuine-seeming encounters with the natural world was an underserved market in the human settlements to the south. He was correct. He spent decades refining the performance: consultations with forest spirits, blessing ceremonies for new fields, interpretations of signs and portents involving excellent illusions and well-timed sound effects. His kit for producing convincing false documents generated testimonials from Forest Councils that may or may not have existed. His ability to speak with animals -- the real gift, the Gnomish birthright -- he kept out of the performance entirely, unwilling to cheapen it.

The patron arrived approximately one year ago. Or made itself apparent. He woke to find his working notebook replaced by something that was not a notebook -- a bound thing that felt different in each hand, opened to different pages depending on his mood, contained workings he had not learned and rituals written in ink he had not supplied. A voice in a dream gave a name he suspects is an alias. He refers to it as the Green Advocate and has begun to wonder whether naming a thing gives it a reality it did not previously have. He has formed the working theory that the patron found him funny. He is not entirely certain this is a comfortable thing to be.

His defining quality is the layered relationship between performance and reality. He will freely tell you he is a charlatan. This is itself a form of misdirection, and he knows it, and will tell you that too if you ask the right question. No one ever does. His ideal -- The Right Story -- marks his current trajectory: he has spent his career giving people the story they needed, and he is beginning to suspect that what is wrong with the world right now does not yet have its right story, and that finding or building one may be the most genuinely useful thing he has ever done. His flaw is the mirror image of this gift: he is excellent at appearing to understand things he does not, which has occasionally prevented him from admitting -- to himself as much as to others -- when he is genuinely lost.

The vignettes capture his layered nature with remarkable precision. On the trail in the Ashen Wood, he approaches Osctra with a performer's timing and a performer's smile -- "exactly the right width and exactly the right warmth" -- and Osctra identifies him instantly as a performer. But then the performance drops: the soil concern is real, the animal reports are real, and the Book at his side is heavier than it should be. The moment Cognodec says "Yes. The real version" -- about his ability to speak with animals -- is written as "though he were still becoming accustomed to the distinction," and Osctra watches him go and wonders "what it looked like when a person discovered that the story they had been telling was true." In the clearing with Ix-Nambuth, his private conversation with a hare in Gnomish is the scene where the performance is most completely absent -- a genuine concern directed at a frightened animal, with no audience and no theater. When she confronts him, gently, in Gnomish, he admits: "I was a charlatan before that. I am still, by trade, a charlatan. But the power is real, and the Book is real, and the animals' fear is real, and I am -- becoming something I was pretending to be. I find this theologically irregular." On the lane with Gwenwyn, the full structure of his predicament is laid bare: the performance is becoming the thing it was performing, and he does not entirely know what that means. Gwenwyn's sideways question -- "When the Book gets heavy, what story are you in?" -- produces the most honest answer he has given in decades, and he knows it.

He is the party's social lead and its most complex moral problem. He is formidable in any conversation -- equally skilled at telling people what they want to hear and at genuinely winning them over -- but the question the campaign will ask is which of those talents he is actually using at any given moment, and whether the distinction still holds.

## The Relationships

The party contains six pairwise relationships, and the vignettes bring each to life with specificity and restraint.

**Osctra and Gwenwyn** are the party's evidential foundation -- two documenters of the same phenomenon who found each other through data before they found each other through conversation. Their dynamic is quiet, competent, and grounded in mutual recognition: she reads the road; he reads the land; both read what is present in what is not said. The Millbrake supper vignette is their definitive scene -- a spare exchange over stew and bread in which six sentences communicate everything that matters and the rest is detail. Their tension is methodological: she moves with available information; he insists on verification before commitment. She is usually right that they need to act; he is usually right that they need to know more. They will not always resolve this cleanly.

**Osctra and Ix-Nambuth** share a physical worldview -- both assess situations by what is present and what is missing -- and a professional overlap in the same rural territory, where they have almost certainly been at the same events without speaking. The initial friction is real: Osctra's contempt for entitlement and authority can misfire against the kind of presence Ix-Nambuth carries, which is not rank but oath, not title but certainty. He is wrong about this, and the question is what makes him look more carefully. The ridge-road vignette answers it: when he watches her practise the viol with the unsentimental precision of a craftsperson -- not performing but making something real -- he recognises the species of care, even if the expression is alien. She is tending the party the way he tends a field. Different crop; same principle.

**Osctra and Cognodec** are the party's sharpest study in contrast -- the man who is exactly what he appears to be and the man who has spent decades being what others needed him to be. Osctra's instinct for detecting falsehood, calibrated by eighty years of trading with Orcish merchants who know their weights, identifies Cognodec as a performer immediately: the theatrical murmur, the assured manner, the smile that is a product of long practice rather than spontaneous feeling. And yet their deep knowledge of the natural world -- Osctra's agricultural, Cognodec's interpretive -- overlaps in ways that neither expected. The Ashen Wood vignette captures the moment when Cognodec's performance drops and the real concern surfaces, and Osctra revises a small number of his initial estimates. Their relationship will be built on this pattern: Osctra watching for the real thing underneath the construction, and Cognodec slowly discovering how much of the construction has become real.

**Ix-Nambuth and Gwenwyn** are two musicians on the same circuit with entirely different aesthetics -- the viol that produces silence in a room and the shawm that makes people dance -- who have been tracking the same absences from different angles. Their dynamic is professional recognition shading toward something more personal: Gwenwyn's quick, sideways intelligence and Ix-Nambuth's careful, embodied attention produce a relationship that takes time to deepen but has a foundation of shared observation from the start. The crucial thread between them is communicative: Ix-Nambuth assumes that being present communicates care; Gwenwyn reads people well enough to notice when this does not land, and will sometimes supply the words Ix-Nambuth does not know she needs. The crossroads vignette closes with Gwenwyn playing a phrase on the shawm she has never played before -- "a line that wanted a viol under it" -- which is, in miniature, the shape of their emerging relationship.

**Ix-Nambuth and Cognodec** share Gnomish as a private language, and through it a quality of honesty that neither of them quite accesses in Common. Ix-Nambuth learned Gnomish from Zib Tumbletock; Cognodec speaks it as a birthright. Their private exchanges have a different texture -- Ix-Nambuth's Gnomish precise and slightly formal, shaped by Zib's influence; Cognodec's native-born but spoken in a voice trained to be other voices. The clearing vignette is the emotional centre of their relationship: two people who have been claimed by things they cannot name, finding in each other a recognition that does not require further explanation. Their deeper connection is theological -- both received power from something in the Greenwild that they did not choose and cannot fully explain. Whether the oath and the patron are related, whether the same attention is watching both of them, is an open question that will define their shared arc. She is the person in the party most likely to ask him a direct question, and the conversations they will have -- each version slightly more honest than the last -- are one of the campaign's slow-burning narrative engines.

**Gwenwyn and Cognodec** are two social operators, two performance-based personas, with one significant difference: hers is real. Gwenwyn's circuit is genuinely functional -- she passes news, she connects communities, the music is real. Cognodec's was, for decades, a constructed service backed by excellent illusions. Gwenwyn sees through the construction with the accuracy of a professional assessing another professional, but she also sees the moments when it stops being a performance -- when the Book is heavier than it should be, when a genuine answer arrives and surprises even the man giving it. Together they are the party's social engine, better in tandem than alone: Cognodec leads, Gwenwyn reads the room and adjusts, and they have developed an informal shorthand that operates at a speed the others do not always follow. The question Gwenwyn will eventually ask him -- not whether he is a real mystic, but what story he is telling himself now -- has already been posed on the lane outside Millbrake, and the answer will be the most honest thing he has said in three decades.

## The Narrative So Far

The campaign setup describes a convergence that occurred over approximately six weeks, through the accumulation of shared evidence and the quiet gravity of a problem too large for any one of them to face alone.

Osctra came down from Hawthorn Valley for the last time with his pitchfork, his pickling-barrel shield, his field journals, and nothing else worth carrying. His farm was in ruin. The bounty was modest but real. He went south toward the Thornvael road because the road went somewhere and the valley no longer did.

Gwenwyn found him. She had been tracking the same wrongness from the road -- displaced predators, frightened wildlife, the land speaking in a register she could not parse -- and when she heard about a Wood Elf farmer with a bounty and a field journal full of observations, she adjusted her circuit. She caught up with him at Briar's Crossing and offered not company but a comparison of notes. His field journal and her road observations, laid out on a table, described the same phenomenon from two angles. He did not invite her to travel with him. She traveled in the same direction, at the same pace, and he did not object. This was as close to an invitation as Osctra has ever produced.

Ix-Nambuth had been circling the same question from the cultural angle: three villages had cancelled their seasonal gatherings in a month, and the particular quality of fear that kept people indoors was erasing the things the oath had charged her to protect. She was taking this personally. She found them at the Greenwild Edge market -- half-shuttered, the traders nervous, the festival atmosphere replaced by watchful quiet. She arrived with her viol case and her quarterstaff and the particular kind of presence that means people notice her and are not sure what to do about it. Osctra looked at her, looked at the quarterstaff, looked at the viol case, and said nothing. Gwenwyn said something sideways that was probably an introduction. Ix-Nambuth sat down.

Cognodec arrived last, and he will tell you this was not by design, though Gwenwyn suspects otherwise. He had been working the Thornvael circuit as usual, performing his consultations, and finding that the performance was becoming increasingly difficult to maintain -- not because his skills had diminished, but because the animals he spoke with were genuinely afraid, and the Book was genuinely heavier, and the things people needed explained were no longer addressable with illusions and reassuring words. He appeared at the Greenwild Edge market on the second morning with the precise theatrical assurance of decades of practice, and offered his expertise. Osctra looked at him with eighty years of calibrated instinct for detecting falsehood. Gwenwyn saw both the presentation and the thing behind it. Ix-Nambuth listened to his Gnomish and filed what she noticed without comment.

It was Gwenwyn who suggested, on the second evening, that they pool their observations properly. Osctra laid out his field journal. Gwenwyn contributed her road notes. Ix-Nambuth described the cancelled fairs and the geography of absence. Cognodec reported what the animals had told him, without theatrical embellishment, and the room went quiet in a way that had nothing to do with performance. The picture was clear, if incomplete: something was wrong in the deep places of the Greenwild, and it was spreading outward, and it had been doing so for longer than any of them had realised.

They are heading south now, along the Thornvael road, toward the edge of the Greenwild. They have not discussed how long they will travel together. They have not needed to.

## The Open Threads

The campaign is designed around eight dormant story threads, each tied to specific characters or to the shared mystery at the centre:

1. **The Wrongness.** What is driving the Greenwild's predators outward from the deep places? What has been wrong in the interior, and for how long? Osctra's journals suggest months; the animals Cognodec speaks to suggest longer. This is the party's central question, and every character's arc feeds into it.
2. **The Green Advocate.** Who or what is Cognodec's patron? Is the name genuine? What does it want beyond what it has already given? Why did it choose a charlatan -- and does the answer to that question change now that the charlatan is becoming the thing he was pretending to be?
3. **The Oath's Source.** What claimed Ix-Nambuth in the clearing? Is it connected to the Green Advocate? Is it connected to the Wrongness? What does it expect beyond what she has already given? The convergence of her oath and his patron -- both responsive to the same phenomenon, both originating in the Greenwild -- is either coincidence or design.
4. **Zib Tumbletock and Mosshallow.** Zib lives near Mosshallow, the Forest Gnome community where Cognodec was born. Neither Ix-Nambuth nor Cognodec has yet made the geographic connection. When they do, it will raise questions about what the edge-wood communities know, whether Mosshallow has been affected, and whether Cognodec's family is safe. He has not thought about this in some time. He will need to.
5. **Lord Torth and the Bounty.** The bounty is a legal nuisance; Lord Torth is a political problem. The hunting party's disruption of Hawthorn Valley -- flushing a displaced predator through settled land -- suggests either ignorance of the Wrongness or indifference to it. Either is relevant.
6. **Osctra's Scattered Herd.** Decades of cultivated livestock, dispersed across four valleys. They are still out there. He will want them back.
7. **Edda.** Gwenwyn's herbalist mentor, buried in a birch copse on the eastern road. There is no active thread here -- only a gap with a shape that other things could fill. Gwenwyn still starts sentences addressed to her.
8. **Cognodec's Family.** Seven siblings in Mosshallow. He has not been home in decades. The edge-wood communities have been unusually quiet.

## What Holds It Together

Three foundations make this group functional despite their differences in method and temperament:

**A shared subject matter.** All four are experts, from different angles, in the same territory: rural communities, woodland ecosystems, the rhythms of seasonal life. When they compare notes, they find they have been observing the same wrongness for months from different vantage points. This shared evidence creates a foundation stronger than personal affection -- the trust of people who have independently verified the same fact.

**Respect for demonstrated competence.** Not one of them arrived at their expertise through institutional channels. Osctra grew his across eight decades of observation. Ix-Nambuth was trained by a demanding Gnomish craftsman. Gwenwyn learned from a wandering herbalist and two years of hard road. Cognodec's expertise was initially constructed but became real through decades of practice. None of them mistake credentials for capability, and none of them dismiss capability because it arrived through unorthodox routes.

**A shared moral compass.** They agree, without needing to discuss it, that the rules of the road are advisory and the protection of living things is not. None of them will watch a community go dark without trying to prevent it. None of them will accept a lord's authority as a reason to stop doing what needs to be done. Their disagreements are about method, not conclusion.

What makes this party distinctive -- and what separates it from Party 1 -- is the nature of what is missing. There is no one inclined toward order and procedure to stop and ask whether a course of action is actually right, no transactional calculator to notice when the party cannot afford the next right thing. They have no internal compass that challenges their more impulsive instincts and no one whose role is to count the cost. They will need to develop these functions collectively, or discover their absence at the worst possible moment. They are a party that agrees on ends, and a party that agrees on ends can do things a more fractious group would have argued itself out of -- and not all of those things will be wise.

But what they have, and what the vignettes make vivid, is a quality of convergence that is almost gravitational. Four people who were parallel lines on the same circuit for years, occupying the same spaces without inhabiting the same world, drawn together not by contract or convenience but by a shared recognition that the land they know is breaking and that the breaking requires more than any one of them can provide. They are not yet friends. They are something that may turn out to be more immediate than friendship: people who have seen the same thing, from different angles, and decided to walk toward it together.
