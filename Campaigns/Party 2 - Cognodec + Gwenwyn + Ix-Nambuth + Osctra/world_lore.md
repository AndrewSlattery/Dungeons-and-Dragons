# World Lore & Setting

---

## Overview

A high-fantasy world experienced at ground level, from the inside of a forest that is going wrong. The great kingdoms exist; the archmages are out there somewhere, doing whatever archmages do; dragons are presumably a going concern. None of this is the party's immediate problem. The party lives in the working margins of that world — rural circuits, forest roads, seasonal markets, the company of people who grow things, play things, and move things between communities that the grand stories have never heard of.

The tone is low-fantasy in focus, not in fact. The Greenwild is genuinely ancient and genuinely strange. It is also, on a normal day, a forest where people farm and hunt and play music at harvest suppers. The extraordinary is there, underneath. It is surfacing.

---

## The Thornvael — The Region

The campaign begins in the Thornvael: a stretch of settled country running roughly north-south along the Thornvael road, with the Greenwild — a vast, old-growth forest — to the west and the upland valleys to the east. This is agricultural and frontier territory — old enough to have established communities, remote enough to be self-reliant, unglamorous enough to be governed mostly by custom and seasonal necessity rather than by the institutions of any distant crown.

The landscape: mixed deciduous forest, deep and old to the west, thinning into farmland and grazing country in the central lowlands. The eastern uplands rise into rocky hill country, with narrow valleys cut by streams that feed the Thornvael river. The soil in the lowlands is good; the soil in the valleys is better, if you know what you are doing. The sky is wide in the lowlands and narrow in the valleys. The weather comes from the west, from the Greenwild, carrying the particular humidity of deep forest and the smell of leaf-rot and growing things.

Communities along the Thornvael are connected by the road, by seasonal markets, and by a shared practical culture: cautious hospitality, strong opinions about fair dealing, and a deep familiarity with the rhythms of the forest and the land. They are not naive about the wider world's dangers — they maintain their walls and their watches — but their day-to-day concerns are planting seasons, livestock prices, and whether the road south is passable after rain.

## Key Settlements

### Kettleford

The largest settlement on the Thornvael road — large enough to have a proper market, a constable, two inns, a smithy, and the kind of mixed-species population that a regional trading hub generates. Kettleford sits where the Thornvael road crosses the river of the same name, and its bridge is the only reliable crossing for twenty miles in either direction. This makes it a chokepoint, a market, and a social centre simultaneously.

The economy runs on the grain harvest from the surrounding farms, livestock from the eastern valleys, and the twice-yearly trade with the Orcish merchants who come up from the south with goods and opinions. Kettleford is prosperous enough to have a cobbler and a brewer but not a proper guild — craft is organised by custom and mutual suspicion rather than charter. The constable, **Aldra Venn**, is a broad-shouldered human woman who has held the post for eleven years and considers paperwork a necessary affliction. She posts contract notices on a board outside the constabulary and expects the work done without unnecessary drama.

**Key locations:**

- **The Greenman Inn.** The larger of the two inns, near the market square. Rooms upstairs, common room below. Travelers, merchants, and working professionals end up here. The ale is local and the food is honest and the fire is the social centre of the town after dark.
- **The Sett.** The smaller inn, down near the river. Cheaper, quieter, preferred by the farming communities and the seasonal workers. The kind of place where local knowledge circulates in its natural habitat.
- **The Kettleford Market.** Open most mornings, larger on the first and third weeks of each month. The twice-yearly Orcish trade days are the major commercial events of the season.
- **The Constabulary.** A stone building near the bridge. Contract board by the door. Aldra Venn's office is inside, along with two holding cells that are mostly used for storing confiscated livestock.

**Atmosphere:** Woodsmoke and river-water. Carts on the bridge at dawn. The particular sound of a community that knows its own business and conducts it at a practical volume. Strangers are assessed quickly, welcomed if they have something to contribute, and watched regardless.

### Briar's Crossing

A crossroads settlement where the Thornvael road meets the track east to Ashgrove and the foothill communities. Smaller than Kettleford, rougher, with the transient character of a place that exists because two roads meet. One inn — **The Briar** — that serves food, drink, and beds of acceptable quality, and which is the default meeting point for anyone traveling between the lowlands and the eastern hills.

Briar's Crossing has no constable. Disputes are settled by **Hollen Marsh**, the innkeeper, whose authority derives from the fact that he is the largest person in the settlement and has been settling disputes for thirty years. He is fair, laconic, and deeply uninterested in explanations longer than two sentences.

This is where Gwenwyn found Osctra. This is where the Thornvael road begins to feel like frontier rather than farmland.

### Greenwild Edge

Not a settlement so much as a seasonal gathering point — a cleared area at the western edge of the lowland farms where the Thornvael meets the Greenwild. Three times a year (spring planting, midsummer, and autumn harvest), a market assembles here: lowland farmers, foothill herders, Orcish traders from the south, and — in better times — Forest Gnome communities from the edge-wood bringing crafts and foraged goods.

Greenwild Edge has no permanent population. What it has is a tradition, a cleared field with stone fire-circles, and a social function that makes it more important than its physical presence suggests. The seasonal markets are where the Thornvael's communities come together across their usual divisions. When those markets are disrupted, the region feels it.

The most recent market was half-shuttered. Fewer traders, fewer visitors, the festival atmosphere replaced by watchful quiet. This is where the party came together.

### Ashgrove

A borderland settlement in the eastern foothills, at the head of a valley where the hill country begins in earnest. Ashgrove is mixed in the way that frontier places are mixed: Orcs, humans, a few Elven families, some Halfling traders who found a niche and stayed. Nobody quite belongs to anything, which means everyone has had to decide for themselves what they are. The settlement has a blacksmith, a healer's cottage, and a community hall that serves as tavern, meeting-place, and — when the weather turns — emergency shelter.

Ix-Nambuth grew up here. Her people — insofar as she claims any — are the borderland Orcs who came to the foothills a generation ago from somewhere further east, settled because the land was available, and stayed because the valley grew on them. Ashgrove does not have a strong opinion about Orcs or about anyone else. It has a strong opinion about whether you can do useful work, and it applies this standard uniformly.

### Mosshallow

A Forest Gnome community in the edge-wood between the Thornvael lowlands and the deep Greenwild. Mosshallow is the kind of settlement that shifts slightly with the seasons, appears in winter differently than in summer, and which most outsiders pass without noticing. It is old — older than Kettleford, older than the Thornvael road — and its relationship with the Greenwild is intimate in ways that the lowland communities do not share.

Cognodec was born here. Zib Tumbletock lives nearby. The edge-wood communities are the first line of contact between the settled lowlands and whatever is happening in the deep forest, and they have been unusually quiet in recent months.

### Hawthorn Valley

A narrow upland valley east of the Greenwild, accessible by a track from the Thornvael road. The soil is exceptional — a particular combination of mineral content and drainage that Osctra spent decades optimising for his botanical research. The valley is remote enough to have been largely ignored by the regional authorities and prosperous enough, under Osctra's management, to have been self-sufficient.

It is now in ruins. The greenhouse is smashed. The botanical beds are trampled. The irrigation system that took twenty years to build is damaged beyond quick repair. Lord Torth's hunting party and the displaced predator they flushed through the property between them destroyed what six decades of patient work had built.

Hawthorn Valley is not accessible to Osctra at present — the bounty makes returning inadvisable, and there is nothing to return to. It is background: a wound that is real and that matters, a reason rather than a destination.

## Species & Communities

### Forest Gnomes

The Gnomish communities of the edge-wood — Mosshallow and its neighbours — are the oldest settled population in the Thornvael region, predating the human and Orcish communities by centuries. They are small, insular by preference, and deeply embedded in the forest's ecology in ways that the lowland communities do not fully understand. Their relationship with the Greenwild is not merely residential; it is participatory. They notice when the forest changes, and they are noticed in return.

Cognodec left Mosshallow decades ago and has not returned. The edge-wood communities have their own knowledge of the wrongness, and it is likely more detailed — and more alarming — than what the lowland settlements have observed.

### Orcs

The Orcish presence in the Thornvael region is commercial and settled. Orcish merchants come up from the southern lowlands for the twice-yearly trade at Kettleford and the seasonal exchange at Greenwild Edge, bringing goods, opinions, and an exacting standard of quality that the local farmers have learned to respect. The borderland communities — Ashgrove and its neighbours — include Orcish families who settled a generation ago and are now part of the landscape.

Osctra speaks Orcish because it has more precise words for agricultural concepts than Elvish does. Ix-Nambuth speaks it as a heritage tongue. The Orcish merchants at Kettleford will know Osctra by reputation — the silent Elf farmer with the extraordinary root tubers — and his absence from the market will be noted.

### Wood Elves

The Wood Elf communities of the region are scattered and solitary — individual families or small clusters rather than organised settlements, distributed through the upland valleys and the Greenwild margins. Osctra is typical of this pattern: a single individual in a remote valley, connected to the wider world through twice-yearly trading rather than continuous social contact. Wood Elves in the Thornvael are patient, territorial, and profoundly connected to the land they inhabit. When the land goes wrong, they feel it in their work before they understand it in their thinking.

### Humans

The majority population of the lowland settlements: Kettleford, Briar's Crossing, and the farming communities along the Thornvael road. Gwenwyn is human, as are most of the people she plays for, passes news between, and sleeps under the same roofs with. Humans in the Thornvael are farmers, traders, innkeepers, and constables — the practical infrastructure of a working region.

## The Wrongness

Something is wrong in the Greenwild, and it is spreading outward.

From ground level, this is what it looks like: predators appearing outside their normal ranges, frightened and disoriented. Prey animals moving in the wrong directions. Seasonal patterns disrupted — bird migrations off schedule, denning animals emerging too early or too late. The forest's ecology is not collapsing; it is being displaced. Something in the deep places is pushing the inhabitants outward, and the wave of displacement has been building for months.

Osctra has the most systematic documentation: his field journals record species, locations, dates, and behavioural observations with the meticulous precision of a man who has spent eighty years reading the land. His data suggests the displacement originates somewhere in the deep Greenwild and has been intensifying gradually since at least six months ago.

Gwenwyn has the road perspective: which villages have reported attacks, which roads have become unsafe, where the fear has reached. Her mental map of the region's disruption patterns is comprehensive and current.

Ix-Nambuth has the cultural perspective: the cancelled fairs, the empty village greens, the communities pulling inward. She tracks the wrongness by its social footprint — the things that should be present and are not.

Cognodec has the animals' testimony: fear without object, displacement without destination, and the consistent report that something is wrong in the deep places and has been wrong for longer than six weeks. The animals cannot say what they are running from. They know only that it is there.

The wrongness is not yet understood. It is felt, documented, tracked, and feared. Whatever is causing it has not been seen by anyone the party has spoken to. It is a presence known only by its effects — like a stone dropped in water, visible only in the expanding rings.

## Religion & the Divine

The gods of this world are real and present, though not uniformly attentive. In the Thornvael, divine practice is local, practical, and informal. The Kettleford chapel serves all comers; roadside shrines mark safe passages and dangerous crossings; farmers bless their fields at planting with whatever divine name their family has always used. This is not a region of cathedrals and organised theology. It is a region of small observances and working faith.

Ix-Nambuth's oath did not come from a temple. She has never been inside a temple she found convincing. What claimed her in that clearing was something older and less institutional — a force or intelligence associated with the Greenwild itself, ancient and unhurried, that looked at her and decided she was adequate to a purpose it did not explain. Her divine power is real. Its theology is her own to work out.

Cognodec's patron, the Green Advocate, occupies a different category entirely. It is Fey — or presents as Fey — and its relationship with Cognodec is less a covenant than an ongoing situation. The name may be an alias. The power is genuine. What the patron wants, beyond what it has already given, is not yet clear. Whether it is connected to whatever claimed Ix-Nambuth — whether these are aspects of the same attention, or separate forces with overlapping interests — is an open question that the party does not yet have the information to answer.

The arcane and the divine overlap in the Greenwild in ways that are not fully mapped. The Forest Gnome communities have their own understanding of this overlap, and it is older and more nuanced than anything the lowland settlements possess.

## The Wider World

The campaign does not begin with the wider world pressing in. It is there — distant, enormous, doing what it does — and it will become relevant when the local threads connect to larger ones.

What circulates in the common rooms of Kettleford and the market chatter at Greenwild Edge: a lord's hunting party that went badly wrong in the uplands (Osctra's story, told with varying accuracy); rumours of a blight on grain stores in two of the southern market towns, cause unclear; a trading company that has been buying forest timber rights aggressively and quietly; Gnomish communities in the edge-wood going silent in ways they have not gone silent before; something seen above the tree canopy of the deep Greenwild on clear nights — not a dragon, probably, but large and not previously noted.

These are the things people mention over ale and then change the subject about. Some of them are nothing. Some of them are threads.

## Factions & Forces

*(To be developed during play. Placeholders for threads already in motion.)*

- **Lord Vaylen Torth.** A landed lord of the eastern uplands with an estate, a retinue, and a grievance against a specific Wood Elf farmer. His reach is regional but real. His interest in the Greenwild is recreational rather than ecological, which makes him a nuisance and potentially an obstacle.
- **The Kettleford Constabulary.** Aldra Venn and her small operation. The party's most likely initial point of contact with regional authority. Pragmatic, competent, and increasingly aware that something is wrong without having the resources to investigate it.
- **The Edge-Wood Communities.** Mosshallow and the neighbouring Gnomish settlements. Silent. Possibly affected by the wrongness. Possibly informed about it. Not currently accessible without going into the Greenwild margin.
- **The Orcish Merchants.** The twice-yearly trading partners at Kettleford and Greenwild Edge. A commercial network with its own information channels, its own observations about the region's disruption, and its own interests in the Thornvael's stability.
- **Whatever Is in the Deep Greenwild.** Unknown. Present. Getting closer.
