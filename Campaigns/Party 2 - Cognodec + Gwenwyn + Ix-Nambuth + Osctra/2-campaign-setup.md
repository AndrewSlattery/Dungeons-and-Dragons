# Campaign Setup — Session Zero Document

*How the party came to exist; where each character was before it; and what brought them together on the Thornvael road with the forest going wrong around them.*

---

## Part One: Before the Party

### Cognodec — The Mystic Who Became Real

Cognodec was born in **Mosshallow**, a Forest Gnome community in the edge-wood between the Thornvael and the Greenwild — the sort of settlement that shifts slightly with the seasons and which most outsiders walk past without registering. He was the fourth of seven children, none of whom were particularly magical. What Cognodec had, from early childhood, was attention: a talent for reading what people expected to see, and for providing it.

He left Mosshallow at forty-three — young for a Gnome — with Minor Illusion, a borrowed coat, and a working hypothesis. The human and mixed-species settlements of the Thornvael lowlands were, he had observed, underserved by genuine Forest Mystics. They were not, however, underserved by people who wanted to believe they had spoken to one. He spent the following decades filling this gap: consultations with forest spirits, blessing ceremonies for new fields, guided encounters with nature's wisdom involving excellent illusions and well-timed sound effects. His Forgery Kit produced testimonials from Forest Councils that may or may not have existed. His Speak with Animals — the real gift, the Gnomish birthright — he kept out of the performance entirely, unwilling to cheapen it.

He was good at this. He was consistent, careful, and almost never wrong about what people needed to hear. He worked the circuit of small towns along the Thornvael road: **Millhaven**, **Briar's Crossing**, **Kettleford**, and the seasonal market at **Greenwild Edge**, where the lowland farmers come to trade with the forest communities. He was known — not famous, but present — and trusted in the way that traveling specialists are trusted: with limited information and considerable goodwill.

The patron arrived approximately one year ago. Or, more precisely, made itself apparent. He woke one morning to find his working notebook replaced by a bound thing that felt different in each hand, that opened to different pages depending on his mood, that contained cantrips he had not learned and rituals written in ink he had not supplied. A voice in a dream gave a name he suspects is an alias. He has since referred to the patron as *the Green Advocate* in his notes and performances, and has begun to wonder whether naming a thing gives it a reality it did not previously have.

Six weeks ago, the animals began speaking differently. Not in vocabulary — animals have limited vocabulary and heavy concerns — but in tenor. Fear without object. Displacement without destination. The Book seems heavier lately. The patron has not commented.

### Gwenwyn — The Road's Own Instrument

Gwenwyn was born on a cart. She says this literally, though she does not mean the farm cart her mother happened to be riding was going anywhere interesting. She does not remember her parents with much clarity. What she remembers is city streets — **Kettleford**'s lower market, the back alleys of **Briar's Crossing** — learning which doors to knock on and which to read from a distance.

She fell in with traveling performers young, drifting between the lowland settlements in the company of people who needed another pair of hands and did not ask difficult questions about where she had come from. Then she found the old herbalist — or, more precisely, the old herbalist found her, somewhere on the eastern road near **Dunmore**, and changed the shape of things. The woman — Gwenwyn called her **Edda**, though she suspects this was not the name she was born with — spoke to plants when she harvested them, apologised to animals she had to put down, and maintained that the world had a great deal to say if you would stop making noise long enough to hear it. Gwenwyn absorbed this selectively. She speaks to animals constantly and plays the shawm at a volume that would embarrass a battlefield. But something from those years took root.

Edda died two winters ago, somewhere on the eastern road between Dunmore and the Thornvael. Gwenwyn buried her in a copse of birch trees off the road and sat there for three days, and then she got up and went back to work, because that was what Edda would have done.

For the past two years, Gwenwyn has been working a circuit of rural settlements — **Kettleford**, **Millhaven**, **Greenwild Edge**, the farming hamlets along the Thornvael road, the winter gatherings at **Briar's Crossing**. She plays music people can dance to, passes news from village to village, and has accumulated a comprehensive mental map of which roads are safe at which seasons, which innkeepers are worth the coin, and which estates are best given a wide berth. She checks sleeping animals. She keeps notes. The old herbalist would have wanted her to keep notes.

Three months ago, she noticed the wildlife moving wrong. Prey heading the wrong direction. Predators appearing far outside their usual ranges. She noted it and kept moving. It did not resolve.

### Ix-Nambuth — The Viol and the Oath

Ix-Nambuth grew up in **Ashgrove**, a borderland settlement in the foothills east of the Thornvael — mixed enough that nobody quite belonged to anything, which meant everyone had to decide for themselves what they were. She decided early that she was not going to be what people assumed when they looked at an Orc of her size, and she has been refining that decision ever since.

She found performance because music crossed borders that her appearance did not. She was seventeen when she first played for coin — a drum, at a market in **Briar's Crossing**, badly — and something in the response of the crowd told her this was worth pursuing with more seriousness than she had applied to anything else. She pursued it with considerable seriousness.

**Zib Tumbletock** found her four years later, in the common room of a roadside inn between Ashgrove and Kettleford. Zib was an elderly Gnomish musician with exacting opinions about technique, a second-hand viol, and the conviction that beauty was a serious undertaking and the world required more of it, not less. She corrected Ix-Nambuth's technique with the patience of someone who had been doing this for longer than most of her students had been alive, and introduced her to the idea that music was not a tool for crossing borders but a thing worth doing for its own sake. Ix-Nambuth absorbed this completely. Zib is the reason she speaks Gnomish, the reason she plays the way she does, and one of approximately three reasons she is not a completely different person. Zib lives, she believes, in a cottage somewhere near **Mosshallow**, in the edge-wood. She tries not to worry about this with any regularity.

The oath came to her three years ago in a clearing in the Greenwild, on a night when she had stopped to sleep between villages. She woke to find something watching her — not a creature, exactly. More like a quality of attention. Ancient, unhurried, and entirely certain about what it was doing. She did not speak any vow she can recall. She woke the next morning understanding one had been made. She has never fully resolved this theologically, but the power is real, and the weight of what it asks is real, and on balance she has decided that whatever claimed her had reasonable taste.

She has been working a rural circuit of festivals and seasonal gatherings for the past several years — **Kettleford**, **Millhaven**, **Greenwild Edge**, the harvest suppers in the farming hamlets, the midwinter fires in the foothill communities near Ashgrove. She is known in these places. Not always welcomed warmly at first, but always welcomed back. When she arrived at a village on the expected date to find the green empty — no stalls, no fire, no music — she began paying attention in a different way.

### Osctra — The Farmer with the Pitchfork

Osctra was born into a small Wood Elf community at the head of **Hawthorn Valley**, a remote frontier valley in the uplands east of the Greenwild. Unlike most of his kin who eventually drifted toward the ancient forests or the wider world, Osctra stayed — not from inability to leave, but because the soil was exceptional and there was work to do.

Over approximately six decades, he transformed a modest plot into something remarkable: a living laboratory of botanical cultivation, centred on a magically-enhanced strain of Orcish root tubers — a variety known among those who knew it at all for extraordinary yield, remarkable shelf life, and maddening sensitivity to growing conditions. It demanded constant attention. Osctra gave it constant attention. The arrangement suited both parties.

Decades of twice-yearly trading at the valley's foot brought him into regular contact with the Orcish merchants who came up from the lowland market at **Kettleford** and the seasonal exchange at **Greenwild Edge**. Their exacting quality standards and their even more exacting opinions about soil composition he found oddly agreeable. He learned Orcish out of professional necessity and kept speaking it because Orcish, in his considered view, has far more precise words for agricultural concepts than Elvish does. He was never social, exactly. But he was known: as a fair dealer, a patient neighbour, and someone who descended from Hawthorn Valley twice a year with a cartload of produce and expected to be left alone the rest of the time.

For months, something had been wrong with the valley's wildlife. Predators in the wrong ranges, frightened and disoriented. Osctra had been documenting it in his field journal with growing precision, chalking individual incidents up to seasonal disruption, telling himself it would settle. It did not settle.

A few weeks ago, a local lord — **Lord Vaylen Torth** of the Torth estate, a man of inherited land, inherited money, and inherited confidence in the primacy of his own amusement — rode into Hawthorn Valley with a retinue of armoured knights and decided it was an ideal hunting ground. They flushed a displaced predator — something large, frightened, and well outside its natural territory — directly through Osctra's property. The beast demolished his greenhouse. The knights demolished his fences. Their horses demolished two hundred years of carefully maintained botanical beds.

When Osctra requested compensation, the lord laughed. He referred to him, specifically, as a "dirt-grubbing peasant." Whatever remained of Osctra's Elven etiquette evaporated on the spot. He went very, very quiet, picked up his pitchfork, and systematically dismantled the entire hunting party. He did not kill anyone. He did not need to. He threw the lord bodily over his own ruined fence and explained, in a voice of absolute calm, exactly how many individual plants had been destroyed and what he expected by way of restitution.

Lord Torth filed a complaint from his sickbed. There is now a modest bounty on Osctra's head.

## Part Two: How They Came Together

Osctra came down from Hawthorn Valley for the last time with his pitchfork, his pickling-barrel shield, his field journals, and nothing else worth carrying. His farm was in ruin. His prize herd was scattered across four valleys. The bounty was modest but real, and staying in the valley while Lord Torth's men came looking for him was not a plan so much as a way of inviting further violence. He went south, toward the Thornvael road, because the road went somewhere and the valley no longer did.

Gwenwyn found him. She is good at finding people who do not want to be found, and she is good at being present when they grudgingly admit they could use someone. She had been tracking the same wrongness he had been documenting — the displaced predators, the frightened wildlife, the land speaking in a register she could not quite parse — and when she heard about a Wood Elf farmer with a bounty on his head and a field journal full of observations, she adjusted her circuit.

She caught up with him at **Briar's Crossing**, at the inn where the Thornvael road meets the track south to Ashgrove. He was not looking for company. She did not offer company; she offered a comparison of notes. His field journal and her road observations, laid out on a table in the common room, described the same phenomenon from two angles: something was pushing the forest's predators outward from the deep places, and it had been doing so for months. The evidence was sufficient. The conversation was brief. He did not invite her to travel with him. She traveled in the same direction, at the same pace, and he did not object. This was as close to an invitation as Osctra has ever produced.

Ix-Nambuth had been circling the same question from her own angle. Three villages on her circuit had cancelled their seasonal gatherings in the space of a month. The reasons were always the same: animal attacks, unsafe roads, the particular quality of fear that means people are staying indoors. She had heard, through the circuit, about the farmer and the woman with the shawm who were heading south together, and about the farmer's journals. She considered this worth investigating.

She found them at the **Greenwild Edge** market — or what remained of it. The market was half-shuttered, the traders nervous, the usual festival atmosphere replaced by the watchful quiet of a community that had pulled in on itself. Ix-Nambuth arrived with her viol case and her quarterstaff and the particular kind of presence that means people notice her and then are not sure what to do about it. Osctra looked at her, looked at the quarterstaff, looked at the viol case, and said nothing. Gwenwyn said something sideways that was probably an introduction. Ix-Nambuth sat down.

Cognodec arrived last, and he will tell you this was not by design, though Gwenwyn suspects otherwise. He had been working the Thornvael circuit as usual, performing his consultations, and finding that the performance was becoming increasingly difficult to maintain — not because his skills had diminished, but because the animals he spoke with were genuinely afraid, and the Book at his side was genuinely heavier, and the things people were asking him to explain were no longer things he could address with well-timed illusions and reassuring words. He needed real answers. He had heard, through the circuit, about people who were collecting them.

He appeared at the Greenwild Edge market on the second morning, introduced himself with the precise theatrical assurance of someone who has been introducing himself to strangers for decades, and offered his expertise. Osctra looked at him with the particular attention of someone whose bullshit-detection has been calibrated by eighty years of trading with Orcish merchants. Gwenwyn watched the performance and saw both what was being presented and what was actually there. Ix-Nambuth listened to his Gnomish — too fluent in some registers, oddly stilted in others — and filed what she noticed without comment.

They did not form a party in any formal sense. They formed a proximity: four people heading in the same direction, looking at the same problem, each contributing a different angle. It was Gwenwyn who suggested, on the second evening, that they pool their observations properly. Osctra laid out his field journal. Gwenwyn contributed her road notes. Ix-Nambuth described the cancelled fairs and the geography of absence. Cognodec reported what the animals had told him, without theatrical embellishment, and the room went quiet in a way that had nothing to do with performance.

The picture that emerged was clear, if incomplete: something was wrong in the deep places of the Greenwild, and it was spreading outward, and it had been doing so for longer than any of them had realised.

They are heading south now, along the Thornvael road, toward the edge of the Greenwild. They have not discussed how long they will travel together. They have not needed to.

## Part Three: Specific Questions Resolved

### The Zib Tumbletock / Mosshallow Connection

Zib Tumbletock, the Gnomish musician who trained Ix-Nambuth, lives near Mosshallow — the same Forest Gnome community where Cognodec was born. Whether they know each other is not confirmed, but Mosshallow is small and Gnomish communities are close-knit. There is a reasonable probability that Zib knew Cognodec's family, or at least knew of them, and that Cognodec — if he returned home, which he has not — would find Zib's name familiar.

This is a thread, not a revelation. Neither Ix-Nambuth nor Cognodec has yet made the geographic connection. When they do, it will produce questions about what Zib knows of the Greenwild's condition, whether Mosshallow has been affected by the wrongness, and whether the edge-wood communities have seen what the lowland settlements have seen. Cognodec has family in Mosshallow. He has not thought about this in some time. He will need to.

### The Oath and the Patron

Ix-Nambuth's oath came to her in a clearing in the Greenwild. Cognodec's patron — the Green Advocate — is a Fey entity connected to the forest. These may be the same source, different aspects of the same source, or entirely unrelated. Neither character has shared enough detail with the other for the connection to surface yet. When it does, it will raise the question of what, exactly, is paying attention in the Greenwild, and what it wants from both of them.

The Book of Shadows grows heavier when something is wrong. The oath charged Ix-Nambuth to protect the things that are going dark. Both are responsive to the same phenomenon. This is either coincidence or design, and neither of them is naive enough to assume the former without evidence.

### The Shared Circuit

Cognodec, Gwenwyn, and Ix-Nambuth have all been working the same rural circuit of Thornvael settlements for years. They have almost certainly been at the same events — harvest suppers, seasonal fairs, midwinter gatherings — without forming a relationship. This is realistic: itinerant performers and specialists in rural areas occupy overlapping territory without necessarily connecting, especially when their roles are complementary rather than competitive. Ix-Nambuth plays things that produce silence; Gwenwyn plays things people dance to; Cognodec performs consultations that happen in private. They have been parallel lines. The wrongness is what made them converge.

Osctra, descending from Hawthorn Valley twice a year to trade, would have been present at the Kettleford market and the Greenwild Edge seasonal exchange. He would remember Ix-Nambuth — an Orc with a viol is not something you forget — without having spoken to her. She would remember him as the enormous silent Elf with the cartload of root tubers. They have occupied the same spaces without inhabiting the same world.

### Party Finances

- **Osctra (18 GP):** What he had in his belt when he left Hawthorn Valley. His real wealth was in his farm, his herd, and his botanical library, all of which are destroyed, scattered, or inaccessible. He has a pitchfork, a shield, and strong opinions about compensation.
- **Gwenwyn (25 GP):** Road earnings from her circuit. Modest, consistent, carefully managed. She knows the cost of every inn on the Thornvael road and has opinions about which ones overcharge.
- **Ix-Nambuth (22 GP):** Performance fees from the rural circuit. Not destitute, not comfortable. She has been spending less since the fairs started cancelling.
- **Cognodec (30 GP):** Consultation fees, supplemented by the occasional Charlatan's creative accounting. The largest liquid holdings in the party, which tells you something about the economics of performed mysticism versus genuine skill.

No one is destitute. No one is comfortable. This is the correct financial pressure for the start of an adventure.

### The Bounty on Osctra

Lord Vaylen Torth's bounty is modest — enough to motivate an opportunistic constable, not enough to attract a professional bounty hunter. It is a legal nuisance rather than a mortal threat, but it means Osctra cannot return to Hawthorn Valley without resolving it, and it means he is technically a fugitive in whatever jurisdiction Lord Torth's writ runs in. The party is aware of this. Gwenwyn considers it a minor complication. Ix-Nambuth considers the lord's behaviour an offence against the things she is sworn to protect. Cognodec has already begun constructing a narrative in which Osctra is the wronged party, which has the advantage of being true.

## Part Four: Open Threads

*Things that exist but have not yet been pulled on. For development during play.*

- **The Wrongness.** What is driving the forest's predators outward from the deep places? What is wrong in the Greenwild's interior, and how long has it been wrong? Osctra's journals suggest months; the animals Cognodec speaks to suggest longer.
- **The Green Advocate.** Who or what is Cognodec's patron? Is the name genuine? What does it want, beyond what it has already given? Why did it choose a charlatan?
- **The Oath's Source.** What claimed Ix-Nambuth in the clearing? Is it connected to the Green Advocate? Is it connected to the wrongness? What does it expect beyond what she has already given?
- **Zib Tumbletock and Mosshallow.** What does the edge-wood look like now? Has the wrongness reached the Gnomish communities? Is Zib still alive and well?
- **Lord Torth and the Bounty.** The bounty is a legal problem. Lord Torth is a political problem. The hunting party's disruption of Hawthorn Valley — flushing a displaced predator through settled land — suggests either ignorance of the wrongness or indifference to it. Either is relevant.
- **Osctra's Scattered Herd.** Decades of cultivated livestock, dispersed across four valleys. They are still out there. He will want them back.
- **Edda.** Gwenwyn's herbalist mentor died two winters ago on the eastern road. There is no thread here yet — only a buried woman in a birch copse — but Gwenwyn still starts sentences addressed to her, and the gap she left has a shape that other things could fill.
- **Cognodec's Family.** Seven siblings in Mosshallow. He has not been home in decades. He has not thought about this. He will need to.
