# World Lore & Setting

---

## Overview

A classic high-fantasy world seen from ground level. The great kingdoms exist; archmages walk the earth; there are dragons somewhere, presumably, and ancient evils in deep places. None of this is the party's immediate concern. The party lives in the working margins of that world — trade roads, port towns, tavern back rooms, the company of people who need things moved, guarded, found, or fixed.

The tone is low-fantasy in focus, not in fact. The wider world is genuinely extraordinary. It's just far away, and most of its extraordinary things are not on the Fenway road this week.

---

## The Fenway Coast — The Region

The campaign begins in the Fenway coastal region: a long stretch of settled country running east–west along the Fenway road, with the sea to the north and wilder hill country to the south. This is agricultural and mercantile territory — old enough to be settled, prosperous enough to be comfortable, unglamorous enough to be overlooked by the grander stories.

The landscape: grey-green hills, scrub oak and limestone outcrops in the uplands, flat arable country nearer the coast. The sea to the north is cold and reliable and smells of brine and fish. The sky is often overcast. Rain is a structural feature of the climate rather than a weather event.

Communities along the Fenway are connected by trade and road and a shared practical culture: pragmatic hospitality, cautious welcome to strangers, strong opinions about fair dealing. They are not naive about the wider world's dangers — the walls of Brindlemark are old but maintained for a reason — but their day-to-day concerns are grain harvests, market prices, and the reliability of the roads.

## Key Settlements

### Stoneveil Reach

A small harbour town at the base of the highland ridge, where the Goliath communities of the upper country meet the sea. Stoneveil Reach is not a Goliath settlement — it is a mixed town of fisherfolk, sail-makers, and small traders that grew up because a natural harbour existed. Goliath families who came down from the ridge a generation or two ago settled here and stayed; their descendants, like Cade's family, are coastal people now, shaped as much by the sea as by the highlands.

The town has a port chapel, a modest market, and the particular social texture of a place that exists at the meeting of two different worlds. The autumn fog that rolls off the highland ridge into the harbour is a Stoneveil institution. In Greymantle dialect, there is a specific word for it.

Stoneveil Reach is Cade's home. He has not been back for some years.

### Greymantle Keep

The highland Goliath settlement immediately above Stoneveil Reach, in the ridge country. Greymantle Keep is one of several such communities in the highlands — stone-built, practical, with the particular culture of a people whose heritage includes the blood of Storm Giants. Some bloodlines in Greymantle carry that ancestry more strongly than others; it manifests differently in different individuals.

Twenty years ago, a Goliath blacksmith named Orrath Stone-Handed emigrated from Greymantle Keep to the city of Ashford, carrying his craft and his language and whatever else he took with him. He is not the last Greymantle emigrant, but he is a significant one, for reasons that are not yet fully understood.

Greymantle Keep is not accessible to the party at present. It is background — a place that is real and that matters, a thread rather than a destination.

### Ashford

A proper city well east along the Fenway — large enough to have guilds, a functioning militia, a thieves' circuit, a significant Goliath blacksmithing community, and a Guild that oversees certain categories of arcane practice in ways that are not entirely transparent. Ashford is where Rixon grew up, where he apprenticed, where he served in the Ward Guard, and where — some years ago — a blacksmith's forge was shut by Guild order and its records sealed.

Ashford does not feature in the campaign's opening, but its past is present in two of the party members. It is the kind of city that generates threads.

### Kelstead Port

The major harbour town of the Fenway coast — large enough to have a hiring hall, a port chapel, a proper harbour authority (with an administration building where Corvan works), several inns, and the kind of mixed-species working population that a busy trading port generates. Kelstead is the hub through which cargo moves between the coastal sea routes and the inland Fenway road.

The party passed through Kelstead Port before the campaign and returned to it after Thornwater. They did not stay. They left Corvan behind, checked the port chapel for Sael's response (none), took a contract at the hiring hall, and went west.

Kelstead is a resource: Corvan is there and knows things, the port chapel sends and receives messages, and the hiring hall posts work. It is not home to any of them.

### Thornwater

A secondary harbour town north of Kelstead, accessible by a day's sail and a short inland transit. Thornwater is smaller and less prosperous than Kelstead — it services fishing fleets and the occasional merchant vessel, and its harbour authority is the kind of institution that moves slowly and keeps careful records.

This is where Captain Vetha was detained. This is where Kilda's cargo was impounded. The sealed cargo shed is still there, and the impound seal is still in the red wax of the Thornwater Harbour-Masters' mark. Two parties in Thornwater had an interest in the undeclared consignment; neither has been identified. The party left before this could be established.

Thornwater is unfinished business, specifically Kilda's.

### Brindlemark

A small walled town at the junction of the Fenway road (east–west) and the Drover's Track (south, into hill country). Population perhaps 1,200. Stone walls twelve feet high, old but maintained. Two gates: the Market Gate facing east onto the Fenway, and the Shepherd's Gate facing south onto the Drover's Track.

The economy runs on trade road traffic, sheep from the southern hills, and a grain harvest from farms outside the walls. Brindlemark is prosperous enough to have a cobbler and a brewer but not a proper smithy — metalwork comes in by wagon. It is the correct level of town: small enough that strangers are noticed, large enough to have work worth doing.

**Key locations:**

- **The Gatehouse.** Where contract work is posted and pay is collected. Run by the town's small constabulary. A corkboard of notices inside the door. The constable who runs it is a person with a pragmatic relationship to paperwork and a clear-eyed sense of what the town actually needs.
- **The Kettle & Crow.** The larger of the two taverns, near the market square. Rooms upstairs, common room below. Travellers and working swords end up here. The ale is adequate; the food is hearty; the fire is reliable.
- **The Lame Dog.** The smaller tavern, down a side street. More locals, fewer travellers. Cheaper, quieter, rougher. The kind of place where things are said that wouldn't be said at the Kettle & Crow.
- **The Market Square.** Open most mornings. Farmers, tinkers, a few merchants trading goods off the Fenway.
- **Brindlemark Chapel.** A modest stone chapel that serves the community for blessings, funerals, and festivals. It is not dedicated to any single god — it serves whoever needs it. The Alderman contributes to its upkeep. Cade will check it for correspondence.
- **Alderman Cray's House.** The largest building in town, near the square. Alderman Cray is the closest thing Brindlemark has to a mayor: practical, unsentimental, and more competent than the modesty of the setting might suggest.

**Atmosphere:** Mud and thatch and stone. Woodsmoke. The sound of carts on cobble. Sheep occasionally tracked through the Market Gate. People who know each other's business and have opinions about it. Travellers regarded with pragmatic hospitality: welcome for their coin, watched for their trouble.

## Species & Communities

### Goliaths

The Goliath communities of the highland ridge are old and insular by instinct, with a tradition of emigration that is individual rather than collective. Those who leave go to cities or coastal towns; those who stay maintain the Keeps and the old ways. Storm Giant blood runs in many Goliath lineages, stronger in some than others; it does not always manifest visibly, and those who carry it do not always understand what they carry.

The Goliaths who came down to Stoneveil Reach a generation ago occupy an ambiguous position: they are coastal people now, shaped by the sea and the mixed community of the harbour, but they speak Giant among themselves and remember Greymantle and know the fog-word.

### Dwarves

The Dwarven mountain holds of the region are several days' travel inland and south — old communities built into the bones of the hills, rich in metalwork and precision instruments and the accumulated capital of generations of careful trade. The Dwarven commercial network extends outward along the Fenway and the river routes: Kilda's background is in this network, managing goods between the holds and the Halfling river communities.

Dwarves in Fenway port towns are a common sight: merchants, factors, the occasional wizard who found mountain academia too provincial. They carry a reputation for exactness in matters of contract and debt, a reputation that is mostly deserved.

### Halflings

The Halfling river-vine communities occupy the winding river valleys east and south of the Fenway — small, close-knit settlements built along waterways, with a tradition of skilled craft and a geographic familiarity with the land between the rivers that no one else can quite match. Mall-Lowick comes from this world, though she has spent most of her adult life in the hill country rather than the river valleys.

Halflings are common in port towns and market towns along the Fenway, working as traders, scribes, and specialists of various kinds.

### Humans

The majority population of Fenway towns, without any single cultural character. Rixon is human; so are most of the people in Brindlemark, Thornwater, and the crew of *The Grey Cormorant*. Humans in this setting are unremarkable in their variety, which is to say they are everywhere and everything.

## The Wider World

The campaign does not begin with the wider world pressing in. It is there — distant, enormous, doing what it does — and it will become relevant in time.

Rumours that circulate in port towns and hiring halls: a succession dispute in one of the eastern kingdoms; strange lights seen above the highland ridge in late autumn; a blight on grain stores in two of the river towns, cause unclear; a trading company that has been hiring heavily and quietly for reasons no one can confirm; a Guild closure in Ashford, some years back, whose records are sealed.

These are the kinds of things that come up at tables in the Kettle & Crow. Some of them are nothing. Some of them are threads.

## Religion & the Divine

The gods of this world are real and present, though not uniformly attentive. Different communities have different patron deities; shrines in Fenway towns tend to serve local and practical functions rather than grand theological ones.

Cade's divine connection is to the storm — specifically to whatever intelligence or force runs through the Storm Giant blood in his lineage. He was not converted; he was recognised, or something in the blood was recognised, in the way that storms notice what they will notice. He does not know exactly what is listening. He knows that something is. His holy symbol is a silver-grey amulet, the shape abstract, like a breaking wave or an open hand; he had it from a travelling divine at Stoneveil Reach when he was seventeen and it has been at his chest since.

The Brindlemark Chapel is ecumenical by necessity and serves all comers. It will accept Cade's presence without complication.

The arcane and the divine overlap in this world in ways that are not fully mapped. Orrath's forge — whatever it was doing — sat at one end of this overlap. Cade's ancestry sits at another. Whether these are connected threads or parallel ones is not yet known.

## The Demimonde

Below the surface economy of the Fenway — the markets, the trade contracts, the legitimate commerce — is the working underworld: mercenaries, fixers, information brokers, thieves' guilds in the larger towns, the loose network of operators who work outside official frameworks for clients who prefer their problems handled quietly.

This is Rixon's native environment. He moves through it with the ease of someone who knows the codes, the rates, the people worth knowing, and the people worth avoiding. The demimonde of the Fenway has its own geography: certain taverns, certain back rooms, certain gaming tables where Knavebones is the social currency and the real business is conducted in what people say while they're watching the dice.

Brindlemark is small for a proper underworld, but the Lame Dog has a back room, and the Drover's Track south of town has its own economies. Kelstead Port is more fertile ground and is a resource the party has not yet fully used.
