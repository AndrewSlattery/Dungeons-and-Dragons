# Pre-Campaign Vignettes

*Six scenes from the period before the campaign's current job begins — one for each pair of characters, set during prior operations, recruitment meetings, and the intervals between work.*

---

## I. Bradkell + Darwhidan
*A tavern called The Millrace, industrial quarter — nine days before the Aldworth job*

The back room of The Millrace smelled of hops and tallow and the particular staleness of a space that had been closed up since morning and opened only when someone needed it for a conversation that couldn't happen in the common room. The walls were damp plaster over old brick, and where the plaster had given up there were patches of bare stone with the faint mineral smell of the district's groundwater. A single candle on the table. Two mugs, neither of them touched.

Bradkell was already seated when Darwhidan arrived, which was unusual — Darwhidan made it a professional habit to arrive first in any room, because the person already seated had chosen their position and the person arriving had to choose theirs in response, and he preferred to be the one with the prepared geometry. He noted this, said nothing about it, and sat down across the table.

Bradkell was in his working clothes: rough canvas jacket, dusty boots, the broad hands of a man who looked like he repaired drays for a living resting flat on the table on either side of a piece of parchment that had been folded twice and then unfolded. He looked, as he always looked, like someone who had wandered in from the brewery floor and was waiting for the shift foreman to finish his drink. His eyes were doing something entirely different from the rest of him.

"The east wing has two access points," Bradkell said. "One from the garden terrace, one from the servants' corridor off the kitchen." He turned the parchment — a floor plan, hand-drawn in ink, annotated in a small, precise script that did not match his appearance. "The terrace door is warded. The servants' corridor is not, but it passes within fifteen feet of the butler's quarters, and the butler sleeps light."

Darwhidan looked at the plan. He had already seen the east wing. He had walked through it six days ago, in fine clothes, carrying the prop notebook and an expression of mild scholarly confusion, while a steward showed him to a drawing room he had no intention of reaching. He had mapped the corridor widths by counting his own strides, noted the lock manufacturer on the terrace door from the maker's mark visible at the keyhole, and identified a third access point that was not on Bradkell's plan.

"There's a window," he said. "Second floor, above the garden wall. The frame is original — older than the lock on the terrace. The latch is a hook-and-pin. I can open it from outside with a dagger blade in less than four seconds."

Bradkell studied him. "You saw this during the walk-through."

"I saw this while the steward was explaining the provenance of a painting in the upstairs hall. He was very proud of it. I was very interested."

"And the drop from the window to the garden wall?"

"Eighteen feet, roughly. Acceptable."

"For you."

Darwhidan's gold irises caught the candlelight — the metallic warmth of them visible even in the dim room, the one part of him that his careful nonchalance could not entirely obscure. He smiled. It was the smile of a man who found the mathematics of falling to be a matter of aesthetic preference rather than survival calculation.

"For me," he agreed.

Bradkell folded the plan. He did not look irritated, exactly — he looked like a man whose model had just been amended by data he hadn't collected, and who was recalculating rather than objecting. This was the thing about Darwhidan that Bradkell found simultaneously useful and difficult: he was almost always right about the physical reality of a space, and almost never able to explain how he'd arrived at his assessment in terms that could be incorporated into a repeatable framework. He just knew. He'd been there, and he'd seen it, and the thing he'd seen was correct.

"The second-floor approach changes the timing," Bradkell said. "If you enter from above, the garden patrol needs to have passed the east wall before you begin the climb, which means Milores holds position at the servants' gate for an additional four minutes."

"Milores can hold position for four hours if she decides to."

"She can. She'll want to know why."

"Because the window is better than the door. That's the whole of it."

Bradkell picked up his mug for the first time, though he didn't drink from it. He held it the way he held everything — as a prop, a thing to do with his hands while his mind finished a calculation. In the common room beyond the closed door, someone was laughing, and the sound of it came through the plaster muffled and warm.

"I'll adjust the sequence," Bradkell said. "But I need you to walk me through the window approach step by step, so I can build the contingencies."

"I can tell you what I'll do. I can't always tell you why one route feels right and another doesn't."

"I know." Bradkell set the mug down. "Tell me the what. I'll build the why."

Darwhidan leaned back in his chair, the broad shoulders easy, the thick neck inclined, the gold eyes bright. He began to describe the window, the wall, the garden below, the angle of the drop and the specific quality of the stone where his hands would go, and as he spoke Bradkell listened with the full attention of someone who was translating instinct into architecture, note by note, the way he translated everything.

## II. Bradkell + Milores
*A rooftop overlooking Candlers' Row — forty minutes before a job*

The roof was flat clay tile, cracked in places and patched with tar, and the smell up there was candle wax and chimney smoke and the cold mineral edge of a clear night in the mercantile quarter. The city spread below in lantern-light — Candlers' Row running south toward the bridge, the guild-houses dark except for their watchman's lamps, the river beyond them catching a thin line of moonlight on its current. It was the kind of night that sat still and waited.

Milores was sitting against the chimney stack with her knees drawn up, her spear laid flat beside her. Her skin was the deep scorched crimson that it always was, visible even in the dark, and the obsidian horns caught the faint light from the street below in two small curves. She had a wooden case open on her knee — compact, worn at the clasps, the size of a thick book — and she was painting.

The canvas was no larger than her hand. The brush moved in small, certain strokes: cadmium yellow laid over a dried ground of Prussian blue, building up the last light of a coastal sunset against a cliff edge she had seen once and remembered precisely. She did not look up when Bradkell's weight settled on the tiles three feet to her left. She had heard him on the ladder. She had been tracking his approach since he crossed the alley below.

He sat down. He did not look at the painting. He had learned, over four jobs and several conversations that were not exactly conversations, that the painting was not something Milores shared by invitation. It existed in a space she had built around it — visible, unmentioned, understood to be present and understood to be private — and the correct response to that space was to leave it alone until she moved the boundary herself.

"Darwhidan's in position," he said. "Tanenni's in the garden. The patrol passed the east gate two minutes ago."

"I heard them." She did not stop painting. "Heavy boots. Three guards, not two. The third is new — his stride is shorter than the others. Recently added to the rotation."

Bradkell processed this. A third guard was not in the plan. He had built the timing around two. His expression, had there been enough light to read it, would have shown the particular quality of a man recalculating a sequence he had been satisfied with.

"Does it change the approach?" he asked.

"No. It changes the withdrawal." She set the brush across the top of the case and closed it — not quickly, not with any urgency, with the same deliberate care she gave to everything. "A third guard on the east gate means the rotation interval drops from eight minutes to six. We lose two minutes on the extract."

"I can adjust. If Darwhidan moves to the second-floor window instead of the terrace — "

"He'll move to the window regardless. He prefers it." She stood, picked up her spear, and rolled her shoulders once. The motion had the economy of someone who had been doing this for a very long time and did not warm up so much as arrive. "The two minutes matter on the back end, not the front. We need to be clear of the garden wall before the new guard reaches the south corner on his return pass."

"Which means Tanenni pulls the animals from the garden four minutes earlier than planned."

"Yes."

Bradkell nodded. He was already drafting the adjustment in his head — the revised sequence, the cascading changes to each position, the message he would need to leave in Druidic on the garden wall for Tanenni to read. He did this the way he did everything: systematically, thoroughly, with the particular pleasure of a theorist whose models were being tested by contact with reality and were holding up.

Milores watched him think. She did not say she respected this — she did not say things like that, because the saying of them served no operational function and the fact itself was legible enough. She respected competence in any form, and Bradkell's competence was the kind that made operations work: not the hands, not the feet, but the thinking that told the hands and feet where to be.

"Two minutes," she said. "Not less."

"Two minutes. I'll signal with the flute — the low phrase, the one Tanenni knows."

"Good." She looked out over the city. The candle-wax smell was stronger now, rising from the workshops below as the evening's work cooled. Somewhere south a dog was barking, and nearer there was the sound of a cart on cobblestones, the ordinary percussion of a city that did not know it was being robbed. "Your plan is sound."

He looked at her. She said this rarely and meant it when she said it.

"Four minutes from now," he said.

She was already moving toward the edge of the roof, where the drainpipe ran down to the alley and the shadows were deepest. He watched her go with the quiet attention of someone who knew exactly what he was looking at: a professional operating within her code, moving toward work that fell inside acceptable parameters, efficient and precise and entirely reliable. He picked up his flute and waited for the time.

## III. Bradkell + Tanenni
*The temple archive, after hours — three weeks before the current job*

The archive was underground, which was the temple's concession to climate and the city's concession to the water table, and the result was a long stone room with a vaulted ceiling where the candles in their iron brackets produced more shadow than light and the air had the cool permanent damp of a space that had never been warm. The shelves ran floor to ceiling on both sides — leather-bound ledgers, rolled parchments in wooden cases, documents in sealed folios marked with the temple's stamp in blue wax. The accumulated records of twelve years of donations, disbursements, correspondences, and the quiet financial architecture of an institution that did a great deal of good and a precise, calculable amount of harm.

Tanenni moved through it the way she moved through every space she had mapped: without hesitation, without wasted motion, her brass scales catching the candlelight in small warm flashes as she passed from one pool of light to the next. Her temple robes whispered against the stone floor. The holy symbol on its chain was the most visible thing about her — thick enough to see from across a room, which was the point, because the symbol was the credential that made her presence here unremarkable.

She stopped at the fourth shelf on the west wall, drew out a folio, and opened it on the reading table. The document inside was a property deed — or rather, it had been a property deed before someone with excellent calligraphic skills had altered two lines of it with ink that matched the original so precisely that the alteration was invisible unless you knew where to look. She knew where to look. She had made the alteration.

Bradkell arrived through the side entrance that connected the archive to the temple's root cellar — a passage Tanenni had shown him on his second visit, after she had decided he was someone whose presence in the archive could be useful. He smelled faintly of fermented grain, which was his permanent condition and also the reason he could move through the industrial quarter without attracting attention: he smelled like everyone else there.

He sat down at the reading table without being invited, because they had moved past invitations after the second job. He set a journal on the table — small, leather-bound, filled with notes in a code that she had not asked him to explain and he had not offered to. He opened it to a page of tightly written text and turned it toward her.

"The Aldworth estate," he said. "Tanenni, the numbers don't match."

She looked at his notes. He had been through the public tax records, the guild-house levy schedules, and the estate's declared charitable contributions for the past three years, and he had found the gap she had found four months ago: the declared income was roughly sixty percent of the actual, and the difference was moving through channels that did not appear in any public ledger.

"I know," she said.

"How long have you known?"

"Since before the crew existed, in this specific case." She turned a page of the folio. "The Aldworth family has used the temple's blessed vaults for storage since at least the year I was assigned to the archive. I identified the discrepancy in my second year. I did not act on it then because I did not have the infrastructure."

"And now you do."

"Now I do."

Bradkell studied her. She was not looking at him — she was reading the folio, her vertical-slit pupils tracking the lines with the speed of someone who had been reading these documents for most of her adult life. Her expression was the expression she always wore: calm, measured, the particular stillness of someone who had decided what mattered and was attending to it.

"Your intelligence on the patrol schedule," he said. "The sparrows?"

"The pigeons. The sparrows cover the roof approaches. The pigeons roost on the estate's east wall and have regular sight lines to the garden and the servants' entrance. They report movement patterns with reasonable consistency — arrival times, departure times, the number of guards visible at the gate at any given interval."

He was silent for a moment. In the archive, the candles guttered slightly in a draft from the cellar passage, and the shadows moved on the vaulted ceiling.

"You built the intelligence network before you built the crew," he said. It was not a question.

"The animals were already there. I learned to listen to them. The crew arrived after the intelligence made it clear that the work was viable." She closed the folio and returned it to the shelf with the care of someone replacing a book she would need again. "You built the operational theory before you found the crew. The sequence is the same."

He looked at her. This was the thing about Tanenni that he found genuinely humbling, and Bradkell was not a man who experienced humility as a frequent or comfortable sensation: she had been doing this longer than he had, with less infrastructure, with better information, and she had never once mentioned it as leverage. She communicated conclusions. She did not narrate the path. He had to ask.

"The Aldworth vault," he said. "What are the wards?"

She sat down across from him and began to describe what the animals had told her and what the ledgers confirmed and what the ward structure was likely to be given the estate's contract with the guild-arcanist whose name appeared in the temple's disbursement records, and Bradkell listened and took notes in his coded journal, and the archive was quiet around them except for the scratch of his pen and the steady, precise voice of a woman who had learned to read a city the way other people read weather, and who had decided, for reasons she had not fully explained to herself, that this particular crew was worth the investment.

## IV. Darwhidan + Milores
*A safe house on Roper Street — two hours after the Brevary job*

The safe house was above a chandler's shop, which meant the air smelled permanently of rendered fat and tallow, and the single room had the particular quality of a space that existed only for the purpose of not being somewhere else. One table, two chairs, a straw pallet against the wall that nobody had ever slept on, a window overlooking the alley that Darwhidan had identified as the best egress point before he had identified anything else about the room. The shutter was open. The alley was dark and quiet. The job was done.

Milores was cleaning her hands.

She did this after every operation — not washing, exactly, but a specific ritual of attention: examining each finger, each knuckle, each callus, the way a craftsman examines tools after use. Her hands were large for a woman, scarred across the backs, the fingertips calloused from two different kinds of precision — the kind that broke things and the kind that painted them. At present the calluses were marked with the faint residue of cadmium yellow from the afternoon's work and a newer darkness at the heel of her right palm where she had struck the side of a guard's neck four hours ago.

Darwhidan was at the window, leaning against the frame in the particular way of someone who was not watching the alley so much as maintaining a professional awareness of it. His fine clothes were slightly disordered — a thread pulled at the shoulder, a scuff on the knee from the wall — and his leather coat was open, and the prop notebook was in his coat pocket where it always was, and his gold eyes were half-closed in a way that looked like relaxation and was actually the specific state he entered after a job when his body was still running on the chemicals of the work and his mind had not yet caught up.

Neither of them had spoken since arriving. This was not unusual. The post-operation silence between them was different from the silence between any other pair in the crew — it was not the silence of people who had things to say and were choosing not to, or of people who were tired, or of people who needed to process what had happened. It was the silence of two people who had both been paying the right kind of attention during the work and did not need to debrief, because they had both already seen everything the other had done and understood why.

"The second guard," Milores said. Not a question. A reference point.

"I saw."

"He was reaching for the whistle."

"I know. You were fast."

She looked at her right hand, the heel of the palm. The guard had been reaching for the alarm whistle on the cord around his neck — a motion that would have ended the operation and begun a different kind of evening. She had crossed four feet of corridor in the time it took his hand to travel from his side to his chest, and she had stopped the motion with a strike that was precise enough to put him down without being precise enough to do permanent damage. She had checked his breathing afterward, because the code required it.

"Clean," she said.

"Yes." He shifted against the window frame. "The lock on the vault anteroom — did you notice the delay?"

"The pin stuck."

"The pin stuck." He flexed his right hand — the one that had been inside the mechanism, the fingers that had felt the resistance and adjusted and worked through it in the dark. "Old mechanism. The tumblers were original to the building. I should have anticipated the stiffness."

"Three seconds."

"Four. I counted."

"Three. I was counting from outside the door."

He almost smiled. This was their arithmetic — the shared language of seconds and distances and the precise timing of physical actions performed under pressure. Other people talked about jobs in terms of outcomes, complications, what went well and what went wrong. Milores and Darwhidan talked about them in terms of the specific mechanics of the work: the lock, the guard, the corridor width, the number of seconds between one thing and the next. It was the conversation of two people who had both arrived at their expertise through years of doing difficult things with their hands and who recognised in each other the quality of attention that made those things look effortless.

The alley below was still quiet. Somewhere in the district a bell was ringing the hour — late, the hollow iron sound of it carrying over the rooftops. The tallow smell from the chandler's shop below had settled into the room like a permanent resident.

"The window exit," Milores said.

"What about it?"

"You went headfirst."

"I went headfirst because the frame was narrower than the plan suggested and a feet-first exit would have cost two seconds of repositioning on the sill."

"You went headfirst because you find it more interesting."

He did smile then. "Both things are true."

She stood, rolled her shoulders, and picked up her spear from where it leaned against the wall. She did not say anything further about the window. She had logged it — the unnecessary risk, the variance it introduced, the fact that it had worked — and she would carry this data into the next operation the way she carried everything: quietly, completely, without requiring it to be discussed again.

"Tanenni will want the debrief by morning," she said.

"I know."

"Tell her about the pin."

"I will."

She left through the door, which was how she always left: through the planned exit, at the planned time, without looking back. Darwhidan remained at the window for a few minutes longer, looking at the alley and the dark rooftops beyond it, and thought about locks and corridors and the specific quality of silence that existed between two people who had both been very good at something difficult on the same evening, and who had known it about each other without needing to say so.

## V. Darwhidan + Tanenni
*A private vault beneath the Harrowell estate — three years before the campaign*

The vault was not supposed to have two people in it.

It was supposed to have one: specifically, Darwhidan, who had entered the Harrowell estate ninety minutes earlier through the servants' entrance wearing the expression of a man who had been sent to deliver something and had gotten mildly lost, and who had since navigated two locked doors, one warded corridor, and a staircase that descended into the limestone bedrock beneath the house, arriving at a reinforced oak door with an iron lock of respectable quality that he had opened in eleven seconds.

The vault beyond the door was small — perhaps twelve feet square, with a low stone ceiling and walls lined with iron-banded chests. The air had the stale, enclosed quality of a space that was opened rarely and ventilated never. A single oil lamp burned on a bracket by the door, which was the first thing that was wrong, because a vault that was supposed to be unoccupied should not have a lit lamp.

The second thing that was wrong was the Dragonborn standing beside the largest chest.

She was brass-scaled, lean, wearing temple robes over hide armour, and she was holding a sickle in one hand and a document folio in the other. Her eyes, in the lamplight, were dark and steady and entirely unsurprised. She had heard him on the stairs.

Darwhidan stopped in the doorway. He assessed the situation with the speed of someone who had spent years reading rooms: the sickle was held low, not raised — not a threat posture, a readiness posture. The folio contained documents she had already selected, which meant she was further along in her own operation than he was in his. The temple robes were genuine. The armour beneath them was not standard temple issue. The holy symbol at her chest was large enough to see from across the room, which meant it was a credential, not a devotion.

She assessed him in approximately the same interval. Broad-shouldered Aasimar in fine clothes. Gold irises — celestial heritage, which she filed and set aside. Thieves' tools visible at his belt, which meant he was not here as a guest. The prop notebook in his coat pocket — she could see the spine of it, and she recognised the specific quality of an object carried for show rather than use. His hands were empty and positioned at his sides in the deliberate openness of someone who wanted to signal that the next few seconds were negotiable.

"Harrowell," she said. It was not a greeting. It was a confirmation of context.

"Harrowell," he agreed.

The lamp hissed faintly. In the silence, the stone walls gave back a quality of enclosed air that made the space feel smaller than it was.

"I've been here for twenty minutes," she said. "The correspondence is already in the folio. The securities are in the second chest from the left, which is where the actual value is. The gold in the front chest is a decoy — real gold, but a fraction of the estate's liquid assets, placed where it will be found first by someone who comes through the door in a hurry."

He looked at the front chest. He had been going to open the front chest. The decoy would have cost him perhaps three minutes before he realised, which was three minutes he would not have had, because the guard rotation would have brought someone to the garden door by then.

"You've been here twenty minutes," he said, "and you haven't taken the securities."

"I don't have the carrying capacity. The securities are in sealed tubes. There are fourteen of them. I can carry six in the folio. I was considering my options when you arrived."

She looked at him with the calm, administrative attention of someone who was evaluating a logistical problem and had just identified a potential solution.

"I'll carry eight," he said. "Split it even."

"You'll carry eight and we leave separately. I go up through the cellar passage to the garden. You go back the way you came."

"The servants' entrance is clear for another six minutes."

"Five. The rotation is faster than you think — the new guard on the east patrol walks quickly."

He noted this. She was right — he had estimated the rotation from his own observation during the walk-through, but her estimate was more precise, which meant she had a better intelligence source than casual observation. He did not ask what it was. He would ask later, if there was a later.

She opened the second chest. The securities were there — rolled documents in sealed brass tubes, each one representing a title, a deed, or a financial instrument whose value was considerable and whose chain of possession was, she had reason to believe, not entirely legitimate. She counted fourteen, divided them into two stacks of seven, and then moved one from her stack to his.

"Eight and six," she said. "Your route is longer. Compensation for the additional risk."

"That's generous."

"That's arithmetic." She handed him the tubes. Her hands were steady, the brass scales warm in the lamplight. "If you're caught, the tubes are unmarked. There is nothing connecting them to this vault or to me."

"And if you're caught?"

"I won't be. The cellar passage exits into the herb garden, and I have a reason to be in a garden at night."

He looked at her. She was already packing the folio, the six tubes fitting neatly alongside the correspondence she had selected, the whole assembly disappearing into the folds of her robes with the practised efficiency of someone who had rehearsed this. The sickle went into a loop at her belt. She looked, in the final arrangement, exactly like a temple acolyte carrying a prayer book and some administrative documents, which was precisely what she was, except for the part where she was also robbing a nobleman's vault.

"I'll want to discuss this further," he said. "Professionally."

She looked at him. For the first time, something shifted in her expression — not warmth, not quite, but the particular quality of someone who had been working alone for a long time and was considering, with genuine interest, the possibility that this was no longer strictly necessary.

"The Crossed Keys," she said. "The back room. Three days from now, after the evening bell."

"I'll be there."

"Bring your half." She moved toward the cellar passage. "I keep accurate records."

She left through the cellar. He left through the servants' entrance with four minutes to spare, the eight tubes inside his coat, the gold irises bright in the dark corridor, and the particular feeling — rare for him, genuinely rare — that he had just met someone whose competence was of a kind he had not previously encountered and whose offer to split a vault evenly with a stranger was the most interesting thing that had happened to him in a year.

Three days later, at The Crossed Keys, she taught him the first seven signs of Druidic, and he understood that the evening in the vault had not been an ending but a beginning of something he did not yet have a name for.

## VI. Milores + Tanenni
*The safe house on Brewer's Yard — the morning after Milores joined the crew*

The safe house on Brewer's Yard was better than the one on Roper Street — it had two rooms, a stove that worked, and a window that faced east, which meant it got the morning light. The morning light was the reason Tanenni had chosen it, though she had not said this to anyone, because the reason was not operational. The reason was that a room with eastern light was a room that felt like a reasonable place to begin a day, and she had spent enough days beginning in windowless archive rooms to have developed a preference.

She was at the table, which she had cleared of everything except a single ledger, an inkwell, and her calligrapher's supplies. The ledger was open to a page of figures — clean columns, temple-standard script, the numbers precise and the margins annotated in a hand so neat it looked mechanical. She was reviewing the accounts of the crew's last operation: expenses, disbursements, the percentage allocated to each member, the percentage she had quietly redirected to three separate charitable accounts that did not know where the money came from.

The door opened. Milores came in.

She had been with the crew for one job — the Brevary operation, four days ago. She had been recruited through a contact in the gambling-house circuit who had described her as the most efficient enforcer he had ever employed and the only one who had never broken anything she hadn't been asked to break. The contact had also mentioned, as a footnote, that she had walked out of a mercenary company rather than burn a village. Tanenni had considered this footnote more informative than the primary recommendation.

Milores filled the doorway for a moment — the heavy frame, the crimson skin dark against the morning light, the obsidian horns — and then came in and sat down at the table across from Tanenni without being directed to a chair. She was carrying her spear, which she leaned against the wall, and the small wooden case on her belt that contained her painting supplies, which she did not remove.

Tanenni noted the case. She had noticed it during the Brevary job — Milores had worked on something during the forty-minute wait before the approach, sitting in the dark on a warehouse roof with her knees drawn up and a brush moving in small strokes across a surface Tanenni could not see. She had not commented on it then. She did not comment on it now. She recognised the particular quality of a private practice carried inside a public life, because she carried one of her own.

"Your share from the Brevary job," Tanenni said, and slid a small cloth pouch across the table. "Sixty gold, minus expenses. The accounting is on the second page if you want to verify it."

Milores picked up the pouch, weighed it in her hand without opening it, and set it down. "I don't need to verify it."

"The offer stands."

"I know." She looked at the ledger, the columns, the clean figures. "You keep careful records."

"I keep accurate records. Careful implies something is being hidden. These are transparent to anyone in the crew who asks."

Milores considered this. It was the first thing Tanenni had said that surprised her — not the accounting, which she had expected to be competent, but the transparency, which she had not. In her experience of criminal operations, the books were the thing most likely to be wrong. A crew leader who invited scrutiny of the books was either foolish or genuinely clean, and Tanenni did not appear to be foolish.

"Four jobs," Milores said.

Tanenni looked at her. "I'm sorry?"

"From the deposit. On the cottage. At this rate, four jobs."

The sentence had the quality of something that had been calculated precisely and offered without decoration, the way Milores offered everything. It was not a confession. It was a data point — the specific reason she was here, delivered with the same economy she brought to every other communication.

Tanenni understood it immediately, because she understood ledgers, and a woman who had calculated the exact number of jobs between herself and a specific property was a woman who was keeping a ledger of her own. She filed this information in the same way she filed everything: completely, without judgment, with the particular attention of someone who knew that the things people counted were the things that mattered to them.

"What kind of cottage?" she said.

Milores looked at her. The question was unexpected — not the content of it, but the fact that it had been asked at all. People did not, as a rule, ask Milores follow-up questions about her plans. People found her plans to be either obvious or opaque, and in neither case did they inquire further.

"Coastal," she said. "North-facing window. For the light."

"For painting."

"Yes."

The morning light came through the east window and laid a long rectangle of warmth across the table between them. Tanenni closed the ledger. She did not say anything about the paintings, or the cottage, or the particular quality of a life planned in precise numerical increments toward a single specific goal. She understood it. She had spent twelve years in an archive counting other people's money and quietly redirecting what she could, and she knew what it was to have a project that required patience and arithmetic and the sustained willingness to do difficult work toward a definite end.

"The next job is in three weeks," she said. "The Aldworth estate. Bradkell is building the plan. I'll have the intelligence ready by the end of the week."

"What do you need from me?"

"Exterior security assessment. Patrol timing, guard quality, physical barriers. You'll have access to the east approach from the canal path — I'll leave the route marked in Druidic on the bridge abutment."

"I can't read Druidic."

"I'll draw a map." Tanenni paused. "I should have remembered that. The others can read the marks."

"The others have been here longer."

"Yes." She looked at Milores with the steady, evaluative attention that was her default mode — not cold, not warm, simply thorough. "You'll have the map by tomorrow evening. The assessment is due three days before the operation."

Milores stood, picked up the pouch and her spear, and moved to the door. In the threshold she stopped.

"The books are clean," she said. It was not a question. It was her assessment, delivered as a statement of professional evaluation, the same way she assessed a guard rotation or a lock or the structural integrity of a drainpipe she was about to climb.

"They are."

"Good."

She left. Tanenni sat in the morning light for a moment longer, looking at the closed ledger and the empty chair and the space where Milores had been. She was thinking about cottages and ledgers and the particular kind of trust that was built not on affection or shared history but on the simple, durable recognition that another person's accounting was honest.

She opened the ledger again and made a note in the margin — a small thing, an annotation beside the crew roster, in her own hand. She did not label it. She would know what it meant.
