# World Lore & Setting

---

## Overview

An urban fantasy world seen from the gutters, the rooftops, and the back offices. The gods are real; the aristocracy is entrenched; the guild system controls commerce with the unhurried confidence of an institution that wrote its own charter. Somewhere beyond the city walls there are forests, kingdoms, and the kinds of problems that produce epic quests. None of that is the crew's concern. The crew lives in **Corraith** --- a large, stratified, commercially powerful city where wealth pools at the top, labour pools at the bottom, and the distance between them is maintained by systems that have been running for centuries and are not interested in stopping.

The tone is heist fiction, not epic fantasy. The extraordinary is present --- sorcery, divine power, druidic communion --- but it exists within a social order that has learned to absorb it, regulate it, and where possible, profit from it. The crew operates in the gap between what the city's systems assume and what actually happens when someone with the right skills decides those assumptions are exploitable.

## Corraith --- The City

Corraith sits at the junction of the **River Thane** and the **Mirrenmere**, a broad tidal estuary that connects the city to the sea twelve miles south. The coastline continues north beyond the city, curving around the promontory toward the more remote northern reaches --- cliff country above the water, where the land is rural and the settlements are few. The river divides the city roughly in half: the western bank rises steeply to the bluffs where the noble estates and the great temples occupy the high ground; the eastern bank spreads flat along the floodplain where the docks, the warehouses, the industrial works, and the lower-city districts have accumulated over centuries of commerce.

Population: approximately 90,000, though the number shifts with the season --- the harbour trade swells the lower city in summer, and the winter court draws the aristocracy back from their country estates. The city is old enough that its walls have been rebuilt three times, each circuit larger than the last, and the oldest stonework --- visible in the foundations of the Thorngate temples --- predates any living institution's records.

The city is governed, in theory, by the **Council of Aldermen**, a body of twelve elected from the city's propertied classes. In practice, the Council ratifies decisions made elsewhere: in the guild halls, in the temple treasuries, in the private studies of the six or seven families whose wealth predates the Council itself. The current **Lord Mayor**, **Alderman Corren Blackwell**, is a competent administrator who understands his role as the public face of decisions he did not make, and performs it with the efficiency of a man who has calculated exactly what his position is worth and does not intend to jeopardise it.

**The smell of Corraith:** river water, coal smoke, wet stone, and the particular chemical tang of the tanneries in the Daerrow. On warm days, the fish markets in the harbour quarter add their contribution. On cold days, the coal smoke thickens and the lower city operates under a yellow haze that does not lift until the wind changes. The city is not beautiful. It is specific.

## The Quarters

### The Thorngate District

The high ground on the western bluffs, above the river. This is ecclesiastical and aristocratic territory: the great temples, the noble estates, the walled gardens and private chapels of families who have been here long enough to consider the city a personal possession. The streets are broad, paved in pale limestone, and maintained by a private levy that the Thorngate residents pay without complaint because the alternative is proximity to the lower city's infrastructure.

The **Temple of Measured Light** is here --- Tanenni's former institution, the largest temple in Corraith, whose compound occupies an entire block. The **Temple of the Risen Flame**, where Darwhidan's family serves, is three streets away. The **Mael-Arath compound** is on Thorngate Close, behind a wall that is exactly high enough to signal privacy without signalling defensiveness.

Security in the Thorngate is private and professional: the noble estates employ their own guards, the temples have their own wardens, and the **Thorngate Watch** --- a separate constabulary funded by the district's residents --- patrols the streets with the particular attentiveness of people whose employers live on those streets. The Thorngate Watch does not answer to the city's general constabulary. This is a point of some political sensitivity.

### The Sullow Quarter

The eastern bank of the Thane, downriver from the harbour. Industrial territory: breweries (those that survived), tanneries, chandleries, the workshops and small manufactories that service the harbour trade. The streets are narrow, cobbled where they are cobbled at all, and the buildings are brick and timber, crowded close, with the particular architectural character of structures that have been extended, subdivided, and repurposed across generations.

This is Bradkell's territory. The **Sullow Compact** operates here through a network of safe houses, distribution points, and the informal infrastructure of a community that has learned to provide for itself because the formal systems do not. The **Charter Hall** --- the Guild of Chartered Commerce's district office --- sits on Foundry Street like an architectural rebuke: limestone facade, iron gates, a clock tower that is visible from every street in the quarter. It was built thirty years ago on the site of a cooperative grain exchange. The Compact remembers.

### The Daerrow

The floodplain south of the Sullow Quarter, where the river spreads and the ground is soft. The Daerrow is the lowest ground in the city in both senses: geographically, it floods in bad winters; socially, it is where the city's least-resourced populations live. Tiefling families, recent immigrants, the displaced and the dispossessed. Milores grew up here. Her mother's metalwork shop is still on Canal Street, still repairing boat hardware, still open.

The Daerrow's economy runs on canal traffic, small-scale manufacture, and the service work that the upper city requires and prefers not to think about. **Madame Oressa's** gaming houses --- three of them, in a row on Lampblack Lane --- are the Daerrow's most visible commercial establishments and its most significant informal employers. Oressa herself is a Tiefling woman of indeterminate age and considerable influence who has survived four attempts by the Guild of Chartered Commerce to bring her operations under licensed regulation. She survived them by being more useful to the right people than the regulation was.

### The Harbour Quarter

The waterfront district where the River Thane meets the estuary. Wharves, warehouses, customs houses, chandlers, ropewalks, and the offices of the trading companies that move goods between Corraith and the wider world. The harbour is Corraith's economic engine: everything that enters or leaves the city by water passes through here, and the **Harbour Authority** --- nominally a city institution, in practice a joint operation of the major trading houses --- controls the flow with the bureaucratic thoroughness of people who understand that every manifest is a revenue opportunity.

The **Harrowgate customs house**, which the crew has already robbed, sits at the harbour's western edge where the customs inspectors process incoming cargo. The crew's operation revealed that the customs house was skimming import duties into a fund controlled by a consortium of merchant-nobles --- a fund that has since been quietly reconstituted under different accounting.

### The Bridgeway

The district that connects the eastern and western banks across the **Thane Bridge**, Corraith's primary river crossing. The Bridgeway is commercial territory: shops, offices, the premises of lawyers and money-changers who serve both sides of the river. It is also the city's information market: the **Candlewick Tavern**, where Garren Thule operates his brokerage, is on Bridgeway Lane, and the back rooms of the Bridgeway's establishments are where the city's informal economy conducts its negotiations.

## Factions and Power

### The Guild of Chartered Commerce

The dominant economic institution in Corraith. The Guild controls commercial licensing, sets charter fees, employs the **Warden-Inspectors** who enforce compliance, and maintains a regulatory framework that has been refined over two centuries to produce a single outcome: the consolidation of commercial activity into operations large enough to afford the Guild's fees, and the elimination of those that cannot. The Guild's leadership --- the **Guildmaster**, **Edra Calloway**, and the **Charter Board** --- operate from the **Guildhall** on the Bridgeway, a building whose marble atrium is larger than most of the businesses the Guild has shut down.

The Warden-Inspectors are the Guild's enforcement arm: plainclothes investigators and uniformed inspectors who audit, cite, seize, and shut down unlicensed operations. They are well-funded, well-connected, and operate with the legal authority of the city charter. They are the crew's most persistent institutional opponent, though the opposition is not personal --- the Warden-Inspectors do not know the crew exists as a crew. They know that certain operations have been disrupted. They have not yet connected the disruptions.

### The Nobility

Corraith's aristocratic families occupy the Thorngate District and the country estates beyond the city walls. Their wealth is old, diversified, and entangled with every institution in the city: the temples store their assets, the Guild regulates on their behalf, the Council ratifies their preferences, and the harbour moves their goods. The **Corvane**, **Voss**, and **Solenne** families have all been the crew's targets. Others remain.

The nobility's security is private, professional, and variable in quality. The best estates employ former military officers and arcane security specialists; the worst rely on locks and the assumption that their social position is itself a defence. The crew has found the latter assumption fertile.

### The Temple Hierarchy

Corraith's temples are religious institutions, financial infrastructure, and political actors. The **Temple of Measured Light** and the **Temple of the Risen Flame** are the two largest, and between them they hold a significant fraction of the city's stored wealth in their blessed vaults --- wealth that is legally untouchable under the city's religious exemption statutes.

**High Archivist Solen Drath** of the Temple of Measured Light is the city's most significant ecclesiastical figure: a man who has managed the temple's dual function as house of worship and aristocratic vault for twenty years with the calm of someone who does not consider these functions contradictory. He is intelligent, patient, and has not, to Tanenni's knowledge, noticed the twelve-percent discrepancy. Tanenni does not find his patience reassuring.

### The Reedmen

Corraith's plainclothes investigative force, nominally under the authority of the Council of Aldermen but in practice semi-autonomous. The Reedmen investigate crimes that the regular constabulary cannot or will not pursue: complex fraud, institutional corruption, and the specific category of theft that targets the city's elite. They are named for the rushes that grow along the Thane's banks --- the idea being that they are present everywhere and noticed by no one.

The Reedmen are competent. Their lead investigator for financial crimes, **Inspector Cerani Whist**, is very competent. She has not yet connected the crew's seven operations into a pattern, but she has noted that the victims share a profile and that the operational methodology suggests a consistent intelligence source. She is working the problem. The crew does not know this.

### The Thieves' Circuits

Corraith's criminal economy is not a single organisation but a loose network of independent operators, fences, information brokers, and specialists who trade with each other when convenient and compete when not. **Garren Thule** at the Candlewick Tavern is the crew's primary fence and intelligence broker. **Madame Oressa** in the Daerrow is an independent power whose gaming houses function as neutral ground. The circuits have their own codes, their own reputation systems, and their own enforcement mechanisms, which are informal but effective.

The crew operates within this economy but is not of it. They are specialists, not generalists; their target profile is narrower than most operators, and their operational security is tighter. The circuits know that someone is working the Thorngate and the Harbour Quarter with precision. They do not know who. Thule knows, because Thule is their fence. Thule has not shared this information, because Thule understands that discretion is the product he is actually selling.

## The Crew's Operational Environment

### How Heists Work in Corraith

The crew's operations follow a consistent structure: intelligence gathering (Tanenni's animal network, temple access, and Bradkell's social reconnaissance), planning (joint sessions between Tanenni and Bradkell, with input from Darwhidan on physical security and Milores on contingencies), execution (typically a coordinated approach combining social engineering and physical infiltration), and extraction (clean exits, pre-planned routes, dead drops for materials that cannot be carried openly).

The city's security environment is layered: the **regular constabulary** patrols the streets and responds to disturbances; the **Thorngate Watch** protects the wealthy district; the **Warden-Inspectors** enforce guild compliance; the **Reedmen** investigate after the fact; and private security varies by target. The crew's advantage is that these layers do not communicate well with each other. The constabulary and the Thorngate Watch have a jurisdictional rivalry. The Warden-Inspectors report to the Guild, not the Council. The Reedmen operate independently. Information moves slowly between them, and the crew has learned to operate in the gaps.

### Arcane Security

Wards, alarms, and arcane locks protect the most valuable targets. The quality varies: temple wards are excellent (maintained by professional arcanists with centuries of institutional knowledge); noble estate wards range from competent to theatrical; guild wards are standardised and therefore predictable. Bradkell's self-taught understanding of ward theory and Tanenni's knowledge of temple security practices give the crew a meaningful edge against most arcane defences. Darwhidan's Mage Hand and Minor Illusion handle the physical and sensory layers. Milores handles everything that cannot be picked, bypassed, or misdirected.

## Religion and the Divine

The gods of Corraith are real, active, and institutionally managed. The city's temples are not houses of personal faith --- or not primarily --- but institutional frameworks that mediate between the divine and the civic. The **Temple of Measured Light** serves a deity of knowledge and order, whose theological position on the storage of aristocratic wealth in blessed vaults is, officially, that all knowledge and all things of worth deserve preservation. The **Temple of the Risen Flame** serves a deity of light and justice, whose martial order produces paladins and whose endowment fund is administered by Darwhidan's father with the careful attention of a man who understands that divine fire and compound interest are both powerful forces.

Tanenni's druidic practice sits outside this institutional framework. The **Circle of the Threshold** is small, operates from the city's western margins, and is not recognised by the temples as a legitimate religious order --- which suits the Circle, which has no interest in legitimacy and considerable interest in being left alone. Tanenni's druidic connection is genuine and her communion with animals is operationally significant: the sparrows, rats, and crows of Corraith are her intelligence network, and they report with a reliability that no human informant has matched.

Darwhidan's celestial heritage is real in the specific, physical sense: the gold irises, the radiant warmth, the Healing Hands that work whether he wants them to or not. The Mael-Arath family's interpretation --- that this heritage implies an obligation to serve --- is one reading. Darwhidan's interpretation --- that what the heritage implies and what he does with it are his business --- is another. The divine has not weighed in. Whether it will is a thread.

## The Wider Context

Corraith is not the only city in the world, and the systems the crew opposes are not unique to it. The guild model, the aristocratic wealth structures, the temple-as-vault arrangement --- these are regional patterns, replicated with local variation across the cities of the **Thanelands**, the broader political region of which Corraith is the commercial capital.

Beyond the Thanelands: kingdoms, forests, the kinds of distance that make other places abstract. The crew has no interest in the wider world except as it affects Corraith, and it affects Corraith primarily through trade --- the harbour is the funnel through which the world's goods enter the city and the city's wealth exits it, and the harbour is therefore the point at which external pressures become local ones.

Rumours that circulate in the Bridgeway and the harbour taverns: a trade dispute with the coastal cities to the south that is driving up import duties; a succession question in the Thanelands' eastern marches that has redirected military spending; a new Guildmaster in a neighbouring city who has been aggressively recruiting Warden-Inspectors from Corraith's force, which has created staffing gaps that the crew has noticed and filed.

These are the kinds of things that come up at tables in the Candlewick Tavern. Some of them are nothing. Some of them are threads.
