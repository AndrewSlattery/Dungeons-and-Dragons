# Party 3: Characters and Narrative

## A Story About Four People Who Rob the Same Targets for Entirely Different Reasons

Party 3 is a heist crew. Not a band of adventurers who happen to steal things, and not a group of thieves who happened to meet -- a crew, assembled deliberately over three years by a woman who had been doing this work alone for four years before she decided it was no longer strictly necessary. The four members share a target profile (the corrupt wealth of the city's elite), a method (precision infiltration), and a city (Corraith, a stratified commercial port where weight pools at the top and the distance between top and bottom is maintained by systems that have been running for centuries). What they do not share, and what makes them interesting, is a reason.

Bradkell steals because the system that concentrates wealth is the system that destroyed his father's brewery and priced mutual aid out of legality. Darwhidan steals because emptying the right vaults produces a specific aesthetic satisfaction he has not found elsewhere. Milores steals because the work is clean, the targets are in scope, and she is four jobs from the deposit on a coastal cottage with a north-facing window. Tanenni steals because the city's ecosystem is out of balance in ways that are not subtle once you are paying attention, and she is a person who corrects what is correctable. These four routes to the same destination do not produce four identical people, and the routes remain visible every time an operation produces an unexpected complication that was not in the plan.

The campaign's tone is heist fiction set inside a high-fantasy world. The gods are real, the aristocracy is entrenched, the guild system controls commerce with the confidence of an institution that wrote its own charter. Somewhere beyond the walls there are forests and kingdoms and the kinds of problems that produce epic quests. None of that is the crew's concern. What matters is the vault, the ward, the guard rotation, the exit, and whether these four people can continue to empty the right rooms without being caught by the Reedmen -- Corraith's plainclothes investigators, who have not yet connected seven operations into a pattern, though the word "yet" is doing significant work.

## The Characters

### Bradkell -- The Theorist of the Street

Bradkell is the crew's social engineer and its only ideologue, and the writing is careful to make those two facts aspects of the same skill. A heavy-built human whose magic surfaced unbidden and unwelcome, with broad shoulders, thick hands, and the permanent smell of fermented grain, he dresses in rough traveller's clothes and looks, in most company, like a very capable labourer who has wandered into somewhere slightly above his station. This is the misdirection. When he speaks, his voice fills a room without effort, and his eyes have been cataloguing everything in the space for the last ten minutes. His charisma is not the polished kind -- "it is the compelling kind, the kind that makes people think they have independently decided to agree with him."

He was born in the Sullow Quarter, Corraith's industrial district, the fourth son of a brewer whose business was destroyed by the Guild of Chartered Commerce's licensing regime. The Three Chimney Brewery on Millrace Lane survived two rounds of guild fee increases and one uninvestigated arson attempt. It did not survive the third round. Bradkell walks past the building -- now a guild-licensed grain storage facility -- when he has reason to be in that part of the quarter, "which is not often, and when he does he does not slow down." By fourteen he was a runner for the Sullow Compact, the mutual aid network that provides what the guilds have priced out of reach. By twenty he was planning operations. By twenty-two, during the Charter Hall riot, a ward-officer seized him, the air cracked, and the officer landed six feet away. Bradkell stood in the empty street and understood that something significant had changed and that its applications were considerable.

He taught himself arcane theory by whatever means presented themselves: borrowed texts, uninvited attendance at lectures under a false name, and one memorable afternoon in Alderman Solenne's private study where he memorised the ward-schema on the desk and relieved the shelf of a monograph on abjuration theory that Solenne has not yet noticed is missing. He understands wards and alarms not as a practitioner builds them but as an opponent analyses them. He knows what assumptions their operators make about who will try to circumvent them, and how those assumptions create exploitable patterns.

His role in the crew changed its operational profile. Before Bradkell, the approach was infiltration and extraction. After Bradkell, the approach includes the front-door entry, the manufactured invitation, the conversation that produces access without anyone needing to climb through a window. Darwhidan still climbs through windows. He considers this an aesthetic choice. Bradkell's planning sessions with Tanenni are the crew's most productive and least comfortable meetings -- productive because both are excellent, uncomfortable because both are precise and neither concedes a point without evidence.

The vignette with Tanenni in the temple archive captures his operational mind at its best. He arrives with a coded journal full of notes drawn from public tax records, guild-house levy schedules, and estate charitable contributions, and he has found the gap -- the declared income roughly sixty percent of the actual, the difference moving through channels that appear in no public ledger. Tanenni tells him she has known since before the crew existed. He studies her, and what he feels is not irritation but something rarer for him: genuine humility. She had been doing this longer, with less infrastructure, with better information, and she has never once mentioned it as leverage. He finds this humbling, "which is rare."

His unresolved threads are structural. He funds the Sullow Compact through intermediaries, in amounts that are real but not traceable, and his father -- alive, in the Quarter -- does not know the funding source. The Guild's enforcement arm, the Warden-Inspectors, have been increasing pressure on unlicensed charitable activity. The intersection of these two facts has not yet produced a crisis, but the geometry is clear. He treats social encounters like dragonchess, "always three moves ahead," and he has a gift for making people believe what he needs them to believe -- formidable in any conversation where the goal is to lead someone to a conclusion they think they reached on their own. He is somewhat weaker at reading what people are actually about to do. These are different skills, and the gap between them is the flaw that will cost him.

### Darwhidan -- The Aesthetic of the Fall

Darwhidan is the crew's infiltrator and its most joyful practitioner, a man whose relationship to the work is fundamentally one of pleasure. A broad-chested Aasimar professional thief built like a tavern bouncer of particular quality, his celestial heritage is visible if you look for it: metallic gold irises that catch light in a way ordinary eyes do not, a faint warmth that radiates from his skin in cold rooms, and "the overall impression that something significant lives behind the ordinary arrangement of his features." He moves through high society in impeccably tailored fine clothes that stretch comfortably over his heavy frame, looking like a very expensive and lightly intimidating bodyguard to someone important. Nobody searches the bodyguard. Nobody follows the bodyguard's hands. His hands, which look as though they belong wrapped around a greatsword, can pick a deadbolt in three seconds in total darkness, or slip a lord's signet ring off during a handshake while maintaining direct eye contact while asking about the lord's horses.

He was born into the Mael-Arath, one of Corraith's oldest Aasimar families, whose compound occupies a walled garden in the Thorngate District. Six generations of clerics, paladins, and temple functionaries. His parents expected, with the settled confidence of people who have already made the plan, that he would take vows and enter the temple's martial order. He was sent instead to the Corvane Estate to be seasoned: court manners, noble protocol, the social grammar of the upper tier. He learned all of these. He also learned that the estate had fourteen external doors, three never locked, and that the guard rotation left the west corridor unobserved for eleven minutes every two hours. The celestial fire expressed itself, in the end, as a conviction that the people in these houses had accumulated far more than they were owed, and as a practical, technical solution to that imbalance.

His prop notebook -- a convincing volume that appears to contain arcane formulae, complete with diagrams and annotations in a scholarly hand -- contains three old letters, a shopping list, and forty-seven pages of his own notes on estate security layouts. Several people have expressed admiration for it. He accepts this graciously. His relationship to gravity is a defining character detail: he has a thrill-seeker's specific preference for the vertical exit, the third-storey window, the spell cast at approximately the last moment that counts as not too late to slow his fall to something survivable. When Milores notes that he went headfirst through a window during the Brevary job, he explains that the frame was narrower than the plan suggested and feet-first would have cost two seconds. She replies that he went headfirst because he finds it more interesting. He smiles: "Both things are true."

The vignette with Tanenni in the Harrowell vault is the scene that defines his character. He arrives at a vault that is supposed to have one person in it and finds a Brass Dragonborn in temple robes holding a curved blade and a document folio. She has been there twenty minutes. She has already identified the decoy gold in the front chest -- real gold, a fraction of the actual assets, placed where it will be found by someone who comes through the door in a hurry. He had been going to open that chest. She offers to split it. He accepts. She gives him eight securities to her six because his route is longer: compensation for the additional risk. "That's generous," he says. "That's arithmetic," she replies. He leaves with the specific feeling, rare for him and genuinely rare, that he has met someone whose competence is of a kind he had not previously encountered.

His family sent two letters after the Corvane vault. The first expressed concern. The second expressed the specific kind of silence that functions as a conclusion. He did not reply to either. His access credentials at the Temple of the Risen Flame have not been formally revoked, which he considers an oversight worth preserving. The Mael-Arath silence is its own kind of thread -- his mother is a senior officiant at a major temple, his father administers its endowment, and they know what he has become. They have stopped writing.

### Milores -- The Code and the Cottage

Milores is the crew's specialist in violence and its moral bedrock -- not because she has the most elaborate ethical framework, but because the one she has is applied with the consistency of arithmetic. A physically imposing Tiefling woman trained in unarmed combat through a decade of mercenary service, with deep scorched-crimson skin and obsidian-black horns, her arms and shoulders carry the dense, functional muscle of a career soldier, crisscrossed with faded white scars she has never bothered to explain. When irritated or preparing to fight, a faint smell of sulfur enters the air and wisps of smoke occasionally curl from her nostrils, "a Tiefling tell she stopped trying to suppress years ago." She considers it adequately intimidating without any effort.

She grew up in the Daerrow, Corraith's floodplain district, the daughter of a metalworker who taught her the discipline of working with material that tells you what it will become if you pay attention. Recruited at sixteen by the Blackshore Company, she spent ten years becoming very good at infiltration of defended positions, silent neutralisation of sentries, and the dismantling of defensive formations from inside by someone who had no business being inside them. The cycle had a natural ending: eventually the difficult jobs include one you will not do. The one she would not do was burn the village of Thornfield to displace a lord's labour base. She walked into Captain Horst's tent, gave him one opportunity to reconsider, he did not reconsider, and she beat him with sufficient thoroughness to remove him from the remainder of the campaign. She does not describe this as a crisis of conscience. She describes it as her professional code encountering a situation that fell outside acceptable parameters.

Her code is simple and absolute: certain things are within scope, certain things are outside it. Hurting civilians is outside it. Harming people who cannot defend themselves is outside it. Everything else is a technical question. She does not want Bradkell's theory of systemic change. She has no use for it. What she respects is competence, and the writing makes clear that she assesses everyone -- including her colleagues -- through this lens. The rooftop vignette before the Candlers' Row job captures this precisely: she identifies a third guard in the patrol from his stride pattern alone ("heavy boots, three guards not two; the third is new, his stride is shorter"), calculates the timing implications, and delivers her assessment of Bradkell's plan -- "your plan is sound" -- with a rarity and a weight that he registers immediately.

What her appearance does not suggest: at every point of rest, her calloused fingertips are stained with paint. Cadmium yellow at the moment. She is working on a series of coastal landscapes based on a place she visited once and intends to return to. She paints before jobs -- on the warehouse roof during the forty-minute wait before the Brevary approach, on the rooftop overlooking Candlers' Row with a canvas no larger than her hand. The painting is not a performance of calm but an actual production of it, and the crew has noticed and not commented, and she is aware they have noticed. Bradkell has seen the paintings and has not said anything, because she has not invited the conversation. He is respecting the space. She is waiting to see whether he will.

She is four jobs from the deposit on the cottage. The cottage is real. It is on the coast north of Corraith, on a cliff above the sea, with a north-facing window. She has seen it once. She has painted it seven times. The arithmetic of the crew's operational tempo means four jobs is approximately one year. What happens when the number reaches zero -- whether she stays, whether she goes, whether the crew survives her departure -- is a question none of them have raised and all of them have considered.

### Tanenni -- The Woman Who Reads the City

Tanenni is the crew's architect, its intelligence base, and the person who has been doing this longest. A Brass Dragonborn whose connection to the natural world serves as her intelligence network, with scales the warm tarnished gold of old candlesticks, she moves with the measured deliberateness of a woman who has learned that urgency attracts attention. Over her hide armour she wears temple robes in layered deep blues and burnished gold -- the colours of no specific god but the colours of a god, and in the right light and the right doorway that is usually sufficient. Her holy symbol is thick enough to see from across a garden party, because the symbol is the credential. Her quarterstaff doubles as a walking staff, which doubles as a focus for her connection to the natural world, which doubles as something that can break a jaw with adequate motivation.

She was raised in the Temple of Measured Light, assigned to the archive at sixteen for her neat scales and neater handwriting. Eight years copying donation records. Four more years understanding that the charitable works represented approximately eleven percent of the temple's total income, and that the remaining eighty-nine percent moved through blessed vaults in ways that had nothing to do with worship. The temple functions, in practice, as a secure storage and laundering facility for Corraith's aristocratic families. Tanenni did not expose this. Exposure would have destroyed the genuine charitable work, provided no structural change, and ended her access. Instead, she quietly rerouted approximately twelve percent of the skimmed surplus back into the charitable accounts, altered the relevant ledger entries with calligraphic skill indistinguishable from the originals, and began making herself genuinely useful to people with resources worth redistributing. High Archivist Solen Drath has not noticed -- or has noticed and attributed the discrepancy to accounting variance. Neither possibility is comfortable.

The connection to the natural world arrived at twenty-nine, in the temple's kitchen garden, while repotting a bay laurel. She does not describe it in mystical terms. She understood something about the plant's intention and accepted this as new information. The animals she subsequently communicated with became her most reliable intelligence network: sparrows that map guard rotations, rats that report on basement storage, and a particular crow named Ashen who sits on the Thorngate bell tower and has opinions about everything that moves below. She did not announce this capability. She simply noted that her information quality improved considerably.

The vignette with Milores on the morning after Milores joined the crew is Tanenni at her most revealing. She slides the payment across the table -- sixty gold, minus expenses, accounting on the second page. Milores does not verify. Tanenni notes this, and notes that Milores has done her own calculation: four jobs from the deposit, at this rate. Tanenni understands immediately, because she understands ledgers, and a woman who has calculated the exact number of jobs between herself and a specific property is a woman keeping a ledger of her own. Milores asks about the cottage, unprompted: "Coastal. North-facing window. For the light." And then, a question that surprises Milores simply by being asked: "For painting?" Tanenni files this information completely, without judgment, "with the particular attention of someone who knew that the things people counted were the things that mattered to them." She makes a note in the ledger margin. She does not label it. She will know what it meant.

Her flaw is that she approaches people as administrative problems -- things with motives to map, access points to identify, vulnerabilities to log. She is working on this. Progress is slow. What she did not expect, in building the crew, was to find herself mildly invested in their success as people as well as as colleagues. She is still calibrating what to do about this.

## The Relationships

The six pairwise relationships within the crew are each drawn with the specificity of a group that communicates almost entirely through work.

**Bradkell and Darwhidan** are the crew's two social operators, and each knows the other is performing. This produces a specific ease: neither can be the other's mark, which means the version of themselves they present to each other is closer to actual than the version they present to anyone else. Their planning sessions are where the friction lives -- Bradkell builds his social approach around a model, three moves ahead, a theory of the mark and the room; Darwhidan reads the room and responds in real time, with intuitive accuracy that defies framework. When Darwhidan's instinct contradicts Bradkell's model mid-operation, the operation usually still works, but Bradkell cannot always explain why, and this bothers him. Darwhidan is aware it bothers him and finds it endearing. The tavern vignette at The Millrace captures this precisely: Bradkell presents a floor plan with two access points; Darwhidan identifies a third -- a second-floor window he spotted while a steward explained a painting. Bradkell recalculates rather than objects. "Tell me the what," he says. "I'll build the why."

**Bradkell and Milores** share a mutual respect built on legibility. His framework is elaborate but consistent; her code is simple but absolute. Each finds the other's decision-making process readable, and this matters more to both of them than agreement. The friction is operational: Bradkell occasionally wants to expand scope based on principle -- a document not on the list, an adjacent problem that is clearly right but was not in the plan -- and Milores objects not on moral grounds but on operational ones. Scope creep costs exit probability. On the rooftop before the Candlers' Row job, they recalculate together with professional efficiency -- a third guard, a two-minute loss on the extract, a cascade of timing adjustments -- and her four-word assessment of his plan carries a weight they both understand.

**Bradkell and Tanenni** are the crew's planning engine, and their working relationship most resembles a partnership, though neither would use the word. Their dynamic is mutual dependency expressed through precision: Tanenni produces intelligence, Bradkell converts it into plans, and the plans rely on the intelligence being correct. She does not exaggerate the confidence of her reports -- she says the animals reported rotation roughly hourly, the variance is unclear -- and this precision is worth a great deal to a planner who needs to know the difference. Their shared recognition that the city is a system with correctable imbalances -- his language political-economic, hers ecological -- produces different vocabulary for the same observation: weight in the wrong places.

**Darwhidan and Milores** are the crew's silent-approach pair, and the post-operation silence between them has a different quality than any other silence in the group. It is the silence of two people who have both been paying the right kind of attention during the work and do not need to debrief. The safe-house vignette after the Brevary job is built entirely from this silence and its small ruptures: she says "the second guard," and he says "I saw"; she counted three seconds on the stuck lock pin and he counted four, and this arithmetic of seconds and distances is their shared language. They recognise in each other a quality of physical precision that makes difficult things look effortless, and this recognition has never been spoken aloud. The friction is real but contained: he leaps through unnecessary windows; she logs this as a known variable. She leaves through the planned exit, at the planned time, without looking back. He remains at the window, thinking about the specific quality of silence between two people who have both been very good at something difficult on the same evening.

**Darwhidan and Tanenni** are the original pair, and their relationship is the crew's foundation. They met in the dark, over a strongbox, from opposite sides of a vault they had independently decided to rob on the same evening. The Harrowell vignette gives this origin the weight it deserves: two professionals assessing each other across a lit lamp in a twelve-foot stone room, each reading the other's posture, credentials, and intent in the same interval. She had been there twenty minutes and had already identified the decoy. She offered to split. He accepted. Three days later she taught him the first seven signs of the secret language she shares with the natural world, and he understood that the evening had been a beginning. They communicate in that private shorthand when operational security requires it -- he leaves scouting reports carved in city stonework using its symbols, she reads them and notes that his observational priorities are consistently correct. The debt from the Voss vault -- half the contents, three years outstanding -- functions as the visible surface of a more complicated question about what they owe each other and whether debt is the right framework. She knows what she is owed. She has a very good memory for ledgers. She has chosen not to call the balance due, which is itself a kind of information she has not explicitly provided and he has not explicitly asked about.

**Milores and Tanenni** share the specific quality of carrying private facts in a small group without those facts being drawn out. Milores has her paintings; Tanenni has her timeline toward whatever comes after the crew. Neither has been asked about these things by the other, and the parallel between their privacies has not been discussed, because it is not the kind of thing that gets discussed. Operationally, their handoff is the smoothest seam in the crew: Tanenni plans, Milores executes, and when the margins do not hold, Milores improvises in ways Tanenni can immediately track because they operate from the same underlying logic. The morning-after vignette is the scene that crystallises them: Tanenni's clean ledger, Milores's unverified payment, the four-word disclosure about the cottage, and Tanenni's single follow-up question -- "for painting?" -- which is the most personal thing either of them has said to the other, and both of them know it.

## The Narrative So Far

The crew's formation is a story told in stages, each stage adding a member and changing the shape of what the group can do.

Three years before the campaign begins, Tanenni and Darwhidan independently targeted Lord Aldren Voss, a textile magnate whose wealth derived from contract labour that was technically legal and comprehensively exploitative. They arrived at the vault from different entrances at approximately the same time. She came through the garden, past a ward that did not recognise someone moving with the rhythm of the garden's own growth. He came through the servants' corridor, past a guard redirected by a conjured sound placed precisely where it would draw attention away from the real approach. They met, split the contents, left through different exits, and began talking about partnership. She taught him her secret language. He began leaving scouting reports in city stonework. The intelligence-and-execution loop that would become the crew's operational core was established in a vault beneath a textile magnate's estate by two people who had not known each other existed an hour earlier.

Six months after the Voss job, Tanenni recruited Milores through Garren Thule, a fence and information broker at the Candlewick Tavern. Milores asked three questions: target profile, civilian exposure, pay structure. The answers were corrupt wealth, none, and equal shares. She accepted. She has since revised her opinion of the crew from "adequate professionals" to something she has not named but which is visible in the fact that she has not, at any point in two and a half years, looked for alternative employment.

Eighteen months before the campaign, Bradkell found the crew through the Sullow Quarter's underworld networks. He had heard that a crew was targeting guild-connected wealth with precision that suggested planning rather than opportunism. He attended one meeting, determined three things -- competent, correct targets, structural gap in the social operation -- and filled the gap. Before Bradkell, the crew's operations were infiltration and extraction. After Bradkell, they included the front-door entry, the manufactured invitation, the conversation that produces access to a building without anyone needing to climb through a window.

Seven major operations in three years. The Voss vault. The Solenne ledgers, redistributed to three broadsheet publishers. The Harrowgate customs house payroll diversion, redirected to the Sullow Compact. The second Corvane operation, Darwhidan's return visit, which drove the duke from the city. Three additional operations of similar profile. No civilian casualties. No crew injuries beyond the manageable. No identification by city authorities. The operational security is excellent, which is to say Tanenni's operational security is excellent, and the others have the discipline to maintain it.

The crew operates from safe houses in the Sullow Quarter and the Bridgeway. Bradkell routes his share to the Compact. Milores banks hers toward the cottage. Darwhidan spends his on tailoring, equipment, and the lifestyle maintenance required to move in noble circles. Tanenni reinvests in the operation -- intelligence acquisition, documentation infrastructure, and the bribes that keep certain officials looking in directions that are not toward the crew.

## The Open Threads

Eight dormant threads, each tied to a character or to the crew's collective exposure:

1. **The Sullow Compact and the Warden-Inspectors.** Bradkell's mutual aid network is funded by the crew's earnings. The Guild's enforcement arm is increasing pressure on unlicensed charitable activity. The intersection of these facts has not yet produced a crisis, but the geometry is clear. This is Bradkell's war, and it is approaching.

2. **The Temple of Measured Light.** Tanenni's twelve-percent rerouting has been running for years. High Archivist Solen Drath is either oblivious or patient. Her access credentials remain valid, which is either an oversight or an invitation. She has not determined which.

3. **The Mael-Arath family.** Darwhidan's celestial heritage and his parents' expectations are unresolved. His mother is a senior officiant at a major temple. His father administers its endowment. They know what he has become. They have stopped writing. The silence is its own kind of thread.

4. **The Blackshore Company.** Milores walked out two and a half years ago. Captain Horst recovered. Whether anyone in the company is still looking for her is a question she checks periodically, and the answer has been "not actively" for some time, which is not the same as "no."

5. **Edric Bradkell.** Bradkell's father is alive, in the Sullow Quarter, unaware that his son funds the network that now provides what the brewery used to provide informally. Whether this remains the case is Bradkell's decision, and he has not made it.

6. **The cottage.** Four jobs. Approximately one year. What happens when the number reaches zero -- whether Milores stays, whether she goes, whether the crew can function without her -- is the question none of them have raised and all of them have considered.

7. **Darwhidan's debt to Tanenni.** Half the Voss vault. Three years outstanding. The amount is known to both. The reason it has not been called due is known to neither, or to both, and the distinction matters.

8. **The Reedmen.** Seven operations, all targeting the same category of wealth, all executed with the same operational signature. Inspector Cerani Whist, lead investigator for financial crimes, has noted that the victims share a profile and that the methodology suggests a consistent intelligence source. She is working the problem. The crew does not know this.

## What Holds the Crew Together

Three things make this group functional despite four fundamentally different moral philosophies pulling in four different directions.

**A shared destination, arrived at from incompatible starting points.** They all concluded, independently, that the specific category of wealth the crew targets is the correct category. Bradkell came to it through political theory. Darwhidan came to it through aesthetics. Milores came to it through scope evaluation -- civilians out, extractive wealth in. Tanenni came to it through observation of a system out of balance. These different routes produce the same answer most of the time. When they do not, the disagreement is real, but it is about reasons, not results, and this distinction has so far been manageable.

**Complementary capabilities with no overlap.** There is no redundancy. Tanenni covers intelligence and the reading of magical defences. Darwhidan covers physical infiltration and the social performance that gets him through a door unchallenged. Milores covers neutralisation and the kind of precise, quiet violence that ends a confrontation before it begins. Bradkell covers the conversation that opens the front door and the magic that controls a room when the conversation fails. Each is irreplaceable in their domain, which means no one is competing for a role, which means internal friction is almost entirely about values and almost never about positioning.

**Competence as the primary respect language.** None of them arrived at their expertise through institutional channels. Tanenni's religious authority was self-created. Darwhidan's court access was stolen. Milores's fighting skill was developed in the field under conditions that were not comfortable. Bradkell's social intelligence was built on the streets of the industrial quarter against an audience that would not have been kind to failure. They all know what real competence looks like, and they all see it in each other. This is not the same as liking each other, but it is more durable.

## What Makes Them Distinctive

This crew's defining quality is the specific absence at its centre. There is no one in the group whose primary mode is openness. Tanenni does not share because openness does not serve the operational mode. Darwhidan does not share because self-disclosure is a vulnerability. Milores does not share because there is not much to say. Bradkell does not share because the role he performs requires maintained distance. They are not unkind to each other. They are simply not, in the ordinary social sense, easy with each other. The understanding they have is real. It is just expressed almost entirely through work.

The drama of this party is not conflict in the conventional sense. It is the slow, episodic realisation -- through a series of decisions where the right action is obvious but the reason for it diverges -- that the people you work well with are not necessarily the people you understand, and that working well together over time produces a kind of trust that is harder to walk away from than it is to enter. Milores's cottage arithmetic is counting down. Tanenni did not expect to become invested in these people. Darwhidan has not examined whether the crew has become something more than a professional arrangement, and the question, from the right person at the right moment, will get past his deflections. Bradkell, who reads everyone else in the room, has not yet been asked to read himself.

They are four people who agree completely on the what and the how, and who have arrived at that agreement from starting points that have almost nothing in common. The tension is latent rather than active. It becomes active when a complication arrives that their frameworks would resolve differently. And in the city of Corraith, where the weight pools at the top and the Reedmen are working the problem and the cottage is four jobs away, complications are not theoretical. They are three weeks out, and the Aldworth estate is next.
