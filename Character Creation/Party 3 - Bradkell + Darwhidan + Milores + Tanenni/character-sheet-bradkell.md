## 5. Features & Traits

### Class Features *(Sorcerer)*

**Spellcasting**
You can cast Sorcerer spells using **Charisma** as your spellcasting ability. You know a set number of spells and can cast them using spell slots. Your prepared spells are fixed — you cannot swap them after a Long Rest without levelling up.

**Innate Sorcery**
As a **Bonus Action**, you can unleash the sorcerous power within yourself for 1 minute. During this time:
- Your **Spell Save DC increases by 1** (to DC 14).
- You have **Advantage on all Sorcerer spell attack rolls**.
You can use this feature **2 times per Long Rest**.

*Key coordination note: Activate Innate Sorcery before casting Sleep (to raise the DC to 14) or before Sorcerous Burst (for Advantage on the attack roll). Do not waste it on cantrips when a levelled spell can follow in the same turn or the next.*

### Species Traits *(Human)*

**Resourceful**
You gain **Heroic Inspiration** whenever you finish a Long Rest.

**Skillful**
You gain proficiency in one additional skill: **Survival** *(chosen to reflect field-campaign experience as a street organiser)*.

**Versatile**
You gain one Origin feat: **Musician** *(Human bonus feat; see below)*.

### Origin Feats

**Tavern Brawler** *(Rebel background)*
- Your Unarmed Strike uses a **d4** for damage and adds your STR modifier.
- Once per turn when you hit a creature with an Unarmed Strike, you can push the creature **5 feet** away from you.
- You gain proficiency with **Improvised Weapons**.

**Musician** *(Human — Versatile)*
You are proficient with three musical instruments of your choice: **Drum, Flute, and Shawm**. During a Short or Long Rest, you can play music for the party. Up to a number of allies equal to your **Proficiency Bonus (2)** who can hear you gain **Heroic Inspiration** when the rest ends. *Allies already carrying Heroic Inspiration don't benefit.*

*Action | V, S, M (a pinch of sand or rose petals) | Concentration, up to 1 minute.*

## 8. Notes & Backstory

### Appearance

Bradkell is a study in deliberate misdirection. He is built heavy — a lifetime of physical labour first in the breweries of the industrial quarter and then in the street protests outside them has left him with the broad shoulders and thick hands of manual work, and he has kept this appearance rather than moving toward anything that would signal arrival or success. He dresses in rough traveller's clothes, perpetually somewhat dusty, occasionally smelling faintly of fermented grain. He looks, in most company, like a very capable labourer who has wandered into somewhere slightly above his station and is comfortable enough with it not to be nervous.

When he speaks, the misdirection arrives: his voice carries a natural resonance that fills a room without effort, and his eyes are sharp with an intelligence that has been quietly cataloguing everything in the space for the last ten minutes. His charisma is not the polished kind — it is the compelling kind, the kind that makes people think they have independently decided to agree with him. He has found this significantly more useful than a pressed collar.

He plays the drum at camp, the flute when thinking, and the shawm at parties where someone needs the room's attention pulled away from something. He is better at all three than he appears.

### Personality Traits

- **Magnetic.** He doesn't just lie — he makes people want to believe him. He treats social encounters like dragonchess: always three moves ahead, always identifying which piece the other player thinks they're winning when they give up the space he actually wanted.
- **Understated.** He deliberately maintains the rough appearance because the city's elite discard what they classify as low-status before it opens its mouth. He finds a particular satisfaction in being the most informed person in a room full of nobles who assume he is there to refill their wine.

### Ideal

**The System.** He is not personally angry, particularly. He has moved past the phase of being angry. He is now in the phase of being precise: he wants specific vaults emptied, specific documents redistributed, specific channels of accumulation interrupted in ways that are permanent and structural. He steals not because he hates the people he robs, but because the system that made them wealthy depends on the fiction that what they hold is secure, and he is in the business of discrediting fictions.

### Bond

The industrial quarter, specifically the three streets where he grew up. He funds the mutual aid network there from the crew's earnings — quietly, through intermediaries, in amounts that are real but not traceable. No one in those streets knows it comes from him. He prefers it that way.

### Flaw

He trusts his own read on people more than is always warranted. He is excellent at reading what people want to believe; he is somewhat weaker at reading what people are actually about to do. These are different skills. In social encounters he is exceptional. In high-pressure moments where someone is frightened and therefore unpredictable, he has occasionally gotten the read wrong in ways that cost him.

### Backstory

#### Origin

Bradkell was born in the soot and noise of the city's industrial quarter, the fourth son of a brewer whose business ran into guild enforcement for the third time and did not survive it. He grew up understanding, with the practical clarity of someone who has to, how power moves and where it pools — in guild houses, in charter fees, in the quiet meetings between men whose names never appeared on any ledger but whose decisions appeared in every consequence.

He became a Rebel in the literal sense at fourteen: a member, then a coordinator, then a planner of the small, technically illegal networks that provided what the guilds had priced out of reach. Not a revolutionary in any grand sense — he was not interested in revolution, which requires a theory of what comes after, and he was a young man with a practical orientation. He was interested in the specific guild-house across the street, and what it would take to deprive it of the political capital it used to maintain the situation in his streets.

#### The Spark

The sorcerous ability arrived during a guild-hall riot when he was twenty-two. A guard tried to seize him — the ordinary grab-and-drag of a city authority managing an inconvenient protest — and the air between them crackled. The guard was thrown back six feet. The riot scattered. Bradkell stood in the empty street afterwards and noted, carefully, that something significant had happened to him, and began thinking about its applications.

He spent the next two years teaching himself arcane theory from whatever texts he could access — legitimately borrowed, occasionally borrowed without asking, once taken directly from the shelf of a guild-arcanist who he was meeting under false pretenses and left a very confused interview having lost a book he was fairly sure he'd had with him when he arrived. Bradkell learned the structure of magical security systems, how wards are keyed, how alarms are triggered, and more usefully, how their operators assume they will be circumvented. They assume they will be circumvented by someone who understands magic well and people poorly. He is the opposite.

#### The Crew

He found Tanenni's operation through the kind of mutual acquaintance that the city's underworld produces — the person who knows someone who knows someone who handles work of a particular type. He attended one meeting to assess the crew and determined three things: they were competent, they were targeting the right people, and they had a structural gap in their social operation that he could fill.

He joined because the mathematics of the crew's approach allowed for real, structural damage to the kind of accumulation he had spent his adult life opposing. He is not sentimental about his colleagues. He respects Tanenni's precision and trusts her judgment on operations. He finds Darwhidan entertaining and occasionally alarming. He has enormous, genuine respect for Milores, whose professional code is the most legible thing about her and is, he has concluded, the most reliable framework in the group.

He plays music at Short Rests because the crew performs better after it, which he tracks as an operational metric, and also because he finds it a reasonable way to spend twenty minutes, which he does not tell anyone.
