## 5. Features & Traits

### Class Features *(Sorcerer)*

**Spellcasting**
You can cast Sorcerer spells using **Charisma** as your spellcasting ability. You know a set number of spells and can cast them using spell slots. Your prepared spells are fixed — you cannot swap them after a Long Rest without levelling up.

**Innate Sorcery**
As a **Bonus Action**, you can unleash the sorcerous power within yourself for 1 minute. During this time:
- Your **Spell Save DC increases by 1** (to DC 14).
- You have **Advantage on all Sorcerer spell attack rolls**.
You can use this feature **2 times per Long Rest**.

*Key coordination note: Activate Innate Sorcery before casting Sleep (to raise the DC to 14) or before Sorcerous Burst (for Advantage on the attack roll). Do not waste it on cantrips when a levelled spell can follow in the same turn or the next.*

### Species Traits *(Human)*

**Resourceful**
You gain **Heroic Inspiration** whenever you finish a Long Rest.

**Skillful**

## 8. Notes & Backstory

### Appearance

Bradkell is a study in deliberate misdirection. He is built heavy — a lifetime of physical labour first in the breweries of the industrial quarter and then in the street protests outside them has left him with the broad shoulders and thick hands of manual work, and he has kept this appearance rather than moving toward anything that would signal arrival or success. He dresses in rough traveller's clothes, perpetually somewhat dusty, occasionally smelling faintly of fermented grain. He looks, in most company, like a very capable labourer who has wandered into somewhere slightly above his station and is comfortable enough with it not to be nervous.

When he speaks, the misdirection arrives: his voice carries a natural resonance that fills a room without effort, and his eyes are sharp with an intelligence that has been quietly cataloguing everything in the space for the last ten minutes. His charisma is not the polished kind — it is the compelling kind, the kind that makes people think they have independently decided to agree with him. He has found this significantly more useful than a pressed collar.

He plays the drum at camp, the flute when thinking, and the shawm at parties where someone needs the room's attention pulled away from something. He is better at all three than he appears.

### Personality Traits

- **Magnetic.** He doesn't just lie — he makes people want to believe him. He treats social encounters like dragonchess: always three moves ahead, always identifying which piece the other player thinks they're winning when they give up the space he actually wanted.
- **Understated.** He deliberately maintains the rough appearance because the city's elite discard what they classify as low-status before it opens its mouth. He finds a particular satisfaction in being the most informed person in a room full of nobles who assume he is there to refill their wine.

### Ideal

**The System.** He is not personally angry, particularly. He has moved past the phase of being angry. He is now in the phase of being precise: he wants specific vaults emptied, specific documents redistributed, specific channels of accumulation interrupted in ways that are permanent and structural. He steals not because he hates the people he robs, but because the system that made them wealthy depends on the fiction that what they hold is secure, and he is in the business of discrediting fictions.

### Bond

The industrial quarter, specifically the three streets where he grew up. He funds the mutual aid network there from the crew's earnings — quietly, through intermediaries, in amounts that are real but not traceable. No one in those streets knows it comes from him. He prefers it that way.

### Flaw

He trusts his own read on people more than is always warranted. He is excellent at reading what people want to believe; he is somewhat weaker at reading what people are actually about to do. These are different skills. In social encounters he is exceptional. In high-pressure moments where someone is frightened and therefore unpredictable, he has occasionally gotten the read wrong in ways that cost him.

### Backstory

#### Origin

Bradkell was born in the soot and noise of the city's industrial quarter, the fourth son of a brewer whose business ran into guild enforcement for the third time and did not survive it. He grew up understanding, with the practical clarity of someone who has to, how power moves and where it pools — in guild houses, in charter fees, in the quiet meetings between men whose names never appeared on any ledger but whose decisions appeared in every consequence.

He became a Rebel in the literal sense at fourteen: a member, then a coordinator, then a planner of the small, technically illegal networks that provided what the guilds had priced out of reach. Not a revolutionary in any grand sense — he was not interested in revolution, which requires a theory of what comes after, and he was a young man with a practical orientation. He was interested in the specific guild-house across the street, and what it would take to deprive it of the political capital it used to maintain the situation in his streets.

#### The Spark

The sorcerous ability arrived during a guild-hall riot when he was twenty-two. A guard tried to seize him — the ordinary grab-and-drag of a city authority managing an inconvenient protest — and the air between them crackled. The guard was thrown back six feet. The riot scattered. Bradkell stood in the empty street afterwards and noted, carefully, that something significant had happened to him, and began thinking about its applications.

He spent the next two years teaching himself arcane theory from whatever texts he could access — legitimately borrowed, occasionally borrowed without asking, once taken directly from the shelf of a guild-arcanist who he was meeting under false pretenses and left a very confused interview having lost a book he was fairly sure he'd had with him when he arrived. Bradkell learned the structure of magical security systems, how wards are keyed, how alarms are triggered, and more usefully, how their operators assume they will be circumvented. They assume they will be circumvented by someone who understands magic well and people poorly. He is the opposite.

#### The Crew

He found Tanenni's operation through the kind of mutual acquaintance that the city's underworld produces — the person who knows someone who knows someone who handles work of a particular type. He attended one meeting to assess the crew and determined three things: they were competent, they were targeting the right people, and they had a structural gap in their social operation that he could fill.

He joined because the mathematics of the crew's approach allowed for real, structural damage to the kind of accumulation he had spent his adult life opposing. He is not sentimental about his colleagues. He respects Tanenni's precision and trusts her judgment on operations. He finds Darwhidan entertaining and occasionally alarming. He has enormous, genuine respect for Milores, whose professional code is the most legible thing about her and is, he has concluded, the most reliable framework in the group.

He plays music at Short Rests because the crew performs better after it, which he tracks as an operational metric, and also because he finds it a reasonable way to spend twenty minutes, which he does not tell anyone.

## 8. Notes & Backstory

### Appearance

Darwhidan does not look like a celestial herald. He is built like a tavern bouncer of particular quality — broad-chested and thick-necked, with the angular ruggedness of a face that has been in a few disagreements and emerged with its structure intact. His celestial heritage is there if you look for it: metallic gold irises that catch light in a way ordinary eyes don't, a faint warmth that radiates from his skin in cold rooms, and the overall impression that something significant lives behind the ordinary arrangement of his features. He has cultivated a careful nonchalance about all of it.

He moves through high society in impeccably tailored fine clothes that stretch comfortably over his heavy frame — he looks, in the right context, like a very expensive and lightly intimidating bodyguard to someone important. This is useful. Nobody searches the bodyguard. Nobody follows the bodyguard's hands. His hands, which look as though they belong wrapped around a greatsword, can pick a deadbolt in three seconds in total darkness, or slip a lord's signet ring off during a handshake while shaking the lord's hand, while maintaining direct eye contact, while asking about the lord's horses.

He carries the prop notebook — a convincing *Book of Arcane Formulae* — in his coat pocket. Several people have expressed admiration for it. He accepts this graciously. It contains three old letters, a shopping list, and forty-seven pages of his own notes on estate security layouts.

### Personality Traits

- **Effortlessly credible.** He understands that people see the broad shoulders and assign a category, and he uses the category against them with genuine, sustained pleasure. He plays the affable, slightly dim-witted court enforcer right up until the vault is empty and the window is three stories below him.
- **Gravity is a negotiating position.** He has a thrill-seeker's specific relationship with heights. Why take stairs when you can leap from a third-storey window, turn to your colleagues mid-fall, say something appropriate, and cast Feather Fall at approximately the last moment that counts as not too late?

### Ideal

**The Game.** He has no grand theory of justice. He has a finely calibrated set of opinions about whose vaults deserve to be empty, and a genuine and detailed enjoyment of the process of emptying them. If the targets happen to be people who have done harm with their resources, that is satisfying. It is not the primary motivation.

### Bond

He owes Tanenni precisely half of the contents of a particular vault from an evening three years ago. He has not paid this debt. He is not sure she has noticed. He is fairly sure she has noticed. The uncertainty is part of what makes her interesting.

### Flaw

He has very poor Wisdom, and he knows it, and he has compensated for this by becoming very good at reading rooms — which is a Charisma skill, not a Wisdom skill, and the difference matters in certain situations. He misses things that should be obvious. He will notice that an earl is nervous; he will not notice the guard outside the door the earl is nervous about.

### Backstory

#### Origin

Darwhidan was born into a family of Aasimar whose celestial heritage had sharpened over generations into genuine religious conviction. They expected, with the quiet certainty of people who have already decided, that he would become a paladin or a cleric — that the celestial fire in his lineage would express itself as devotion, as duty, as some form of armoured service to a cause larger than himself.

He was instead sent, at nineteen, to serve as a guard and attaché in the courts of the city's highest nobility: a posting meant to season him before taking holy vows. He found the courts extremely interesting, though not in the way intended. He spent his first year mapping estate layouts, studying patrol rotations, and developing a precise understanding of where the valuables were relative to where the guards weren't. He spent his second year teaching himself Mage Hand from a borrowed text, practicing Minor Illusion until he could place sounds precisely enough to make a guard look at exactly the wrong door at exactly the right moment, and identifying which nobles had the best locks and the most to hide.

The celestial fire expressed itself, in the end, as a conviction that the people in these houses had accumulated far more than they were owed, and as a practical, technical solution to that imbalance.

#### The Duke's Estate

The specific event that ended his court career was the complete removal of the contents of a corrupt duke's private vault — securities, correspondence of the sort that becomes actionable when shared, and a collection of objects whose chain of acquisition bore close examination. He walked through the servants' entrance, past two guards who were looking at the wrong door, through a locked vault that required thirty seconds of attention, out through a window on the third floor, and down with the assistance of a small feather and basic arcane physics.

He sent the correspondence to three separate parties who would find it useful. He kept the gold.

He has not been back to the court. His religious order, to which he never actually took vows, sent two letters. He did not reply to either.

#### Tanenni

He met Tanenni in the dark, over a strongbox, from opposite sides of a vault they had independently decided to rob on the same evening. He considered the situation for a moment — she was a Dragonborn in temple robes holding a Sickle, which was not a combination he had prepared scenarios for — and she offered to split it. He accepted.

She subsequently taught him Druidic, which he uses to leave scouting reports in carved runes on city stonework in places that guards walk past without looking at. He considers this one of the most practically useful skills he possesses. She considers it operational security. They have not discussed whether this constitutes the beginning of a debt or its repayment. He suspects she knows exactly which it is.

## 8. Notes & Backstory

### Appearance

Milores is physically imposing in the particular way of someone who has done heavy work for a long time and was not slight to begin with. Her skin is the deep, scorched crimson of cooling forge-iron, and her horns are obsidian black, curving tightly over the crown of her head in a shape that her former company used to joke about, until they saw her fight and stopped finding it funny. Her arms and shoulders carry the dense, functional muscle of a career soldier, crisscrossed with faded white scars she has never bothered to explain and does not consider significant.

She dresses in dark canvas reinforced with leather — practical, utilitarian, nothing that will catch on a windowsill or restrict a kick. When she is irritated or preparing to fight, a faint smell of sulfur enters the air and wisps of smoke occasionally curl from her nostrils, a Tiefling tell she stopped trying to suppress years ago. She considers it adequately intimidating without any effort.

What her appearance does not suggest: at every point of rest, her calloused fingertips are stained with paint. Cadmium yellow at the moment. She is working on a series of cliff-edge coastal landscapes based on a drawing she made from memory of a place she visited once and intends to return to, when the current situation is concluded.

### Personality Traits

- **Disciplined.** She rarely speaks unless she has something worth saying. She treats violence as a technical problem — the most efficient path between a situation and its resolution — and applies the same focused, dispassionate attention to it that she gives to mixing paint or assessing a patrol rotation.
- **The Artist.** She finds her life noisy, unpredictable, and aesthetically chaotic. She paints to find the inverse of that. On stakeouts, before vault approaches, in the forty minutes between arrival and action, she will calmly remove the small case from her belt and work on something careful and beautiful, which is not a performance of calm but an actual production of it.

### Ideal

**The Code.** She has a strict personal framework. It is not a legal framework, or a moral framework in any conventional sense — it is a professional one. Certain things are within scope; certain things are outside it. Hurting civilians is outside it. Harming people who cannot defend themselves is outside it. Everything else is a technical question. She applies this code with the consistency of arithmetic.

### Bond

She owes the cottage. That is the entire bond: a specific piece of property on a specific section of coast, with a north-facing window good for painting in the afternoon light, and enough quiet to actually use it. Every job brings it closer. This is not sentiment. It is a project plan.

### Flaw

She is not good at explaining herself. She makes decisions she considers correct and then does not narrate them, which means that people around her occasionally experience her actions as opaque or alarming. She is aware that communication is a skill. She considers it lower-priority than her other skills. This assessment has not yet caused a catastrophic problem, but it will.

### Backstory

#### Origin

Milores grew up in the lower city, the daughter of a Tiefling metalworker whose craft discipline she inherited more directly than she recognised at the time. She was recruited into a mercenary company at sixteen — young, but large for her age and demonstrably capable of things her size suggested, plus things it didn't — and spent the following decade becoming very good at a specific type of work: infiltration of defended positions, silent neutralisation of guards, and the breaking of defensive formations from inside them by someone who had no business being inside them.

She was exceptional at this. The company recognised it, which meant she was given the difficult jobs, which meant she had a great deal of practice at the difficult jobs, which meant she became more exceptional. This cycle had a natural ending: eventually the difficult jobs will include one you will not do.

#### The Incident

Her company took a contract to drive out a target — a minor lord who had wronged a larger lord — by making his holdings untenable. The method chosen by her commanding officer was to burn the village that provided his labour base. The officer's logic, which Milores understood and did not accept, was that displacing the civilians permanently solved the problem without requiring the risk of a direct assault.

She walked into the officer's tent. She gave him one opportunity to reconsider. He did not reconsider. She beat him with sufficient thoroughness to remove him from the remainder of the operation, left him outside the tent for his second-in-command to manage, and walked out of the camp. She took nothing that wasn't already hers.

She does not describe this as a crisis of conscience. She describes it as her professional code encountering a situation that fell outside acceptable parameters, and the appropriate response to that situation. She is not emotionally troubled by the memory. She is occasionally annoyed by the consequences, which included eighteen months of having to check her surroundings more carefully than usual.

#### The Underworld

Hunted by her former company and lacking the kind of references that get a person legitimate work, she disappeared into the urban underworld and found employment as an enforcer for gambling houses and private interests that needed disputes settled quietly. She developed a reputation for efficiency and precision — she rarely broke anything she wasn't paid to break, and she never broke anything twice.

Tanenni's crew found her through a mutual contact and made an offer: one high-value job per season, clean targets, professional crew, no civilians. She agreed because the mathematics worked. The coastal cottage is a real place at a real price, and the crew's payouts have made it a realistic near-future acquisition rather than an eventual abstract one.

She is four jobs away from the deposit.

#### The Painting

She started painting during her first year with the mercenary company, on the recommendation of a Halfling camp surgeon who had noticed she didn't sleep well and suggested making something with her hands before trying to sleep. The surgeon was correct. She has continued.

The current series is coastal landscapes. She has seven completed and is working on the eighth. She will hang them in the cottage when the cottage exists. Until then, she carries the smaller completed ones rolled in a leather tube at the bottom of her pack, which she does not mention to her colleagues and which none of them have yet commented on, though she is aware they have noticed.

## 8. Notes & Backstory

### Appearance

Tanenni is a Brass Dragonborn who has spent a great deal of care on being imperceptible in the places that count. Her scales are the warm tarnished gold of old candlesticks — kept meticulously clean, which in a working city reads less as vanity and more as religious fastidiousness, which is precisely the point. She is built on the lean, compact lines of someone who has spent years in high-ceilinged temple rooms and narrow archive passages, and she moves with the measured deliberateness of a woman who has learned that urgency attracts attention.

Over her hide armour she wears temple robes in layered deep blues and burnished gold — the colours of no specific god but the colours of *a* god, and in the right light and the right doorway that is usually sufficient. The holy symbol on its chain is thick enough to be seen from across a garden party. Her Quarterstaff doubles as a walking staff, which doubles as a Druidic focus, which doubles as something that can break a jaw with two hands and adequate motivation.

Her eyes, when she chooses to activate Thaumaturgy, shift to bright amber with vertical pupils. She deploys this rarely, and only when she wants to be remembered. Most of the time, she prefers not to be.

### Personality Traits

- **Meticulous.** She treats heist plans with the same rigorous, detail-oriented scrutiny she once applied to sacred texts. There is no room for imprecision — she has seen what imprecision costs, and what it costs is usually someone else's problem becoming hers.
- **Pragmatic.** She views the city's accumulated elite wealth as a stagnant pool: unhealthy for the ecosystem, correctable by redistribution. This is not ideology. It is ecology.

### Ideal

**Balance.** The city's systems are not broken because evil people exist. They are broken because weight has accumulated in the wrong places for a very long time, and the people holding it have confused weight for worth. She is in the business of redistributing load.

### Bond

The temple where she served for twelve years still considers her a senior acolyte on administrative leave. Her access credentials remain valid. She has not corrected the impression.

### Flaw

She is not good at people. Animals, yes — she reads them clearly, speaks to them honestly, and trusts them implicitly. People she approaches as administrative problems: things with motives to map, access points to identify, vulnerabilities to log. She is working on this. Progress is slow.

### Backstory

#### Origin

Tanenni was raised in the incense-and-marble silence of a prominent city temple, assigned to the archive and records office at sixteen because she had neat scales and neater handwriting. She spent eight years copying donation records, correspondence, and the internal accounts that paid for the temple's charitable works — and four more years quietly realising that the charitable works were a very small percentage of what the accounts showed coming in.

The temple's private reliquaries, she eventually understood, functioned primarily as a secure storage and laundering facility for the city's aristocracy. Illicit goods moved through blessed vaults. Tax obligations dissolved in religious exemption clauses. Blackmail documents rested in alcoves consecrated to saints of wisdom. The divine sanctity was real; it had simply been redirected.

Tanenni did not expose this. Exposure would have destroyed the temple's genuine charitable work along with the corruption, would have provided no lasting structural change, and would have made her name mud in every institution she might need access to later. Instead she quietly rerouted approximately twelve percent of the skimmed surplus back into the charitable accounts, altered the relevant ledger entries with the calligraphic skill she had been practising her entire adult life, and began making herself genuinely useful to people with resources worth redistributing.

#### The Druid

The druidic connection arrived late — she was twenty-nine, which is not late for a Dragonborn but is not early either. She had always been competent with the herbalism kit, attentive to the temple's sacred groves, more interested in the garden's annual rhythms than in the liturgical calendar that governed them. She does not describe the moment of recognition in mystical terms. She was repotting a bay laurel, noted that she understood something about the plant's intention, and accepted this as new information.

She found the Druidic circle that operated on the city's outskirts, learned the language and the practice, and incorporated both into her temple work in ways that were not obviously distinguishable from standard religious observance. The animals she subsequently communicated with became her most reliable informants about the movement of guards, the presence of unfamiliar people, and the general texture of a building's inhabited spaces. She did not announce this capability. She simply noted that her information quality improved considerably.

#### The Crew

She encountered Darwhidan when a heist she was running independently crossed paths with one he was running from the other direction, which is to say they robbed the same lord's vault on the same night from different entrances and met in the dark over the same strongbox. She considered him professionally for a moment and offered to split it. He accepted. She has since taught him Druidic for use in leaving field-reports in city stonework, which she considers a reasonable investment in operational security.

The others she recruited or accepted as they arrived. The crew has the right shape for the work: someone who can move in silence, someone who can talk their way through a front door, someone who can carry the rough work. What she did not expect was to find herself mildly invested in their success as people as well as as colleagues. She is still calibrating what to do about this.
# Party Synergy — Strategic Analysis

---

## Overview

The crew of Bradkell (Sorcerer), Darwhidan (Rogue), Milores (Monk), and Tanenni (Druid) presents a distinctive tactical profile: **no heavy armour, no dedicated healer, and a full suite of stealth-viable movement options.** Unlike Party 1's single-frontliner configuration and Party 2's double-armoured front rank, this party fights at medium-to-close range, with Milores as the durable anchor and three supporting members who contribute precise, well-positioned pressure rather than sustained hits. The key insight is that this party's power comes not from combat buffs stacked on frontliners but from **action economy** — Darwhidan's Nick mastery freeing his Bonus Action, Milores's free Bonus Action unarmed strike, Bradkell's Sleep converting an encounter before it starts, and Tanenni's Healing Word recovering a party member without spending her action. Every character contributes on multiple axes simultaneously. The party is weakest in head-on sustained combat against multiple resilient enemies; it is strongest when it controls the terms of engagement.

---

## Combat Synergy Chains

### Chain 1: The Clean Takedown — Bradkell's Sleep + Darwhidan's Sneak Attack

The party's single most efficient combat sequence, and the correct approach to any encounter involving isolated human-type guards:

**Step 1 — Bradkell: Sleep** (WIS save DC 13, 5-ft Sphere, two-stage Incapacitated → Unconscious) → Creatures that fail both saves fall Unconscious. Note: the Sphere is 5 feet — position the centre between tightly-clustered targets for maximum effect.

**Step 2 — Darwhidan: Melee attack vs. Unconscious target** → Attacks against Unconscious creatures from within 5 feet are **automatic critical hits**. A critical hit with Sneak Attack at Level 1 is **4d6+3** damage, which is likely lethal on a standard guard (assuming ~10 HP) and guaranteed to produce the deepest possible wound on anything.

**Step 3 — Milores (if a second target remains):** Unarmed Strike + Bonus Action Unarmed Strike → clean, bludgeoning, subdual-appropriate.

The cost: one of Bradkell's two Level 1 spell slots. The return: an encounter resolved without alarm, without noise, and without the kind of wound pattern that creates subsequent complications. This is the party's standard operating procedure for guarded spaces.

**Coordination rule:** Bradkell identifies the target cluster before combat. Darwhidan positions within 5 feet of where the nearest target will fall. The sequence runs as: Bradkell casts → wait for both fails to resolve → Darwhidan walks to the nearest unconscious target and takes the auto-crit → done. Do not have Darwhidan attack before Sleep's first-save Incapacitation resolves — the auto-crit requires the Unconscious condition, which doesn't arrive until the second failed save at the end of the target's next turn.

---

### Chain 2: The Milores Anchor — Milores in Melee + Darwhidan Sneak Attack Trigger

The party's most reliable sustained-combat damage chain, and the default when Sleep is unavailable or inappropriate:

**Milores engages a target in melee.** She is within 5 feet of the enemy.

**Darwhidan attacks the same target with a Finesse or Ranged weapon.** The Rogue Sneak Attack condition: *another enemy of the target is within 5 feet*. Milores fulfils this condition permanently while engaged. Darwhidan gets Sneak Attack **every round** against any target Milores is fighting, regardless of his own positioning, with no need for Advantage.

The result: Milores contributes ~10 damage per round (Unarmed + Bonus Unarmed). Darwhidan contributes ~12 damage per round (Shortsword + Nick Dagger + Sneak Attack 1d6). Against a single target, that is approximately **22 damage per round** from two characters, without using any limited resources.

**Call the target.** Both should announce the same target at the start of the round. Milores strikes first to establish adjacency; Darwhidan follows. If Milores is attacking Target A and Darwhidan is attacking Target B, the chain does not function. The party's best combat rounds are the ones where all physical attacks point at a single priority.

---

### Chain 3: The Fog Repositioning — Tanenni's Fog Cloud + Darkvision Crew

**Tanenni: Fog Cloud** (Concentration, 20-ft sphere) → All creatures in the sphere are Heavily Obscured. Attack rolls against targets in the sphere have Disadvantage. Attack rolls made by targets in the sphere have Disadvantage.

Three of the four crew members have Darkvision (60 ft). The important clarification: **Darkvision does not pierce magical Fog Cloud.** The fog creates Heavy Obscurement regardless of light — it is not a darkness effect, it is a visibility effect. However, a creature with Darkvision who cannot *see* through the fog still benefits from the *tactical cover* it provides: enemies targeting the crew from outside the fog have Disadvantage on attacks into the Heavily Obscured area, even if the crew is moving within it.

**The practical use:** Fog Cloud placed on a doorway, corridor junction, or enemy ranged position creates a Disadvantage zone on all attacks originating from that area. The crew can advance behind it, using it as mobile cover. Bradkell, who has no Darkvision and AC 10, should move *behind* the cloud rather than through it — the cloud screens incoming fire while he operates just outside it.

**Coordination rule:** Before casting Fog Cloud, identify where Bradkell is relative to the cloud's position. He cannot operate inside it usefully. Position the sphere to block the enemy's line of sight on him while leaving his own line of sight clear. If this is not possible, Fog Cloud may be the wrong choice for this encounter.

---

### Chain 4: The Guidance Check — Tanenni + Pre-Roll Buff

**Tanenni: Guidance** (Concentration, 1 minute) → touches Darwhidan (or any crew member) → **Tanenni chooses a specific skill at cast time**; the target adds 1d4 to any ability check using that skill for up to a minute.

Darwhidan's Thieves' Tools check (Sleight of Hand +7 + 1d4 Guidance) is one of the most reliable mechanical operations in the game at Level 1. Most DC 15 locks become trivially accessible. DC 20 locks become reliably accessible. On a critical operation where the lock is known to be exceptional, this combination is worth using.

Guidance also applies to:
- **Bradkell's Persuasion (+5 + 1d4)** in a high-stakes social interaction where the contact is resistant
- **Tanenni's own Insight (+5 + 1d4)** before a crucial NPC read
- **Milores's Intimidation (+3 + 1d4)** for the pre-combat Thaumaturgy intimidation sequence

**Concentration note:** Guidance conflicts with Fog Cloud. Tanenni cannot maintain both simultaneously. In practice: use Guidance during approach and skill phases; switch to Fog Cloud if combat begins. If combat begins unexpectedly while Guidance is active, the Guidance ends unless the Concentration save is made — this is typically the correct trade.

---

### Chain 5: The Musician Recovery — Bradkell + Short Rest

**After any Short or Long Rest where Bradkell plays 10+ minutes of music:**

Up to 2 allies who can hear him gain **Heroic Inspiration** when the rest ends. Heroic Inspiration is one instance of Advantage on any d20 roll, spent when the player chooses.

The party should plan Short Rests to include a musical component whenever safe. Bradkell also receives Heroic Inspiration from his Human Resourceful trait at the end of every Long Rest, meaning he can hold his own inspiration and pass Heroic Inspiration to two others simultaneously.

**The optimal sequence entering a significant encounter:** Bradkell holds his own Heroic Inspiration (from Resourceful), and two allies enter with Inspiration from the Short Rest. Three crew members with Advantage available on demand. Heroic Inspiration is best saved for: a saving throw against a critical effect (Milores against a paralysis save), a decisive attack roll where the difference between hit and miss is the encounter (Darwhidan's Sneak Attack on a high-AC target), or a crucial social check under time pressure (Bradkell's Persuasion with a resistant contact).

---

### Chain 6: The Speak with Animals Intelligence Web — Tanenni + Pre-Operation Recon

Not a combat chain — the party's primary pre-mission intelligence operation.

**Tanenni: Speak with Animals** (2 free casts per LR, or Ritual) → any Beasts in the area of the planned operation → patrol timing, guard positions, unusual presences, recent disturbances, physical layout of sections the crew hasn't accessed.

City animals are the party's most reliable free intelligence source:
- **Pigeons and sparrows** patrol the exterior, note arrivals/departures, observe rooftop access
- **Rats** move through building interiors, know the occupied vs. unoccupied spaces, follow food sources to kitchen areas and guard common rooms
- **Cats** map territorial boundaries with high precision and notice irregularities in human patterns
- **Dogs** track scent trails and can identify how recently spaces were occupied

The quality of the information depends on the Beast's intelligence and range of experience, but at minimum: *something is wrong in the east wing tonight* or *the big one comes back at the same time, always, except not three days ago* is intelligence that changes a plan. Use at least one Speak with Animals cast per major operation. The cost is negligible; the information is often decisive.

---

## Turn Order Guidelines

At Level 1, the optimal combat-opening sequence is:

1. **Bradkell** (if acting early): Cast Sleep, centring the 5-ft Sphere between the most tightly-grouped enemies. Move behind cover or Milores.
2. **Darwhidan**: Hold one turn — Sleep's Unconscious condition doesn't arrive until the target fails its second save. On the following turn, move to within 5 feet of the nearest Unconscious target → auto-crit with Shortsword + Nick Dagger.
3. **Milores**: Engage the highest-threat non-sleeping target with Spear or Unarmed Strike → Bonus Action Unarmed Strike.
4. **Tanenni**: Healing Word if someone needs it (Bonus Action) + Guidance on Darwhidan if he needs a skill check, or Command on a remaining threat. Cast Fog Cloud if positioning warrants it.

In practice, initiative is random and this ideal sequence rarely occurs exactly as written. The core principles:
- **Sleep before melee attacks**, if possible — the auto-crit dividend is worth waiting two turns (first save → Incapacitated; second save → Unconscious).
- **Milores enters first, draws attention**, so that Darwhidan's Sneak Attack trigger is established before he acts.
- **Bradkell stays behind Milores**, at 30 feet of distance minimum from any enemy that is not already down.
- **Tanenni's Healing Word is never spent speculatively** — keep a slot in reserve for the round someone drops.

## Exploration Synergy

### The Stealth Advantage

Unlike Party 2, which had Chain Mail as a hard constraint on full-party stealth, this party has **no heavy armour anywhere**. All four crew members can make Stealth checks without Disadvantage. The actual party Stealth values:

| Character | Stealth Modifier | Darkvision |
|---|---|---|
| Darwhidan | +5 | 60 ft |
| Milores | +5 | 60 ft |
| Tanenni | +1 | 60 ft |
| Bradkell | +0 | None |

**The full-party stealth approach:** Darwhidan and Milores lead at +5. They are the advance team — scouts, lock-breakers, and guard-neutralisers before the rest of the crew commits. Tanenni follows at +1 (serviceable; she should not be the lead). Bradkell follows last at +0 (a liability in a stealth context — he should either be established at a different entry point or in a held position while the others proceed).

**Bradkell's stealth problem:** His DEX +0 makes him the party's loudest member. In full-stealth operations, his role is not infiltration — it is cover. He is in the bar where the mark's household staff drink, asking about their day. He is at the front gate with a legitimate reason to be there. He is the reason the staff are distracted and two guards are having a conversation they didn't plan to have. He enables the stealth approach by ensuring the perimeter is occupied.

### Darkvision Coverage

Three Darkvision members (60 ft each) and one Human with no Darkvision. Bradkell's darkness vulnerability is similar to Gwenwyn's in Party 2 — he is dependent on a light source in dark environments, and a light source in a dark infiltration is a liability. Solutions:

- **Tanenni's Light cantrip on an object** (controllable — can be smothered by wrapping the object): provides full lighting for Bradkell while the Darkvision members can operate ahead of the light bubble
- **Hold position with light source** while Darkvision members advance, reuniting once the area is confirmed clear
- **Plan around lighting conditions**: most urban interior environments have functional lighting by default; the darkness problem is specific to certain settings (sewers, vault interiors, roof approaches at night)

### Magical Security Detection

The party has two layers of magical security detection:

1. **Bradkell's Arcana (+4):** Active check when approaching a known or suspected magical security element. Identifies what it is from description and appearance if it is within his knowledge base.
2. **Tanenni's Detect Magic (Ritual):** Free detection of magical effects within 30 feet, with school identification. Takes 10 minutes; Concentration for up to 10 minutes. Run this as standard procedure on every new space before the crew physically approaches mechanisms, doors, or objects of value.

The combination means the party almost never enters a warded space without prior knowledge of what the wards are. This is a significant operational advantage.

## Resource Management Between Encounters

After any significant operation or fight, assess:

1. **Bradkell's slots:** Two Level 1 slots, Long Rest. Slot one is pre-spent on Mage Armour each morning. If Sleep has also been used, all levelled spell contribution is exhausted — he is operating on cantrips and social skills only. A Long Rest is necessary before the next significant encounter.
2. **Tanenni's slots:** Two Level 1 slots, Long Rest. If Healing Word has been used, carefully consider whether to continue with a reduced safety margin. The second slot should be held in reserve until it is clearly needed.
3. **Bradkell's Innate Sorcery:** 2 uses per Long Rest. With Chromatic Orb gone, the primary pairing is Innate Sorcery + Sorcerous Burst for maximised cantrip output. One use remaining covers the highest-priority cantrip moment of the remaining day.
4. **Darwhidan's Feather Fall:** 1 free cast per Long Rest. Once expended, unexpected falls require a plan. Note this when planning high-altitude egress.
5. **Darwhidan's Healing Hands:** 2 uses per Long Rest. If expended, the Aasimar triage option is gone; Milores's Healer's Kit becomes the stabilisation fallback.
6. **Tanenni's Breath Weapon:** 2 uses per Short or Long Rest. Refreshes on Short Rest — this is the most accessible refresh in the party. If both were used and combat is expected, a Short Rest restores them.

**When to Short Rest:** When Tanenni's Breath Weapon is depleted (restores on SR) and any further encounter is expected. Short Rests are cheap and Bradkell should always play during them — the Heroic Inspiration dividend from Musician is worth the time investment.

**When to Long Rest:** When Bradkell has used both spell slots and Sleep is expected to be needed; when Tanenni has used both spell slots and healing reserve is needed; when the party's aggregate HP is substantially reduced and Milores in particular is below half. Milores going into a serious fight at 5 or fewer HP is the party's most urgent physical crisis — she is the frontliner, and a frontliner who is trying not to die is an ineffective anchor for Darwhidan's Sneak Attack chain.

**The non-negotiable Short Rest habit:** After any encounter in which Bradkell plays music and at least two allies spend Hit Dice — guaranteed Heroic Inspiration distributed, resources partially replenished, crew enters the next phase of the operation at full capacity. This should be the default behaviour between significant encounters, not an optional recovery step.
# Party Dynamics — Analysis

---

## Overview

Four characters across four different alignment positions — Neutral Good, Chaotic Neutral, Lawful Neutral, Neutral — and four entirely different frameworks for understanding why what they are doing is the right thing to do. This party shares a target (the corrupt wealth of the city's elite) and a method (precision infiltration), and almost nothing else in terms of philosophical infrastructure. Party 1's central tension was alignment — a Lawful Good cleric generating genuine friction against pragmatic neutrality. Party 2's tension was method — uniform Chaotic Good alignment disagreeing on how, not whether. This party's tension is *motivation*: they agree completely on the what and the how, and they have arrived at that agreement from starting points that, examined closely, have almost nothing in common.

The drama of this party is not conflict in the conventional sense. It is the slow, episodic realisation — through a series of decisions where the right action is obvious but the *reason* for it diverges — that the people you work well with are not necessarily the people you understand, and that working well together over time produces a kind of trust that is harder to walk away from than it is to enter.

---

## Alignment & Motivation at a Glance

| Character | Alignment | Core Motivation |
|---|---|---|
| Bradkell | Neutral Good | Systemic change; the city's structural injustice is the target, not any particular vault |
| Darwhidan | Chaotic Neutral | The game; the pleasure of the work and the specific aesthetic satisfaction of taking from the right people |
| Milores | Lawful Neutral | The code and the cottage; a specific professional framework applied consistently, and a clear exit point |
| Tanenni | Neutral | Ecological balance; the city's weight is in the wrong places and this is correctable, and she is a person who corrects what is correctable |

**The central dynamic:** They all, independently, came to the same conclusion about the same targets. Bradkell came to it through theory. Darwhidan came to it through aesthetics. Milores came to it through scope evaluation (civilians: out; extractive wealth: in). Tanenni came to it through observation — the city's ecosystem is out of balance in ways that are not subtle once you are paying attention. These four different routes to the same destination do not produce four identical people, and the routes remain visible in how each of them responds when an operation produces an unexpected complication that wasn't in the plan.

**The absence of a truly selfish character** means the crew will occasionally overspend on doing the right thing. There is no one calculating the exit cost. When a complication arrives that could be managed by abandoning a secondary objective — a person in trouble, a document that should be redistributed but wasn't in the plan, a situation that is someone else's problem but is happening right in front of them — none of the four has a reliable internal voice that says *not our job, move on*. Bradkell wants to make it his job. Darwhidan finds it interesting. Milores assesses whether it's in scope. Tanenni adjusts the plan. The result is a party that takes on complications with somewhat more frequency than is strictly optimal.

---

## Pairwise Dynamics

### Bradkell + Darwhidan — The Idealist and the Opportunist

**The dynamic:** Two social operators with opposite orientations — one whose charisma is in service of a framework, and one whose charisma is in service of the game. They are more similar than either would say and more different than either would admit.

Bradkell builds his social approach around a model: he has a theory of the mark, a theory of the room, a theory of the outcome three moves ahead. Darwhidan reads the room and responds to what is there, in real time, with the quick intuitive accuracy of someone who has been doing this for years without a framework. Both produce excellent results. They produce them differently, and when the results diverge — when Darwhidan's in-the-moment read contradicts Bradkell's prepared model — the conversation that follows is the one worth having.

**The friction:** Bradkell needs to understand the model; Darwhidan needs the model to remain flexible. When Bradkell has built a social operation on a specific assumption and Darwhidan adapts away from that assumption mid-execution, the operation usually still works (Darwhidan is very good), but Bradkell is never entirely sure why, which bothers him. Darwhidan is aware this bothers him and finds it somewhat endearing.

**What builds the relationship:** Both are performing a version of themselves in social contexts, and both know the other is doing it, and neither of them can be the other's mark in the way they can be other people's marks. There is a specific ease that comes from being known to someone whose primary skill is not being known. Bradkell is the only person in the crew who understands exactly what Darwhidan is doing when he does it; Darwhidan is the only person in the crew who can tell when Bradkell's charisma is a choice rather than a reflex.

**Alignment arithmetic:** Neutral Good vs. Chaotic Neutral. Bradkell asks "is this the right thing to do?" Darwhidan asks "does this serve the job and is the job pointed at the right targets?" These questions produce the same answer most of the time. When they don't, the disagreement is real: Bradkell will not take a job he considers harmful regardless of the payoff; Darwhidan will consider any job whose target meets his aesthetic criteria, and the question of harm is a secondary filter. This has not yet produced a genuine conflict. It will.

---

### Bradkell + Milores — The Revolutionary and the Professional

**The dynamic:** The member of the crew with the most elaborate moral framework and the member of the crew with the most explicit absence of one. Bradkell thinks about why; Milores thinks about how. They are, on the basis of this, remarkably compatible.

Milores does not want Bradkell's theory. She has no use for systemic change as a concept — she thinks in specific jobs, specific targets, specific operations. What she respects is competence, and Bradkell is competent in the specific domains she relies on: social intelligence, pre-operation planning, and the particular skill of anticipating what the other party will do. She watches his work the same way she watches anyone's work, with the full attention of someone assessing craft.

**The friction:** Bradkell occasionally wants to expand the scope of an operation based on principle. A document found in the vault that isn't on the list. A prisoner mentioned in passing who has some claim on the crew's attention. An opportunity to do something that is clearly right but wasn't in the plan. Milores does not object to any of these things on moral grounds — she objects to them on operational grounds, which is a different disagreement. The code says civilians are out of scope. The code says the operation is the operation. Scope creep costs exit probability.

**What builds the relationship:** He respects that her code is consistent. She respects that his framework produces accurate reads on targets. Both of them find the other's decision-making process legible in a way they find valuable, even when it conflicts with their own. When the crew needs to make a hard call under time pressure — stay or go, expand or extract — Bradkell and Milores are the two most likely to reach a decision the others can work with.

**A note on the paintings:** Bradkell has noticed the paintings. He has not said anything, because Milores has not invited the conversation. He is fairly certain she knows he has noticed, and is equally fairly certain she is waiting to see whether he respects the space she has created around this part of herself. He is respecting the space.

### Bradkell + Tanenni — The Rabble-Rouser and the Archivist

**The dynamic:** The crew's plan-maker and the crew's intelligence base. In operational terms, they are a single system: Tanenni produces the information, Bradkell converts it into a plan, and the plan relies on the information being correct. This mutual dependency creates a specific kind of professional trust that is different from — and in some ways deeper than — personal trust.

Bradkell thinks of Tanenni as the most reliable person in the crew. She does not misrepresent what she knows. She does not exaggerate the confidence of intelligence. She says *the animals reported guard rotation roughly hourly, the variance is unclear* rather than *the guards come every hour*. This precision is worth a great deal to a planner who needs to know the difference. He calibrates his plans around her reports and they have, so far, been accurate.

**The friction:** Tanenni does not explain her reasoning unprompted. She arrives at conclusions — the guard in the east wing is nervous tonight, something is wrong with the third-floor ward, this mark is not who they say they are — and communicates the conclusions without the path. Bradkell is a person who wants the model, not just the output. He has learned to ask the right questions, and she has learned to recognise that the questions are genuine rather than performative, but the initial gap between her communication style and his is the one he works hardest to bridge.

**What they share:** Both see the city as a system with identifiable failure modes and correctable imbalances. Tanenni's ecological framing and Bradkell's political-economic one produce different language for the same observation: weight in the wrong places. He has noticed this overlap. She has noted that he has noticed, which she considers an indication that his model is reasonably accurate.

**The calligraphy:** He has watched her produce forged credentials and understood, with genuine appreciation, that she is the reason their cover identities hold up to scrutiny. He has also understood that she has been doing this for much longer than the crew has existed, which is a reminder that his role as planner is relatively recent in her operational history. He finds this humbling, which is rare. He has not mentioned it.

### Darwhidan + Milores — The Thrill-Seeker and the Surgeon

**The dynamic:** Two people who are both, in their different ways, defined by physical precision — one surgical in violence, one surgical in manipulation — and who find in each other a specific professional recognition that neither quite gets elsewhere.

Darwhidan is precise with his hands. Milores is precise with her hands. The contexts are different (locks, pockets, social situations vs. guard neutralisation, combat positioning), but the underlying quality — the kind of attention that makes difficult physical things look effortless — is the same. They have both noticed this in each other. Neither has said anything about it. They are the crew's most reliable silent-approach pair, which means they spend a significant amount of time working in close proximity without speaking, and this is, for both of them, an entirely comfortable situation.

**The friction:** Darwhidan has a thrill-seeker's relationship to risk. He will take an unnecessary leap through an unnecessary window because the arc is beautiful and the landing is clean and the operation succeeds with a narrative quality it wouldn't have otherwise had. Milores finds this inefficient. Not wrong — she does not evaluate it morally — but she notes that unnecessary risk creates unnecessary variance, and unnecessary variance costs exit probability. She has mentioned this. He has acknowledged it. He has continued to leap through windows when the opportunity arises. She has logged this as a known variable.

**What builds the relationship:** They do not need to debrief. After an operation, Darwhidan does not explain his moves to Milores and Milores does not explain hers to Darwhidan — they both understand what happened and why, because they were both paying the right kind of attention. The post-operation silence between them has a different quality than the post-operation silence elsewhere in the crew: it is the silence of two people who have already processed everything and don't require speech to confirm it.

### Darwhidan + Tanenni — The Partnership

**The dynamic:** The original pair. Pre-existing professional relationship, shared history of a specific vault on a specific evening, and a debt that is not a debt and an alliance that is not exactly a friendship but is not not one either.

They work together with the particular ease of people who have already established that they will not try to manage each other, who have learned each other's operational rhythms, and who have independently concluded that the arrangement is worth maintaining. Tanenni does not try to make Darwhidan less chaotic; Darwhidan does not try to make Tanenni less opaque. Each uses the other's capabilities without friction.

**The Druidic thread:** The language Tanenni taught Darwhidan is also the operational mechanism he relies on to leave field reports in places the crew can later retrieve. This creates a dependency: he uses the language, she reads the messages, both of them can communicate in a register that no one else in the crew shares. It is, in practice, a second relationship occurring in parallel with the primary one — the one where they plan and execute together, and the one where the messages are left in stone and she reads them later and notes that he noticed the right things.

**The debt:** He owes her half the contents of a vault from three years ago. This is technically outstanding. Neither of them has brought it up. It functions, in the crew's social economy, as the visible surface of a more complicated question about what they owe each other and whether debt is the right framework for describing it. Tanenni knows what she is owed. She has a very good memory for ledgers. She has chosen not to call the balance due, which is itself a kind of information that she has not explicitly provided and he has not explicitly asked about.

**What builds the relationship:** Trust. The operational kind: he trusts her intelligence, she trusts his execution. And the rarer kind: each has information the other gave them that could cause problems, and neither has used it, and this has been true across three years and multiple operations. At some point, that stops being discretion and becomes something else.

---

### Milores + Tanenni — The Architect and the Executioner

**The dynamic:** Two people who are both highly competent, both process-oriented, and both completely uninterested in being perceived as either of those things. They move in the same operational world without friction, and the absence of friction might be the most interesting thing about them.

Tanenni plans. Milores executes. The handoff between these phases is the point where most crews generate friction — the planner wants fidelity to the plan; the executioner finds plans are always approximate once contact is made with reality. With these two, the handoff is clean: Tanenni's plans have good margins, and Milores's execution stays within them, and when the margins don't hold, Milores improvises in ways that Tanenni can immediately track because they are using the same underlying logic. The plan is a prediction of the probable; Milores operates in the actual; the gap between them is managed without drama.

**A specific shared quality:** Both of them have things they do that the crew has noticed and not commented on. Milores has her paintings; Tanenni has her four years from retirement. Neither has been asked about these things by the other, which means they share, unusually, the experience of carrying a private fact in a small group without it being drawn out. This has not been discussed, because it is not the kind of thing that gets discussed. But both of them are aware of the parallel, in the way that observant people notice parallels without requiring them to be made explicit.

**Tension:** Milores's code and Tanenni's ecological balance framing occasionally point in different directions. Tanenni's orientation is systemic — she is comfortable with approximation and aggregate effects; Milores's code is case-by-case, and it has sharp edges on specific categories. When an operation presents a complication that falls into the grey zone between Tanenni's acceptable-variance and Milores's outside-scope, the decision needs to be made somewhere, and neither of them will make it loudly.

## Group Dynamics

### The Deliberation Pattern

When the crew must make a collective decision under pressure, they tend to break as follows:

| Option | Who Supports It |
|---|---|
| Execute the prepared plan | Tanenni (always); Milores (if scope is clean) |
| Adapt the plan based on new information | Bradkell (enthusiastically); Tanenni (if her intelligence supports it) |
| The unconventional exit that wasn't in the plan | Darwhidan (reliably); Milores (if the conventional exit is compromised) |
| Expand scope to address an adjacent problem | Bradkell (principled motivation); Darwhidan (if it sounds interesting) |
| Decline and extract | Milores (if scope becomes unclear); Tanenni (if the risk-information balance shifts) |

**The crew does not have a single swing vote.** Decisions under pressure are genuinely collective, which means they are slower than in parties with a clear decision-maker. The crew's best outcomes happen when there is time to discuss; their worst happen when time is unavailable and each of the four is trying to execute their own read on the situation simultaneously.

### What Holds the Party Together

**A shared destination, arrived at from different starting points.** They all concluded, independently, that the specific category of wealth the crew targets is the correct category. The fact that they arrived at this conclusion through incompatible frameworks does not reduce its operational force. When the crew faces a decision about whether an action is justified, the discussion is fast because the answer is usually the same — the paths to it are different, but the destination is shared.

**Complementary skill domains with no overlap.** There is no redundancy in this crew. Tanenni covers intelligence and magical security. Darwhidan covers physical infiltration and social deception. Milores covers physical neutralisation and frontline engagement. Bradkell covers social engineering and battlefield control. Each of them is irreplaceable in their domain. This produces a specific cohesion: no one is performing a function someone else does better, which means no one is competing for a role, which means the team's internal friction is almost entirely about values and almost never about positioning.

**Competence as the primary respect language.** None of them arrived at their expertise through institutional channels or social recommendation. Tanenni's religious authority was self-created; Darwhidan's court access was stolen; Milores's martial expertise was developed in the field and refined in circumstances that were not comfortable; Bradkell's social intelligence was built on the streets of the industrial quarter against an audience that would not have been kind to failure. They all know what real competence looks like, and they all see it in each other. This is not the same as liking each other, but it is more durable.

### What Is Different From Parties 1 and 2

**Party 1** had a fundamental alignment split — a Lawful Good character whose role was to stop and ask whether the party's direction was actually right. This party has no equivalent. Bradkell is the closest: Neutral Good, with genuine principled investment in doing the right thing. But he also planned the heist, which means his moral framework already encompasses the work. There is no one whose job is to challenge the premise.

**Party 2** was uniformly Chaotic Good — everyone agreed on ends, and friction was about method. This party has four different alignments pulling in different directions. The irony is that the alignment spread produces *less* friction than Party 2's uniformity, because the different frameworks have found a specific project they all endorse, and the project doesn't require them to reconcile the frameworks. The tension is latent rather than active. It becomes active when a complication arrives that the frameworks would resolve differently.

**The specific absence** this party feels most acutely is a character whose primary mode is openness. Party 2 had Gwenwyn, who asked questions and listened to answers and connected people to each other. This party has four people who are fundamentally private about their internal lives — Tanenni because openness doesn't serve the operational mode; Darwhidan because self-disclosure is a vulnerability; Milores because there is not much to say; Bradkell because the role he performs requires maintained distance. They are not unkind to each other. They are simply not, in the ordinary social sense, easy with each other. The understanding they have is real; it is just expressed almost entirely through work.

### Flashpoints

The scenarios most likely to produce genuine intra-party tension:

- **An operation produces a target who is not who they appeared to be** — someone the crew robbed on good intelligence turns out to have been in a worse position than their apparent wealth suggested; Bradkell's framework requires grappling with this; Milores's code says the scope was met; Darwhidan's aesthetic says the targets were identified correctly; Tanenni recalibrates the model for next time. The disagreement is about what the obligation is when good information turns out to be incomplete.

- **Darwhidan uses a social deception on someone the crew has a relationship with** — not a mark, but a contact, a fence, an NPC who has extended trust to the crew in some form; the question of whether the deception was necessary, and what it costs the crew's social infrastructure, produces different answers from different frameworks.

- **Milores declines to act on something that falls outside her code** — a situation where a person needs help, the crew has the capacity to help them, and the scope assessment says it's outside the operation; Bradkell cannot do nothing; Milores can; the crew is in the middle.

- **Bradkell's information on a mark is wrong, and the crew discovers this mid-operation** — his role as plan-architect depends on the quality of his intelligence, and a significant intelligence failure implicates his judgment in a way that the others will need to decide how to hold.

- **Tanenni's four years becomes three, becomes two, becomes one** — at some point the arithmetic of the crew's operations starts to imply that she has a planned exit, and the question of whether she stays past the point of her exit plan is one that none of the others can answer for her, and she has not answered it herself.

- **Someone asks Darwhidan why he's still here** — the specific question that, answered honestly, requires him to examine whether the crew has become something more than a professional arrangement, and whether that is a comfortable fact or an uncomfortable one. He has good deflections prepared. The question, from the right person at the right moment, will get past them.
# Bradkell — Strategic Analysis

---

## Role

**The Face / Battlefield Controller / Plan Architect**

Bradkell is the crew's social engineer, crowd-control caster, and operational planner. He is the person talking to the mark, the one who has already learned the guard schedule from three casual conversations with three separate people who each thought they were talking to someone else. In combat, he is a threat multiplier rather than a damage dealer: Sleep is the most powerful Level 1 spell in a heist context, and Mage Armour is what keeps him functional long enough to cast it. His AC of 13 with Mage Armour active is still the worst in the crew, and this should inform every positioning and engagement decision he makes. He is never in the line of fire; he is in the position that lets him reshape the encounter.

---

## Combat Priorities

### The Two Spell Slots

Bradkell has two Level 1 Sorcerer spell slots, refreshed on a Long Rest. Both have a clear, fixed purpose: one goes to Mage Armour at the start of each adventuring day; one goes to Sleep when the moment demands it.

| Slot | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Slot 1 — Mage Armour** | Cast on himself at the start of each adventuring day. AC rises from 10 to 13. 8-hour duration, no Concentration. This is not a combat decision — it is a morning ritual. Do not enter an operation without it active. |
| **Slot 2 — Sleep** | The crew's primary encounter-resolution tool. Hold it until the optimal target cluster is identified, then spend it decisively. |

**Mage Armour is mandatory.** The difference between AC 10 and AC 13 is the difference between being targeted first and being targeted second. Three additional points of AC on a character with 8 HP does not make him durable — it makes him slightly less fragile, which at Level 1 is enough to matter. Cast it without deliberation every morning.

**Sleep is the correct default combat-slot expenditure in almost all infiltration contexts.** A 5-foot-radius Sphere centred on a point within 60 feet; each creature of your choice within it must succeed on a Wisdom saving throw (DC 13) or be Incapacitated until the end of its next turn — then save again, falling Unconscious on a second failure. Against a guard with +0 or −1 WIS, the probability of both saves failing is high. Against two guards standing close together, this is a decisive opening — one cast, encounter controlled.

**The practical implication of this split:** Bradkell effectively has one combat spell slot per day. Once Sleep is expended, his levelled-spell contribution is exhausted. He should be positioned and resourced with this constraint in mind — Sleep is the single shot, and it should be the shot that ends the encounter, not the one that reduces it.

### Innate Sorcery: The Amplifier

Innate Sorcery (Bonus Action, 1 minute, 2 uses per Long Rest) raises spell save DC by 1 and gives Advantage on Sorcerer spell attack rolls.

| Pairing | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Innate Sorcery → Sleep** | Spell DC rises to 14 — directly improves the chance targets fail their WIS save; activate before casting Sleep when fighting enemies with WIS +1 or higher, where the DC difference matters |
| **Innate Sorcery → Sorcerous Burst** | Advantage on ranged spell attack; the 8-chaining mechanic becomes notably more likely to trigger; the correct use once Sleep is expended and he is operating on cantrips alone |

**The optimal use of Innate Sorcery once Mage Armour is the second spell:** Pair it with Sleep to raise the save DC and improve the chance of landing both required fails, or hold it for Sorcerous Burst when levelled slots are exhausted. Against Sleep-immune enemies (undead, constructs), activate Innate Sorcery and spend rounds on Sorcerous Burst — Advantage on the attack roll turns a cantrip into his best available damage option.

**Do not waste Innate Sorcery on cantrips when a levelled spell is available this encounter.** It refreshes on a Long Rest, not a Short Rest — two uses over an entire day is not generous. Spend it on the moment it changes the outcome of the encounter, not on improving a routine cantrip round.

### AC 13 Is Still The Information

His AC with Mage Armour active is 13 — still the lowest in the crew (Milores: 15, Tanenni: 15, Darwhidan: 14), but no longer the kind of liability that invites immediate targeting. Every positioning decision should still reflect this gap. He should never be the first to enter a space, never be adjacent to an enemy that has not already been dealt with, and never be the person standing between an aggressive threat and the crew's exit. His job is to be behind Milores, behind Tanenni, and at sufficient distance that an enemy would need to cross the full engagement zone to reach him.

When he is targeted, three things are true:
1. Something has gone wrong with the crew's positioning
2. He is two or three hits from unconsciousness regardless of Mage Armour — the AC improvement buys time, not survival
3. He has a Spear, two Daggers, two Handaxes, and True Strike, and can contribute physically in an emergency — this is not ideal, but it exists

**True Strike as an emergency option:** When no spell slots remain and the cantrips have done their work, True Strike (CHA-based weapon attack; weapon damage type or Radiant, your choice) gives him a +5 attack roll, which is the same as Darwhidan's weapon attack. Against a target within Spear or thrown Dagger range, this is a meaningful last-round option rather than a desperate swing.

---

## Social Operation Priorities

Bradkell is the crew's social engine. Outside of combat, his priority ordering is:

**1. Information gathering before the operation.** Three casual conversations with three separate people — a guard's off-duty drinking companion, the quartermaster's delivery supplier, the scribe who handles the estate's correspondence — produces more reliable intelligence than a single risky infiltration attempt. He builds the picture through Persuasion (+5) and Performance (+5) in contexts where his working-class appearance and natural credibility make him invisible to the people he is talking to.

**2. Establishing the cover story.** He is the person who decides what legitimate reason the crew has for being where they are, creates the social and documentary infrastructure for that cover, and rehearses it with each crew member to the point where it can withstand questions. Tanenni handles the calligraphy; he handles the script.

**3. Live social management during the operation.** When something unexpected happens — a guard who wasn't scheduled, a visitor who wasn't expected, a functionary who notices a detail that is slightly wrong — he is the one who intercepts the conversation and redirects it. Persuasion +5, Performance +5, and the practiced ease of someone who has been talking his way into restricted spaces for most of his adult life.

**4. Friends (Concentration) as a targeted social tool.** Friends is powerful and creates a specific problem when it ends: the target knows they were magically influenced and becomes hostile. The correct use of Friends is in situations where the post-spell hostility can be managed — the target is isolated, the crew is about to leave, or the target will not retain access to the group long enough for the hostility to matter. Do not use Friends on a recurring contact or on someone whose relationship the crew needs to preserve.

---

## Skill Usage

**Arcana +4:** The crew's primary magical knowledge skill. When the crew encounters an unfamiliar magical effect, a warded door, or an artifact of uncertain provenance, Bradkell makes the Arcana check. This is one of two INT-based skills that matter in an infiltration campaign (Investigation is the other). He should be the one examining the magical alarm before Tanenni casts Detect Magic — if the alarm is obvious enough to see, Arcana identifies it first.

**Investigation +4:** Searching spaces for hidden compartments, false floors, concealed mechanisms, and non-obvious document locations. In a vault environment, Investigation is the skill that finds the real strongbox, not the decoy. He should systematically investigate spaces the crew enters on planned operations rather than relying on Passive Perception.

**Performance +5:** He can play the drum, flute, and shawm well enough to be plausible as a street musician, a festival performer, or a court entertainer. This provides a cover identity that places him naturally in diverse social environments. It also triggers the Musician feat — see below.

**Persuasion +5:** High-end social negotiation. The difference between Persuasion and Deception: Persuasion is for genuine attempts to convince someone of something you want them to believe, without them being hostile. When a contact needs to agree to something, when a fence needs to accept a price, when a lord needs to be convinced that the crew belongs in his house — Persuasion is the tool.

---

## Feature Usage

**Musician feat (Short and Long Rests):** During any Short or Long Rest where Bradkell plays for 10+ minutes, up to 2 allies gain Heroic Inspiration when the rest ends. Heroic Inspiration is Advantage on any single d20 roll — saved for the moment it matters most (a crucial save, a decisive attack, a high-stakes social check). **He should always play at rest.** The crew benefits from Heroic Inspiration entering encounters, and the cost is zero beyond the time invested in the rest they were taking anyway.

**Resourceful (Human):** He gains Heroic Inspiration at the end of every Long Rest. This is personal insurance — he can spend it before passing it to an ally, or retain it through a high-stakes moment. Combined with the Musician feat, it means he often enters encounters with his own Heroic Inspiration already in reserve.

**Tavern Brawler (Rebel):** Unarmed Strikes deal 1d4+STR (+1) damage and can push the target 5 feet. This is a last-resort combat option — 1d4+1 is not a significant contribution to a Level 1 fight. However, the 5-foot push creates specific tactical value: pushing a melee enemy away from himself (or from Darwhidan, who needs to stay adjacent to Milores), or setting up positioning before Milores closes.

**Prestidigitation:** Underestimated utility cantrip in an urban context. Clean or soil an object (restore a prop's appearance after use; soil a guard's uniform to create a distraction incident); warm or chill food or drink (trivial but occasionally useful in an NPC interaction); create a small illusory image for one round (very small, but visible at point-blank); produce an odd odour (scent-based misdirection in a small space). He should ask "can Prestidigitation handle this?" before taking a significant action to produce a minor effect.

---

## Where Bradkell Shines

- Social encounters — the party's most effective face and only dedicated social engineer
- Sleep — the single most impactful Level 1 spell in a guard-heavy infiltration campaign, and he is the only crew member who has it
- Mage Armour — a mandatory morning cast that buys meaningful survivability for a single slot; frees his second slot to be Sleep without compromise
- Musician — Heroic Inspiration generation at every rest is multiplicative with the crew's effectiveness over a session
- Pre-operation intelligence — gathering information through social channels before a planned operation costs no resources and produces genuinely reliable intelligence
- Sorcerous Burst burst potential — the chain mechanic with CHA +3 maximum extra dice produces occasional exceptional cantrip rounds

## Where Bradkell Struggles

- AC 10 — the worst physical durability in the crew; he is two hits from danger in almost any direct combat scenario
- DEX +0 and Stealth +0 — he cannot participate in a quiet approach and should not attempt one; his role in a stealth infiltration is to create a legitimate-presence distraction while the others go through the quiet entrance
- WIS −1 — he will miss things that Tanenni or Milores would catch; he should not be on watch; his Passive Perception of 9 is the lowest in the crew
- Sleep against non-standard enemies — constructs, undead, and anything immune to the Charmed condition is entirely immune to Sleep; his backup is Innate Sorcery + Sorcerous Burst, which is a significant step down from Sleep in terms of encounter impact
- One effective combat slot per day — Mage Armour consumes slot one; Sleep consumes slot two; once both are gone, he is a cantrip caster and a social asset, not a spell threat

## Roleplaying Notes

Bradkell has decided, at some point in his early twenties, that the most dangerous thing a person can be is underestimated, and has been systematically cultivating that condition ever since. He moves through the world in rough clothes, speaks in the straightforward register of the industrial quarter, and has a face that is distinctive once noticed and forgettable until then. He is the most strategic person in any room he enters, but he maintains this as a private fact rather than a demonstrated one — demonstrated intelligence is a resource that announces itself and then gets managed; private intelligence can be deployed.

He genuinely believes in what the crew is doing. This distinguishes him from Darwhidan (who is in it for the pleasure of the game) and from Milores (who is in it for the specific arithmetic of the cottage). His investment in the targets being the right targets — the corrupt, the extractive, the systemically harmful — is real, and it shows up in his planning. He researches the crew's marks with more thoroughness than operational efficiency requires, because he wants to be certain. He has turned down jobs. He has not always explained why.

His relationship with each crew member is distinct:
- **Tanenni**: professional alignment and mutual respect; he trusts her planning without question and finds her nature-ecological-redistribution framing accurate if slightly depersonalised relative to his own framework
- **Darwhidan**: genuine warmth and tactical trust, complicated by the awareness that Darwhidan's motivation is fundamentally aesthetic rather than principled; he finds this useful and occasionally unsettling
- **Milores**: deep and unsentimental respect; he considers her code the most reliable framework in the group and defers to her professional judgment on operational questions even when they diverge from his own read

He plays at rest because the crew performs better after it. He would play anyway, but this is the version of the reason he tells himself.
# Darwhidan — Strategic Analysis

---

## Role

**Primary Infiltrator / Precision Striker / Social Second**

Darwhidan is the crew's lockpick, second face, and primary damage spike. He is the person who goes first and alone into the dark, comes back with a report, and then goes back in to open the thing that needed opening. In combat, he is a controlled detonation: relatively low-impact unless the conditions for Sneak Attack are met, at which point he is the crew's single highest-damage round. The key to playing him well is understanding the interplay between his Nick mastery, his freed Bonus Action, and Sneak Attack positioning — and understanding that his WIS −1 means he should never be the one making the Perception or Insight call.

---

## Combat Priorities

### Sneak Attack: The Central Resource

Darwhidan's Sneak Attack (1d6 extra damage on a qualifying hit) is the single most important mechanical feature he has. At Level 1, it is the difference between being a character who deals 1d6+3 per round and a character who deals 2d6+3 per round. The conditions:

| Method | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Advantage on the attack** | Easiest to achieve via Milores's adjacent positioning, Sleep, or Hiding first |
| **Ally within 5 ft of target** | Milores is almost always in melee range if she's engaged with the same enemy — this is the default trigger |

**The Milores adjacency trigger is the crew's most reliable Sneak Attack setup.** When Milores is fighting the same enemy, Darwhidan can attack with a Finesse or Ranged weapon at normal odds and still get Sneak Attack, as long as Milores is within 5 feet of that enemy. This does not require Advantage — just Milores being where she usually is. Call the same target as Milores before acting.

Against a target that Bradkell has put to Sleep: that target is Unconscious. Attacks against Unconscious targets are automatic critical hits if made within 5 feet. A melee critical hit with Sneak Attack at Level 1 is 4d6+3, which is likely lethal on a sleeping guard. This is the crew's cleanest elimination option.

### The Nick Mastery: The Hidden Action Economy Advantage

Darwhidan's Dagger has the Nick mastery property: *the extra attack from the Light property can be made as part of the Attack action instead of as a Bonus Action*.

In practical terms, a standard dual-wielder must:
1. Attack action with main-hand weapon
2. Bonus Action with off-hand weapon (Light property)

With Nick, Darwhidan does:
1. Attack action with Shortsword + Dagger (both within the Attack action)
2. **Bonus Action: available for anything else**

This is significant at Level 1. His freed Bonus Action can now be used for:
- **Hide** (Bonus Action with Cunning Action — when he gains it at level 2; note this at level 1)
- **Healing Hands** (his Aasimar feature, 2d4 HP on a touched ally — costs his Bonus Action, not a spell slot)
- **Feather Fall** (if a fall is incoming — this is a Reaction, not a Bonus Action, but worth tracking as a separate resource)
- **Mage Hand** setup on a previous turn (spectral hand for lock manipulation or item retrieval)

At Level 1 before Cunning Action is available, the freed Bonus Action is most likely to go unused or toward Healing Hands. At Level 2, it becomes the hide-and-vanish mechanism that makes Darwhidan genuinely difficult to pin down.

### Vex Mastery: The Attack Chain

When the Shortsword hits and deals damage, the Vex mastery property grants Advantage on the next attack roll against that same target before the end of his next turn. The flow:

**Turn 1:** Shortsword hits → Vex applies → Dagger attack (Nick, same action) → potential Sneak Attack on one of these if Milores is adjacent.
**Turn 2:** Opens with Advantage on the first attack against the Vexed target → higher chance of Sneak Attack qualification → can go Shortsword again to continue the Vex chain.

In a sustained fight against a single significant enemy, this chain self-perpetuates: hit with Shortsword to gain Vex, open next turn with Advantage to hit more reliably, repeat. The chain breaks if the Shortsword misses on turn N or the target is defeated. Against a single dangerous enemy, this makes Darwhidan's damage output surprisingly consistent across rounds.

---

## Skill Usage

**Sleight of Hand +7 (Expertise):** The highest modifier in the crew on any skill. This is the lockpicking roll, the pickpocket roll, the document-extraction-from-a-pocket roll, the sleight-of-hand check for hiding a stolen item on your person while being searched. With Thieves' Tools and this modifier, most locks at Level 1 open reliably. Use it for every physical manipulation that requires precision.

**Deception +6 (Expertise):** The crew's primary social deception skill. While Bradkell handles social engineering and persuasion (his domain), Darwhidan handles the specific skill checks where someone is actively trying to catch a lie — the guard who is suspicious, the functionary who has seen the documents before, the fence who knows the market value of what you're selling. Deception +6 is robust enough to hold up under most scrutiny.

**Stealth +5:** He moves quietly. He should be the lead scout on every entry — going first into a space to assess it before the rest of the crew commits. His Darkvision (60 ft) means he can do this in darkness without a light source.

**Thieves' Tools (proficient):** Open locks, disable mechanical traps. The primary mechanical tool in every planned infiltration. He should assess every vault door and secured space himself; do not delegate lockpicking to a character without this proficiency.

**Acrobatics +5:** Climbing through windows, dropping from heights (with Feather Fall backup), navigating unstable surfaces, escaping grapples. The physical vocabulary of his job.

---

## Spell and Feature Usage

**Mage Hand:** Manipulation from 30 feet away without touching. Uses: retrieve items from sealed-but-accessible containers, trigger pressure plates remotely to test for traps, turn handles and open unlocked doors from outside a room, pour a vial's contents at range. Not all of these will come up every session, but the habit of asking "can Mage Hand handle this first?" before physically approaching an unknown object is worth developing.

**Minor Illusion:** Sound or image within 30 feet. Creates diversions — footstep sounds from the east corridor while the crew moves through the west, a brief image of a figure in a doorway to redirect a guard's attention, a voice saying something in a guard's own apparent voice, once. Uses Charisma DC 12 for disbelief if investigated. Most credulous targets will not investigate the first distraction; most skeptical ones will. Know your guard.

**Feather Fall (Reaction; 1 free/LR):** The signature exit. The mechanism is: act normally until the window is the better option, then go through it and cast on the way down. The 60-feet-per-round fall rate is slow enough to land safely and controlled enough to have a conversation on the way. Up to 5 creatures within 60 feet can benefit — this means the whole crew, if they are in range when the jump happens. A coordinated multi-floor exit is possible; it requires everyone to jump at approximately the same moment and be within 60 feet of Darwhidan.

**Healing Hands (2 uses/LR; Magic Action, Bonus Action):** 2d4 HP on a touched ally. At Level 1 HP totals (8–10 HP across the crew), this is a meaningful emergency triage — Bradkell at 2 HP is considerably less useful than Bradkell at 8 HP, and Healing Hands costs no spell slot. Use it on the party's squishiest member (Bradkell) before it becomes a recovery-from-zero situation. It is a Bonus Action in 2024, meaning it doesn't compete with his attack action.

---

## Key Traits in Play

**Celestial Resistance (Necrotic + Radiant):** Against enemies or traps that deal Necrotic or Radiant damage, Darwhidan halves the damage received. In an urban infiltration campaign, this most commonly applies to: cursed vault mechanisms, undead guardians, and certain magical ward triggers. It is passive and requires no activation — just note it when it applies.

**Darkvision (60 ft):** He operates comfortably in the dark. The crew's standard approach: Darwhidan scouts the dark space first, returns with information, the crew then uses a light source (Tanenni's Light cantrip, a torch) for the entry phase. In fully dark extractions where a light source would draw attention, he and Milores (also Darkvision 60 ft) can operate without one; Tanenni (Darkvision 60 ft) can follow; Bradkell is at a disadvantage and should remain at a held position until the coast is clear.

**Druidic language:** The hidden-message system with Tanenni allows him to leave carved rune-reports in city stonework for the crew to read later. In campaign terms, this is the crew's dead-drop communication network. Characters who know Druidic are: him and Tanenni. Characters who know Thieves' Cant are: him alone. He has access to two separate hidden communication registers.

**AC 14 with DEX +3:** Reasonably durable for a Rogue at Level 1, but not a frontliner. If enemies begin targeting him in preference to Milores, something has gone wrong with positioning or the tactical situation. He should be at the flanks or behind the encounter, reaching in for Sneak Attack opportunities and retreating to the edge of engagement distance.

---

## Where Darwhidan Shines

- Locked spaces: Sleight of Hand +7 with Thieves' Tools opens most Level 1 locks routinely
- Stealth approach: highest consistent damage output of any crew member who can operate quietly at range
- Sneak Attack against sleeping or prone targets: auto-crit in melee against unconscious targets turns his damage into a lethality tool that cleanly resolves a single guard without alarm
- Social deception: when a lie needs to hold up under scrutiny, he is the check to make
- Vertical and unusual egress: Feather Fall makes multi-storey exits trivial
- Sustained damage against a single priority target: Vex + Nick mastery self-perpetuates in a focused fight

## Where Darwhidan Struggles

- WIS −1 everywhere: Perception at −1 and Insight at −1 mean he will not notice things and will not read people correctly on Wisdom-based checks. He should not be on watch. He should not be making the NPC read. He should absolutely not be doing both simultaneously while Bradkell is talking to a contact.
- No spell slots: all his magical effects are cantrips or once-per-day freebies. He cannot contribute nova damage or have a dramatic spell moment.
- Single-target orientation: Sneak Attack only applies once per round against one target. Against a crowd of four guards, he is considerably less efficient than Bradkell's Sleep or Tanenni's Command.
- Relatively low HP (10): two hits from a reasonably dangerous enemy will threaten him. He needs Milores or Tanenni between him and trouble, not the reverse.

---

## Roleplaying Notes

Darwhidan has two modes, and the transition between them is his defining characteristic. In social contexts, he is warm, apparently slightly guileless, and possessed of the easy manner of a large man who has learned that people relax when he does. In operational contexts, he becomes very still and very precise — the warmth doesn't disappear exactly, but it stops being the primary thing present, and what replaces it is a quality of complete attention that some people find more unsettling than hostility.

He is not ideological about the work. He does not share Bradkell's framework of systemic change, and he finds Tanenni's ecological metaphors accurate but dry. He thinks the correct targets are the ones they are targeting, for reasons that are more aesthetic than philosophical — people who use their resources to harm others deserve to have them removed; people who don't are not his problem — and he is comfortable with this framework. He does not interrogate it.

He is genuinely fond of the crew, in the unsentimental way of a person who has spent time with competent people and found this surprising. He trusts Tanenni's planning implicitly. He trusts Milores's execution equally. He trusts Bradkell's social judgment conditionally, which is to say: in rooms where Bradkell is the most charismatic person present, he defers; in rooms where that condition does not hold, he watches more carefully.

He owes Tanenni half the contents of a vault from three years ago. He has not paid this debt. He is aware she knows. He is enjoying the ambiguity.
# Milores — Strategic Analysis

---

## Role

**Silent Muscle / Guard Neutralizer / Tactical Frontliner**

Milores is the crew's physical problem-solver — the person who enters a space ahead of the others, neutralises the resistance quietly enough not to need an alarm plan, and holds a position when the crew needs a body between it and harm. She is not a damage dealer in the explosive sense; she is a damage dealer in the *sustained, methodical, this-will-be-over-in-two-rounds* sense. At Level 1, her combination of Unarmoured Defense (AC 15), Martial Arts (Bonus Action unarmed strike), and Savage Attacker (reroll damage dice) makes her the party's most durable melee combatant and its most reliable guard-neutraliser. The key to playing her is understanding that the Spear and the Unarmed Strike serve different purposes, and that her Bonus Action is as valuable as her main action.

---

## Combat Priorities

### The Two Attack Modes

Milores has two distinct combat configurations, and the choice between them should be made based on whether the primary goal is maximising single-hit damage or maximising consistent output across rounds:

| Mode | Weapon | When To Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Maximum Hit** | Spear (two-handed, 1d8+3 Piercing) + Savage Attacker | When one decisive hit matters: subduing a dangerous target before they raise an alarm, finishing a wounded enemy, or a high-stakes round where the result of the hit is important |
| **Sustained Pressure** | Unarmed Strike (1d6+3) + Bonus Action Unarmed Strike | Default combat rounds; against any enemy where consistent reliable hits over multiple rounds is better than a single big one; the Bonus Action strike is free and adds ~7 damage on a hit each round |

**The Spear/Savage Attacker combination** is her highest single-hit potential at Level 1 — 1d8+3 with a reroll available is an average of approximately 7–8 damage, with the ceiling higher if the natural roll is favourable. Savage Attacker is once per turn, so if she's attacking twice (spear + bonus action), the first attack gets the reroll; the second does not. Assign Savage Attacker to the Spear attack (higher dice ceiling) rather than the Dagger.

**The Unarmed Strike combination** generates two attacks per round without spending a resource. Her Martial Arts Bonus Action unarmed strike applies every round she takes the Attack action with an unarmed strike or Monk weapon — which means, in a sustained fight, she averages more consistent damage output than a single Spear hit allows, because the bonus strike is free.

**The practical guidance:** open with the Spear against a priority target if the first hit needs to do maximum work (e.g. knocking a guard out before they call for help). Transition to unarmed strikes in a continuing fight where sustained output matters more than burst. Mix as needed.

### The Guard Neutralisation Problem

The crew's primary operating preference is no dead bodies — unconscious guards, not corpses, create fewer complications. Milores is equipped to subdue rather than kill if the intent is there, but this requires active attention:

- Unarmed Strikes deal Bludgeoning damage, which produces unconsciousness without guaranteed lethality — a guard dropped to 0 HP by unarmed strikes is likely disabled rather than killed, subject to the DM's interpretation of the encounter
- The Spear deals Piercing damage; daggers deal Piercing damage. Against a human-type guard, these are more likely to produce genuine fatalities
- **For guard neutralisation specifically: default to Unarmed Strikes.** The Bonus Action extra strike means she can still generate two attacks per round. Spare the Spear for threats that need to be dropped quickly and regardless of condition.

**Thaumaturgy as a neutralisation tool:** Before combat, a booming voice (Advantage on Intimidation) plus direct physical presence may be enough to convince a single guard that discretion is the better option. Intimidation +3 is not spectacular, but Advantage from Thaumaturgy on top of a 7-foot Tiefling who smells of sulfur is a meaningful additional factor. Use it before violence when possible — a guard who surrenders creates fewer consequences than a guard who is rendered unconscious.

### Positioning

Milores needs to be in melee range to function, which means she needs to get there. At 30 feet of movement per round, she can close most combat distances reliably. Once engaged:

- **Position between the crew and incoming threats.** Her AC 15 (the highest in the crew) and 10 HP make her the person best equipped to absorb hits.
- **Stay adjacent to an enemy Darwhidan is attacking.** The Milores-adjacency trigger for Darwhidan's Sneak Attack is the crew's most reliable damage chain. She doesn't need to do anything differently — she just needs to be hitting the same enemy he is.
- **Do not chase.** If an enemy withdraws to range, let Tanenni handle it (Command: Approach, or a ranged weapon), or let Darwhidan (Shortbow, Sneak Attack at range from Advantage) deal with it. Milores closing on a ranged enemy costs her movement, her attack, and her positioning for everyone else.

---

## Spell and Feature Usage

**Fire Bolt:** A ranged option (120 ft) when she cannot close to melee. 1d10 Fire damage on a hit at +4. Against an enemy at distance who cannot be reached this round, or when she needs to contribute while remaining in a held position, Fire Bolt provides meaningful damage output. The Tiefling fire resistance means she is slightly more comfortable in fire-adjacent environments than her colleagues.

**Thaumaturgy:** Three main uses:
1. **Pre-confrontation intimidation** — booming voice (Advantage on Intimidation) before a guard encounter that might be resolved with presence rather than violence
2. **Distraction** — slam a door open or shut at range, create phantom sounds, cause harmless tremors to direct attention away from a crew member's movement
3. **Psychological pressure** — altered eye appearance and a voice that fills the room, in an interrogation or standoff, costs nothing and produces genuine discomfort in most NPCs

**Savage Attacker:** Once per turn. Decision point: it applies to any weapon attack's damage roll — roll the damage dice twice and use either result. The best targets are high-variance dice: the Spear's 1d8 or the Dagger's 1d4. Against the Spear at two-handed (1d8), a natural 1 or 2 for damage becomes a second chance at a better roll. Don't overthink this: use it on the first attack of the turn, on the Spear, against the highest-priority target. The reroll is not worth saving for a better moment — there is no better moment than now.

**Healer's Kit:** 10 uses; stabilise a dying creature without a Medicine check. The crew has no dedicated healer aside from Tanenni's Healing Word. Milores carries the kit and should use it immediately when an ally drops — stabilise first, get back into the fight, revive properly afterward. Stabilised allies are not on the death-save clock.

---

## Key Traits in Play

**Unarmoured Defense (AC 15):** The highest AC in the crew, despite wearing no armour. This is a direct function of her DEX +3 + WIS +2 combination. She should be first in the line of fire, absorbing hits rather than the crew's spellcasters — she is better equipped to take them and better equipped to survive them.

**STR +0, DEX +3 (Martial Arts):** She uses DEX for both attack and damage on unarmed strikes and Monk weapons, meaning her STR score is irrelevant to her core function. Her STR save proficiency remains useful for grapple effects and physical forced movement. Her DEX save proficiency (+5) is one of the best in the crew and gives her meaningful protection against area effects, traps, and Dexterity-save-based spells.

**Perception +4 (proficient):** The second-best Perception in the crew after Tanenni (+5). She and Tanenni should be the party's primary watchers — she on watch at night, Tanenni running ambient awareness during operations. A Passive Perception of 14 is solid enough to catch most non-enhanced approaches.

**Stealth +5:** Surprisingly stealthy for someone who looks like a former shock-infantry specialist. She and Darwhidan are the crew's primary silent-approach pair. If the crew needs a two-person advance team to get into position before the others follow, it is Darwhidan (for locks and precision) and Milores (for quiet elimination of anything Darwhidan finds on the other side).

**Painting (Painter's Supplies, proficient):** This is not a combat trait. It is a rest trait. Milores paints during downtime — before operations, after operations, during the wait while Bradkell is socialising a mark. This is worth noting mechanically because at higher levels it creates opportunities for Tools interactions and proficiency uses. At Level 1 it primarily functions as a character signal: she is the person in the crew who is most deeply committed to there being an after, and what she does in the after.

---

## Where Milores Shines

- Sustained melee pressure: two attacks per round (Unarmed + Bonus) with no resource cost
- Guard neutralisation: unarmed bludgeoning creates clean, professional takedowns without escalating consequences
- Sneak Attack enablement: adjacent positioning is the crew's most reliable Darwhidan damage multiplier
- Physical durability: AC 15 with 10 HP is the best physical position of anyone in the crew
- Dark operations: Darkvision 60 ft, Stealth +5, and Fire Bolt at 120 ft make her viable in any light condition
- High-stakes single hits: Savage Attacker + Spear (two-handed) produces the crew's best burst damage against a single target when one decisive hit matters
- Intimidation with Thaumaturgy: the combination of presence, fiendish appearance, and a voice that fills the room can resolve encounters before they escalate

## Where Milores Struggles

- INT −1 everywhere: no Arcana, no Investigation, no History. She should not be examining magical objects, searching for clues, or trying to recall relevant facts. She is aware of this and does not object.
- No healing capability: Healer's Kit stabilises; it does not restore HP. If multiple crew members are badly hurt and Tanenni has expended her slots, Milores can only prevent deaths, not reverse damage.
- Ranged combat: Fire Bolt (+4) and Shortbow (+5) exist as options, but her optimal damage is melee. An encounter that forces her to fight entirely at range — an enemy with a large gap between them, a flying target — reduces her effectiveness significantly.
- No social skills: Intimidation +3 is the extent of her social tool set. She should not be talking to the lord whose vault they are robbing, the fence who is buying the output, or the contact who needs to be reassured. She can be present in the room; she should not be the one speaking.

---

## Roleplaying Notes

Milores is the member of the crew who is most fully herself at all times, which is not the same as being the most legible. She doesn't perform, doesn't manage how she is perceived, and does not calibrate what she says based on how it will land — she says the accurate thing, at the necessary moment, in the minimum required words, and if this produces an unexpected reaction in her audience she notes the reaction as information. She is not cold. She is precise.

Her professional code is the most readable thing about her. It has clear categories — within scope, outside scope — and she applies it with the consistency of someone who has found that consistency is what prevents the code from being negotiated away in difficult moments. Civilians: outside scope. Non-combatants: outside scope. Guards doing their jobs: within scope, handled with minimum necessary force. Guards doing more than their jobs: different calculation. She applies this arithmetic in real time and with very little visible processing.

The paintings are the inverse of everything else about her. They are careful, beautiful, detailed, and take a long time. She works on them in the spaces the crew creates — the forty minutes before an operation, the downtime while the others plan or socialise, the quiet of a night watch. She is currently on the eighth in a coastal series. She has a specific cottage in mind for where they will eventually hang. The cottage is not hypothetical; she has seen it. The only outstanding question is the purchase, and the purchase depends on four more operations going correctly.

She does not discuss the paintings with the crew, not because she is protective of them but because they have not asked. If asked, she would describe the technical problem she is currently working through — the particular quality of afternoon light on a cliff face at a certain time of year — with the same focused attention she gives to explaining a combat approach or a route through a guard rotation. It is all the same kind of attention. She does not separate the things she gives it to.
# Tanenni — Strategic Analysis

---

## Role

**Environmental Scout / Inside Man / Support Caster**

Tanenni is the crew's reconnaissance engine, logistical backbone, and on-site authority figure. She is not the primary damage source in a fight, and she knows it — her WIS-heavy build exists to serve the crew's pre-combat and non-combat operations more than its encounters. The Warden subclass makes her a capable combatant in a pinch, but her greatest value is in what happens before the vault door opens: animal-sourced intelligence, environmental concealment, Guidance on critical skill checks, and the religious authority that walks through restricted doors that would take Darwhidan twenty minutes to pick. Play her as the architect of the situation the crew enters, not as a combatant within it.

---

## Combat Priorities

### The Two Spell Slots

At Level 1, Tanenni has two Level 1 Druid spell slots, refreshed on a Long Rest. Both are meaningful, and both have an obvious correct use:

| Slot Choice | Use When |
|---|---|
| **Fog Cloud** *(Concentration)* | Combat opens in an unfavourable position; the crew needs obscurement to reposition, or a bottleneck exists where making 20 feet of space Heavily Obscured prevents enemies from targeting the group effectively |
| **Healing Word** *(Bonus Action)* | An ally drops or is badly hurt; the Bonus Action cost is the critical advantage — it costs no action, allowing her to cast it and still attack or use another action spell on the same turn |

**The core principle:** Fog Cloud creates a situation; Healing Word manages a crisis. Do not spend Fog Cloud as a combat opener if the tactical situation does not require it — save it for the moment it changes the fight. Healing Word, similarly, is for genuine emergencies: spending a slot on a conscious ally who has lost a quarter of their HP is not a good investment; spending it to get an unconscious Bradkell back on his feet is.

**Command** from Magic Initiate is a third option and costs no slot (1 free cast per Long Rest). Against a single threatening enemy, a one-word command that removes their action for a turn — Grovel (Prone, action gone), Halt (no movement or action) — can prevent more damage than Fog Cloud costs in the same round. Reserve the free Command cast for the moment it shuts down a real threat.

### Concentration Management

Fog Cloud and Detect Magic both require Concentration. Tanenni's Guidance also requires Concentration. **Only one of these can run at a time.** The choice:

| Scenario | Concentration Choice |
|---|---|
| Pre-infiltration scouting | Detect Magic (ritual — free; audit for wards) |
| Skill-heavy non-combat moment | Guidance on the ally making the critical roll |
| Combat in a defensible bottleneck | Fog Cloud on the most effective choke point |
| Combat in an open space | Healing Word (no concentration) + attack, saving Fog Cloud for a better moment |

Do not activate Guidance and then walk into combat — it will end on the first saving throw forced by damage. Cast Guidance before high-stakes skill moments, not as a standing buff.

### Bonus Action Economy

Tanenni has two primary Bonus Action options:
- **Healing Word** (emergency heal, costs a slot)
- **Jump** (prepares an ally for high-mobility engagement — costs a slot, but the recipient can leap 30 ft per turn for a minute without a STR check)

At Level 1, Jump is a premium investment in Milores or Darwhidan for a vertical entry or egress situation. Healing Word is the higher-priority hold. Unless the operation specifically involves dropping from height, keep the Bonus Action slot reserved for Healing Word.

---

## Spell Usage

**Fog Cloud:** The crew's best tactical reset. 20 feet of Heavily Obscured space means attack rolls against targets within it have Disadvantage, and attackers within it attack targets they can't see with Disadvantage. Note that the crew has three Darkvision members (Tanenni herself, Darwhidan, Milores) and one Human (Bradkell) who sees nothing in magical fog the same as anyone else — coordinate before casting. If Bradkell is the one in the cloud, he needs to have already acted or the obscurement helps the enemy as much as it helps the crew.

**Healing Word:** The most efficient healing spell at Level 1, period. Bonus action delivery means it never costs the action economy of a concentrated healer — she can cast it and still use her action to quarterstaff, Command, or cast Guidance. 2d4+3 averages 8 HP, which is substantial at Level 1 HP totals. Do not save this forever; a crew member at 0 HP costs more turns (and danger) to revive than the slot is worth.

**Detect Magic (Ritual):** Free. Costs 10 minutes. Use it every time the crew enters a space with unknown magical security. Do not skip this step in operations. Magical wards that go undetected are the single most common failure mode in urban infiltration. The ritual does require Concentration while active, so don't begin it when trouble is expected imminently.

**Speak with Animals (Free, 2/LR or Ritual):** Tanenni's most undervalued asset in the urban context. City animals — rats, pigeons, cats, street dogs — are present in every building, every sewer line, every garden and rooftop. They notice patrols. They feel the presence of people who shouldn't be there. They do not have complex vocabulary, but *something is wrong in the east wing tonight* or *the big man comes back every hour* or *she feeds the birds but not the ones on the third floor* is intelligence that no other crew member can obtain. Use at least one free cast per operation. The information is almost always worth it.

---

## Key Traits in Play

**Perception +5 (proficient):** She is the party's best-equipped watcher — the highest Passive Perception in the crew by a significant margin (Milores at 14, everyone else below 10). She should be on watch during Long Rests, should be positioned at doorways when the crew needs advance warning, and should be the one whose attention is pointed at the room while others are speaking to it.

**Insight +5 (proficient):** She reads people with the precision of someone who spent twelve years in a temple environment full of people performing piety. She will notice when an NPC is lying, uncomfortable, or concealing something, even when that NPC is a very practiced liar. Deploy her in any interaction where a follow-up or calibration read on the contact matters.

**Guidance (Concentration, 1 minute):** Choose a skill at cast time; the target adds 1d4 to any ability check using that skill for up to a minute — +1 to +4 per roll. The skill must be declared at casting, not when the check arrives — anticipate the moment. Pre-cast it on Darwhidan *(Sleight of Hand)* before a difficult lock. On Bradkell *(Persuasion)* before a social check where the contact is resistant. On herself *(Insight)* before a crucial NPC read. Don't forget it exists — it is the most frequently-overlooked tool in her kit.

**Warden: Martial Weapon Proficiency:** She can use martial weapons. At Level 1 with STR 10 and DEX 12, she won't hit hard, but she can hold a line. A Spear (Versatile, 1d8) with two hands while she coordinates through Guidance or Message is a plausible combat contribution when the crew needs a third warm body in range rather than a support caster at the back.

**Message cantrip:** 120-foot range, whisper to a specific target only. No one else hears it. No one watching knows she said anything. In an infiltration with multiple positioned crew members, this is the coordination layer — real-time updates on patrol position, confirmation of entry signal, warning of incoming threat. It requires a component (a piece of copper wire) and line of sight to the target, but in practical terms it is a one-way, one-target, line-of-sight radio. Use it as one.

**Calligrapher's Supplies (proficient):** Forged credentials, altered ledger entries, duplicated authorisation documents. This is an operations capability, not a combat one, but it is the capability that opens the front door in the first place. With advantage-level execution and the appropriate materials, she can produce documents that pass casual scrutiny in the religious and administrative contexts she knows best.

**Herbalism Kit (proficient):** Craft antitoxins and healing poultices during Long Rests, if the ingredients are available. An antitoxin worth 50 GP in a shop can be produced for component costs. In a campaign involving potentially poisoned locks or guards with paralytic supplies, this is worth thinking about before each operation.

---

## Where Tanenni Shines

- Pre-mission intelligence: animals are everywhere, notice everything, and cost only ten minutes of ritual casting to interrogate
- Magical security detection: Detect Magic as a ritual is a free step in every infiltration plan that cannot be skipped
- Critical skill check moments: Guidance as a Bonus Action pre-cast turns a DC 15 check with a +5 modifier into effectively a +6 to +9 attempt
- Restricted access: religious authority opens doors in temple environments, archives, noble chapels, and the kind of consecrated spaces where guards let a known face through rather than challenge it
- Long-rest watch duty: Passive Perception 15 means things don't approach camp without being noticed
- Healing in the action economy: Healing Word's Bonus Action delivery makes her the single most efficient healer relative to what it costs her to heal

## Where Tanenni Struggles

- Direct damage: her weapons hit at +2 and deal modest die sizes; she is not the answer to "we need someone to hurt this enemy quickly"
- INT −1 across the board: Arcana, Investigation, and History checks are not hers. She should not attempt to identify magical items by inspection, research archives, or recall historical facts
- Stealth +1: she moves quietly for a medium-sized scaled humanoid in armour and carrying a shield, which is to say: not very quietly. She should not be in the stack during a full-party stealth approach — she creates the conditions for the stealth approach and then finds a legitimate-looking position
- High-pressure social contexts outside her domain: she is credible in religious, administrative, and noble-charity contexts. She is considerably less credible in criminal taverns, guild halls, and situations where her careful, official bearing reads as conspicuous
- Concentration fragility: she has no feature that helps her maintain Concentration saves (CON +2, no War Caster or Resilient). If she is hit while concentrating, she is more likely than a hardier caster to lose the spell

---

## Roleplaying Notes

Tanenni moves through the world as though she has already assessed it and concluded it is approximately what she expected. This is not disdain — she is not contemptuous of people, animals, or situations — it is a professional posture that she adopted somewhere in her twenties and has maintained since as a baseline from which surprise occasionally departs. She is observant, measured, and genuinely good at waiting.

She does not explain her reasoning unless asked, and often not when asked. She tends to arrive at a conclusion and act on it, and if the action is questioned, she will describe the conclusion, but she will not always describe the path from observation to conclusion, which is a form of communication gap that occasionally creates friction with Bradkell, who is process-oriented and wants to understand the model, not just the output.

Her relationship with animals is not tender in any obvious way — she communicates with them practically, in a low murmur, with the matter-of-fact tone of someone consulting a reliable but limited reference source. The animals appear to find this reassuring. Several of the city's pigeons have begun following her at a distance. She has not commented on this.

She is four years from retirement if the mathematics hold. She has not told anyone this. She is not sure she believes it herself.
