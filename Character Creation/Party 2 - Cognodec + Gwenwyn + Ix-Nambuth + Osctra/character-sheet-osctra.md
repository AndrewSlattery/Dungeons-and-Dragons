## 7. Notes & Backstory

### Appearance

Osctra does not look like a traditional Elf. He has traded ethereal grace for raw, sun-baked mass. At roughly 80 years old — old enough that he stopped counting with any precision — he has had an extraordinarily long time to accumulate calluses. After decades of hauling soil, digging irrigation trenches, and wrestling stubborn livestock, he is built like a brick oven: broad, thick-muscled, and covered in a permanent dusting of dirt. His skin is deeply tanned and weather-beaten, his hands thick with the calluses of decades of hard labour — marked here and there by old fertiliser stains and the faint burn scars of a glasswork accident he declines to discuss.

His dark hair, the colour of good loam, is tied back in a utilitarian knot that mostly — but never quite entirely — conceals his pointed ears. His eyes are the clear, sharp green of a healthy leaf held up to sunlight: the one detail that gives his heritage away to anyone who looks closely enough, and the one feature that doesn't seem to fit the rest of him at all.

He wears heavy, mud-stained canvas trousers, thick leather work boots, and a workman's apron worn smooth with decades of use. His "trident" is a beautifully balanced, wickedly sharp pitchfork — clearly a craftsman's tool, not a soldier's weapon. Two heavy iron framing hammers hang from his belt. His shield is the reinforced ironwood lid of a massive pickling barrel, strapped to his forearm with worn leather, and it fits there with the comfortable naturalness of something that has always been there.

He moves with the steady, unhurried efficiency of someone who has never once needed to rush. When he uses Druidcraft, there is no mystical flourish: he snaps his fingers to check the wind direction, sniffs the air to gauge soil composition, or once — to win an argument about alkalinity — made an entire hillside of wildflowers bloom mid-sentence and then went back to eating.

### Personality Traits

- He communicates in brief sentences, long silences, and the occasional pointed clearing of the throat. He considers small talk a form of agricultural waste.
- He assesses the health of every living thing he encounters as a reflex — plants, animals, and, disconcertingly, people. He will interrupt a conversation to quietly inform someone that they look malnourished.

### Ideal

**Growth.** Anything worth having takes time, patience, and the willingness to get your hands dirty. He has no patience for anyone who expects results without tending, and no respect for anything that takes without giving back.

### Bond

Decades of cultivated research. A prize herd scattered across four valleys. A greenhouse in ashes. He will rebuild every last square foot of it.

### Flaw

His contempt for authority and entitlement is so deeply ingrained that it can misfire against genuinely reasonable people simply because they happen to be wearing a title or a fine coat.

### Backstory

Osctra was born into a small, isolated Wood Elf community on the edge of a remote frontier valley. Unlike most of his kin who eventually drifted toward the ancient forests or the wider world, Osctra stayed — not out of any inability to leave, but because the valley's soil was exceptional and there was work to do.

Over the next decades, he quietly transformed a modest plot of land into something remarkable: a living laboratory of botanical cultivation, centred on a magically-enhanced strain of Orcish root tubers. The variety was known among those who knew it at all for its extraordinary yield, its remarkable shelf life, and its maddening sensitivity to grow conditions. It demanded constant attention. Osctra gave it constant attention. The arrangement suited both parties.

Decades of twice-yearly trading at the valley's foot brought him into regular contact with Orcish merchants, whose exacting quality standards — and even more exacting opinions about soil composition — he found oddly agreeable. He learned Orcish out of professional necessity; he kept speaking it because Orcish, in his considered view, has far more precise words for agricultural concepts than Elvish does. He was never social, exactly. But he was known: as a fair dealer, a patient neighbour, and someone who descended from his valley twice a year with a cartload of produce and expected to be left alone the rest of the time.

**The Snap**

For months, something had been wrong with the valley's wildlife. Predators were appearing in the wrong ranges, frightened and disoriented — driven out of their territories by something Osctra hadn't yet been able to identify. He had been noting it in his field journal with growing unease, chalking individual incidents up to seasonal disruption, telling himself it would settle.

It did not settle. A few weeks ago, an arrogant local lord and his retinue of armoured knights decided that Osctra's valley was an ideal hunting ground. They flushed one of these displaced predators — something large, frightened, and well outside its natural territory — directly through his property. The beast demolished his greenhouse. The knights demolished his fences. Their horses demolished two hundred years of carefully maintained botanical beds.

When Osctra emerged from the ruins of his glasswork and politely requested compensation, the lord laughed. He referred to him, specifically, as a "dirt-grubbing peasant."

Whatever remained of Osctra's Elven etiquette evaporated on the spot. He didn't raise his voice. He made no threats. He went very, very quiet — the kind of quiet that had the knights' hands moving toward their hilts before they fully understood why — and then he picked up his pitchfork and systematically dismantled the entire hunting party. He did not kill anyone. He didn't need to. He threw the lord bodily over his own ruined fence and explained, in a voice of absolute calm, exactly how many individual plants had been destroyed and what he expected by way of restitution.

The lord filed a complaint from his sickbed. There is now a modest bounty on Osctra's head.

**The Road**

With his farm in ruin and his herd scattered, Osctra has been forced onto the road. He is not adventuring for glory. He is adventuring because something has been driving the valley's predators out of their natural ranges for months, and he intends to find out what it is, stop it, and earn enough coin to rebuild his greenhouse. The fact that this mission incidentally involves a significant amount of violence is, in his view, an unfortunate but manageable inconvenience.

He is, however, keeping a meticulous field journal of every plant species he hasn't previously catalogued. There is no reason to let the whole enterprise go to waste.
